# Environment, Climate Change, and Biodiversity of Bangladesh

Bangladesh faces some of the most severe environmental challenges of any country on Earth, yet it also harbours extraordinary biodiversity and has pioneered community-based approaches to environmental management. For the DU Admission examination, understanding Bangladesh's environmental context — its vulnerability, its natural heritage, and its conservation efforts — is essential.

### 🟡 Standard — Regular Study (2d–2mo)

> Standard content for students with a few days to months.

#### 1. Climate Vulnerability

Bangladesh is consistently ranked among the world's most climate-vulnerable countries. Several major indices place Bangladesh in the top 5:

**Germanwatch Global Climate Risk Index (2021):**
Bangladesh was ranked the **5th most climate-vulnerable country** in the world (over the period 2000–2019). Over 170 countries are ranked.

**Key Climate Hazards Bangladesh Faces:**

**Cyclones:**
Bangladesh's location at the head of the Bay of Bengal makes it extraordinarily susceptible to cyclones:
- The **1970 Bhola Cyclone** killed an estimated 300,000–500,000 people — the deadliest tropical cyclone in recorded history
- **Cyclone Sidr** (2007): killed approximately 3,000 people; caused $1.7 billion in damage
- **Cyclone Amphan** (2020): killed over 20 people in Bangladesh; caused extensive damage to the Sundarbans; total damage estimated at $2 billion

**Floods:**
Bangladesh floods regularly — the annual monsoon causes predictable flooding that replenishes the soil but also destroys crops and homes:
- The **1998 floods** were catastrophic — 75% of Bangladesh was underwater for weeks
- Flash floods affect northern Bangladesh in pre-monsoon season
- Riverbank erosion displaces an estimated 100,000 people annually

**Sea Level Rise:**
According to IPCC projections, a 1-metre sea level rise by 2100 could inundate:
- 17% of Bangladesh's land area
- 30% of its agricultural land
- Affect 20 million people
- The Sundarbans would be severely threatened

**Salinity Intrusion:**
Sea level rise and reduced freshwater flow from upstream rivers are increasing soil and water salinity in coastal Bangladesh. This threatens agriculture (especially rice cultivation) and drinking water supplies.

#### 2. The Sundarbans — World Heritage

The Sundarbans is Bangladesh's most internationally significant ecological landmark:

**Geography:**
The Sundarbans is the world's largest contiguous mangrove forest, covering approximately 10,000 square kilometres of Bangladesh and India:
- **6,017 sq km** of the Bangladesh portion
- Located in the southwestern coastal region of Bangladesh
- Named after the Sundari tree (Heritiera fomes), a dominant mangrove species

**Biodiversity:**
- **Royal Bengal Tiger:** Approximately 100 tigers live in the Bangladesh portion — the world's largest tiger population in a single mangrove forest
- **Irrawaddy Dolphin:** Found in the Sundarbans' waterways
- **Saltwater crocodile:** Also found in the Sundarbans
- Over 300 species of birds
- Numerous fish and crustacean species

**UNESCO Status:**
The Sundarbans Mangrove Forest was designated a **UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1992**. In 2017, it was listed as a **UNESCO in Danger** site due to climate change threats.

**Ecological Function:**
The Sundarbans provides critical ecosystem services:
- Protects coastal communities from cyclones and storm surges
- Supports commercial fisheries (providing livelihoods for millions)
- Carbon sequestration (mangroves store vast amounts of carbon)
- Prevents coastal erosion and salinity intrusion

**Threats:**
- Cyclone Amphan (2020) caused extensive damage to Sundarbans
- Rising sea levels and salinity
- Illegal logging and hunting
- Pollution from shipping through the Passur River

#### 3. Rivers and Water Resources

Bangladesh's relationship with its rivers is central to its environment:

**Major River Systems:**
- **Padma (Ganges):** Enters Bangladesh from India at Rajshahi region; joins the Meghna
- **Jamuna (Brahmaputra):** Enters from India at Kurigram; joins the Padma
- **Meghna:** Formed by Surma and Kushiyara; joins the combined Padma-Jamuna
- **Tista:** Major tributary of the Brahmaputra; its flow has been disrupted by dams in India, causing severe drought in northern Bangladesh

**River Issues:**
- **Farakka Barrage (India):** India constructed the Farakka Barrage on the Ganges (completed 1975), which has significantly reduced dry-season water flow in the Padma. This has been a source of ongoing dispute between Bangladesh and India.
- **Teesta River:** The Teesta is the largest tributary of the Brahmaputra. Water-sharing agreements between India and Bangladesh have been contentious. A 2011 framework agreement was not implemented.
- **Tipaimukh Dam (India):** Proposed dam on the Barak River (Surma's source) is contested by Bangladesh, which fears reduced water flow.

#### 4. Groundwater and Arsenic Crisis

**Arsenic Contamination:**
Bangladesh faces one of the largest arsenic poisoning crises in history:
- Millions of tube wells were installed in the 1970s–1990s to provide safe drinking water
- However, the groundwater in many areas contains naturally occurring arsenic
- An estimated 20–30 million people in Bangladesh are at risk of arsenic poisoning
- Symptoms include skin lesions, cancers, and organ damage
- Bangladesh and UNICEF worked to identify and mark affected wells
- This is one of the worst environmental health disasters in history

#### 5. Conservation and Environmental Policy

**Bangladesh's Climate Actions:**

**Bangladesh Climate Change Trust Fund (BCCTF):**
Established in 2009, Bangladesh was the **first country in the world to establish a Climate Change Trust Fund using its own national budget**. The BCCTF funds local adaptation and mitigation projects.

**Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC):**
Under the Paris Agreement, Bangladesh submitted its NDC:
- Unconditional target: 5% reduction in emissions by 2030 (vs. business-as-usual)
- Conditional target: 15% reduction with international support
- Adaptation is Bangladesh's priority over mitigation

**Bangladesh's Climate Change Strategy and Action Plan (BCCSAP):**
Adopted in 2009; outlines 44 programmes across 8 priority areas including food security, disaster management, and infrastructure.

#### 6. Protected Areas and Wildlife Conservation

**National Parks and Wildlife Sanctuaries:**
Bangladesh has established a network of protected areas:

**Largest Protected Areas:**
- **Sundarbans East Wildlife Sanctuary** — UNESCO World Heritage
- **Sundarbans South Wildlife Sanctuary**
- **Sundarbans West Wildlife Sanctuary**
- **Lawachara National Park** (Moulvibazar) — home to the endangered Hoolock Gibbon
- **Satchari National Park** (Habiganj)
- **Rema-Kalenga Wildlife Sanctuary**

**Key Endangered Species:**

**Royal Bengal Tiger (Panthera tigris tigris):**
- Approximately 100 tigers in the Bangladesh Sundarbans
- Critically endangered globally
- Bangladesh's national animal

**Hoolock Gibbon (Hylobates hoolock):**
- One of Bangladesh's most endangered primates
- Found in the northeastern forests (Lawachara, Satchari)
- The Hoolock Gibbon is one of Bangladesh's 5 species of non-human primates

**Gangetic Dolphin (Platanista gangetica):**
- Found in the major rivers of Bangladesh
- Classified as endangered
- A symbol of the Sundarbans ecosystem

**Asian Elephant (Elephas maximus):**
- Small population in the Chittagong Hill Tracts (CHT)
- Human-elephant conflict is a growing problem

#### 7. Deforestation and Environmental Degradation

**Rate of Deforestation:**
Bangladesh has lost significant forest cover over the decades. Forest cover is now approximately 11% of land area, down from much higher historical levels.

**Causes of Deforestation:**
- Expansion of agriculture (especially in the Chittagong Hill Tracts)
- Fuelwood collection
- Illegal logging
- Infrastructure development

**Reforestation:**
Bangladesh has launched massive reforestation programmes, including social forestry initiatives that involve local communities in tree planting and forest management.

#### 8. Air Pollution in Dhaka

Dhaka is consistently ranked among the world's most polluted cities:
- In 2023, Dhaka frequently topped global air quality indices
- Vehicle emissions, brick kilns, and construction dust are major sources
- Air pollution causes significant public health impacts (respiratory diseases)

> **Exam Watch:** Bangladesh's environment is a popular examination topic. Key facts to remember: The Sundarbans is the world's largest mangrove forest and a UNESCO World Heritage Site (1992); the Royal Bengal Tiger lives in the Sundarbans (~100 tigers); Bangladesh is the 5th most climate-vulnerable country; Bangladesh established the world's first national Climate Change Trust Fund; Cyclone Amphan (2020) was the most recent major cyclone; the 1970 Bhola Cyclone was the deadliest in history (300,000+ deaths).
