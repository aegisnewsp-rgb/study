# Living World and Cell Biology

## The Living World: Characteristics and Classification

Life on Earth exhibits certain fundamental characteristics that distinguish living organisms from non-living matter. These include **nutrition** (obtaining and assimilating food), **respiration** (energy release through oxidation), **excretion** (removal of waste products), **growth** (permanent increase in size), **reproduction** (production of offspring), and **response to stimuli** (irritability). Movement is also considered a key characteristic — animals show明显的locomotion while plants show only localized movement (e.g., tropism).

### Five Kingdom Classification (R.H. Whittaker, 1969)

Modern biological classification divides all organisms into five kingdoms based on cell type, complexity, and mode of nutrition:

| Kingdom | Cell Type | Nutrition | Examples |
|---------|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Monera** | Prokaryotic | Autotrophic/Heterotrophic | Bacteria, Blue-green algae |
| **Protista** | Eukaryotic | Autotrophic/Heterotrophic | Amoeba, Paramecium, Euglena |
| **Fungi** | Eukaryotic | Saprophytic (extracellular digestion) | Mushroom, Yeast, Penicillium |
| **Plantae** | Eukaryotic | Autotrophic (photosynthesis) | Mango, Rose, Fern |
| **Animalia** | Eukaryotic | Heterotrophic (ingestion) | Human, Fish, Insects |

**Important CTET Points:**
- Virus is placed between living and non-living — it shows crystallization (non-living property) but reproduces inside host (living property)
- Lichens are symbiotic associations between fungi and algae
- Mycoplasma are the smallest living organisms lacking a cell wall

## Cell: The Basic Unit of Life

### Cell Theory

The cell theory, developed by Schleiden (1838) and Schwann (1839) and later expanded by Rudolf Virchow (1855), consists of three main postulates:

1. **All living organisms are composed of cells** — the basic structural and functional unit
2. **The cell is the fundamental unit of life** — all metabolic processes occur within cells
3. **Omnis cellula e cellula** — all cells arise from pre-existing cells (Virchow's contribution)

### Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells

| Feature | Prokaryotic Cell | Eukaryotic Cell |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Size** | 1–10 μm | 10–100 μm |
| **Nucleus** | No nuclear membrane (nucleoid) | True nucleus with double membrane |
| **Membrane-bound organelles** | Absent (no mitochondria, ER, Golgi) | Present |
| **DNA** | Single, circular, naked | Linear, associated with histones |
| **Ribosomes** | 70S (50S + 30S) | 80S (60S + 40S) |
| **Cell wall** | Present (peptidoglycan in bacteria) | Present in plants; absent in animals |
| **Examples** | Bacteria, Cyanobacteria | Plant and animal cells |
| **Mitosis** | Binary fission (no spindle) | True mitosis with spindle formation |

### Cell Organelles and Their Functions

**Nucleus:** The control center of the cell containing genetic material (DNA). It is surrounded by a double-layered **nuclear membrane** with pores that allow selective transport of substances between nucleus and cytoplasm. The nucleolus (dark staining body inside nucleus) is the site of **rRNA synthesis**.

**Mitochondria:** Called the "powerhouse of the cell" because they produce **ATP** through oxidative phosphorylation. They have their own DNA (circular) and ribosomes (70S), supporting the **endosymbiotic theory** (originally independent bacteria that established symbiosis). Mitochondria are abundant in metabolically active cells like liver and muscle cells.

**Chloroplast:** Found only in plant cells, chloroplasts are the sites of **photosynthesis**. They contain green pigment **chlorophyll** embedded in thylakoid membranes stacked into grana. Like mitochondria, chloroplasts have their own DNA and ribosomes, supporting the endosymbiotic origin theory.

**Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER):** A network of membranes continuous with the nuclear membrane. **Rough ER** has ribosomes attached and is involved in protein synthesis and transport. **Smooth ER** lacks ribosomes and is involved in lipid synthesis and detoxification.

**Golgi Apparatus:** Composed of flattened membrane-bound sacs called **cisternae**. It modifies, packages, and transports proteins received from rough ER. It also synthesizes lysosomes and secretory vesicles.

**Ribosomes:** The sites of **protein synthesis (translation)**. They consist of two subunits — larger (50S in prokaryotes, 60S in eukaryotes) and smaller (30S in prokaryotes, 40S in eukaryotes). The unit used in prokaryotes is 70S; in eukaryotes 80S.

**Lysosomes:** Membrane-bound structures containing hydrolytic enzymes for intracellular digestion. They are abundant in white blood cells (phagocytes). Lysosomes are involved in autophagy (self-digestion) and programmed cell death (**apoptosis**).

**Centrosome/Centrioles:** Present near the nucleus, these structures organize the **spindle fibers** during cell division. In animal cells, centrioles form the **basal bodies** of cilia and flagella.

### Plant Cell vs Animal Cell

| Feature | Plant Cell | Animal Cell |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Cell wall** | Present (cellulose) | Absent |
| **Chloroplast** | Present | Absent |
| **Vacuoles** | Large central vacuole (turgor pressure) | Small, numerous vacuoles |
| **Centrosome** | Absent (centrioles absent) | Present |
| **Shape** | Rectangular, fixed shape | Irregular, flexible shape |
| **Storage** | Starch grains | Glycogen granules |
| **Plasmodesmata** | Present (cell-cell connections) | Absent or minimal |
| **Lysosomes** | Rare (vacuoles serve digesting role) | Common |

**High-yield CTET fact:** Plant cells have a large **central vacuole** that maintains turgor pressure, keeping the plant erect. Animal cells have smaller, more numerous vacuoles.

### Summary for CTET Exam

The cell is the basic unit of life. Prokaryotic cells (bacteria) lack a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles, while eukaryotic cells (plants, animals, fungi, protists) have a true nucleus and organelles. Plant cells differ from animal cells by having a cell wall, chloroplasts, and a large central vacuole. Key organelles to remember: nucleus (control), mitochondria (energy), chloroplast (photosynthesis — plants only), ribosomes (protein synthesis), ER and Golgi (transport and packaging).

⚡ **Exam tip:** Questions comparing plant and animal cells are very common in CTET. Focus on the three distinguishing features: cell wall, chloroplast, and large central vacuole.
