# Topic 2: Legal Systems in Kenya — Common Law, Civil Law, African Customary Law, Islamic Law

## 🟢 Quick Review

Kenya's legal system is pluralistic — it draws from multiple traditions. Understanding the hierarchy and application of each source of law is essential for any law student. The Constitution of Kenya 2010 formally recognizes common law, African customary law, and Islamic law alongside statute.

---

## 📚 1. Common Law System

### Origin and Application

Kenya's legal system is rooted in the **common law tradition** inherited from British colonial rule. Under the East Africa Order in Council 1897 and later the Judicature Act (Cap 8, now repealed and replaced by the Judicature Act 2011), English common law, doctrines of equity, and English statutes of general application as at 12 August 1897 were received into Kenya.

**Section 3 of the Judicature Act 2011** (formerly Section 2 of Cap 8) provides that the High Court shall be guided by:
- Common law and principles of equity
- African customary law where applicable, subject to the Constitution and written law
- Islamic law in matters of personal status, marriage, divorce, and inheritance for Muslims who voluntarily elect it
- Any other law that is applicable

### Key Features of Common Law

- **Stare decisis** — Courts follow binding precedents from superior courts
- The **supervisory jurisdiction** of the High Court (certiorari, mandamus, prohibition, habeas corpus) — exercised through * Judicial Review*
- **Adversarial system** of litigation
- **Classification of law** into public law (criminal, constitutional, administrative) and private law (contract, tort, property)

### Sources of Common Law in Kenya

1. **Case law** — Decisions of Kenyan courts and, where persuasive, decisions of courts in other common law jurisdictions (UK, South Africa, India)
2. **Equity** — Doctrines of trust, equitable remedies (injunctions, specific performance), equitable maxims
3. **Received English statutes** — Those declared applicable by the Interpretation and General Provisions Act (Cap 2) and those stated as applicable under the Judicature Act

### Judicial Precedent in Kenya

The Court of Appeal is the highest appellate court below the Supreme Court. Its decisions are binding on the High Court and Magistrate courts. The High Court is bound by its own previous decisions except where sitting as a Constitutional Court under Article 22 (in which case it may depart from its own precedents).

## 📚 2. Civil Law Tradition

### In the East African Context

Kenya does not have a pure civil law system, but civil law influences exist through:

- **Roman-Dutch law** — Applied in South Africa, Kenya's neighbour. Though not directly received, Kenyan courts have sometimes cited South African jurisprudence for principles not covered by English common law
- **Ethiopian law** — Some influence in the Horn of Africa context
- **German law** — Had limited colonial influence in Tanganyika (now Tanzania), not directly in Kenya

### Civil Law Characteristics (Contrast with Common Law)

- **Codified law** — Primary sources are written codes (e.g., civil codes in civil law countries)
- **Inquisitorial system** — Judge plays an active role in investigating facts
- **Less emphasis on precedent** — Supreme courts issue binding decisions but codification reduces need for case law interpretation
- **No concept of "equity" as a separate body of law** — rights and remedies flow from the code

Kenya has some ** codified statutes** that reflect civil law influence:
- The **Contract Law** (General Principles) drawn from common law but codified
- The **Sale of Goods Act** (Cap 31) — a UK statute (ancient) applied as received law
- The **Hire Purchase Act** (Cap 29)

### The Commercial Court

Kenya's Commercial Court (established 1999, now part of the High Court) applies both common law principles and codified commercial law. It handles cases involving negotiable instruments, banking, insurance, corporate disputes, and intellectual property.

## 📚 3. African Customary Law

### Constitutional Recognition

**Article 2(5) and (6) of the Constitution of Kenya 2010** expressly recognizes the role of customary law:
> "The general rules of international law shall form part of the law of Kenya under the Constitution."
> "Any treaty or convention ratified by Kenya shall form part of the law of Kenya."

More specifically, **Article 159(2)(c)** requires courts to apply customary law where applicable, subject to the Constitution and written law.

### What is African Customary Law?

African customary law refers to the traditional norms, customs, and practices of Kenya's indigenous communities. It governs areas such as:
- **Land tenure** — Under the Constitution, land may be held under customary tenure (Article 65)
- **Marriage** — Traditional/customary marriages (recognized under the Marriage Act 2014 for registration; the personal law aspects may be governed by customary rules)
- **Succession** — Intestate succession under customary law (for communities that practice it and where parties have not opted for statutory succession)
- **Chieftaincy and leadership** — Community leadership structures, council of elders
- **Offences** — Some traditional dispute resolution mechanisms

### Key Cases on Customary Law

- **Koitalel v Samoei [1900]** — The famous case involving the Nandi chief Koitalel, where the British colonial court addressed customary law of war and sovereignty
- **Re Mumo [2013]** — Courts applying constitutional principles over customary law claims
- **Muru Kunguru [2016]** — Constitutional rights override customary practices inconsistent with dignity
- **Re Estate of Mwaura [2014]** — Application of customary law in succession and the rights of widows

### Limitations on Customary Law

Article 44 of the Constitution protects the right to culture, but Article 159(2)(c) makes clear that customary law applies only where it is **not inconsistent with the Constitution or any written law**. Practices such as:
- Female genital mutilation (FGM) — prohibited by the Prohibition of Female Genital Mutilation Act 2011
- Wife inheritance (*承继*) without consent — potentially unconstitutional
- Denial of education to girl children — inconsistent with Article 43

## 📚 4. Islamic Law

### Recognition in Kenya

Islamic law is formally recognized under the Judicature Act 2011, Section 3(c) which provides that courts may apply Islamic law in matters of:
- **Marriage** (nikah)
- **Divorce** (talaq, khula, faskh)
- **Inheritance** (fara'id)
- **Maintenance (nafaqa)**
- **Trust (waqf)**

This applies to **Muslim parties who voluntarily elect** to have their personal matters governed by Islamic law. The Constitution (Article 170) also provides for Kadhis' courts at the High Court level to handle Islamic law matters.

### Kadhis' Courts

**Article 170(5) of the Constitution** establishes a Kadhis' Court within the High Court, with jurisdiction to hear and determine disputes relating to Islamic law in matters of:
- Marriage, divorce, nullity of marriage
- Dissolution of property relationships (waqf)
- Inheritance and succession where all parties are Muslims

Kadhis' courts operate in Mombasa, Nairobi, Kwale, and other areas with significant Muslim populations. The appointment of Kadhis is subject to nomination by the Judicial Service Commission and appointment by the President.

### Key Features of Islamic Law as Applied in Kenya

- **Nikah** — Islamic marriage contract; must be registered under the Marriage Act 2014 for civil recognition
- **Mahr** — Dower; a contractual obligation payable by the husband to the wife
- **Talaq** — Husband's right to divorce by repudiation; must be pronounced in prescribed form
- **Faskh** — Judicial divorce on grounds such as cruelty, desertion, non-maintenance
- **Fara'id** — Islamic rules of succession (Inheritance Act 2011 partly codifies some aspects for Muslims)

### Relationship with Other Legal Systems

Islamic law operates **alongside** common law, customary law, and statute. Where a Muslim party does not elect Islamic law application, the general law (common law or statute) applies. Courts have held that Islamic law principles may be used interpretatively even where the general law applies to a Muslim party.

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. Kenya is primarily a **common law jurisdiction** — stare decisis applies, the adversarial system operates, equity is a separate jurisdiction
2. **African customary law** applies where consistent with the Constitution and written law — it is not a general override
3. **Islamic law** applies only to Muslims who elect it, and only in specified personal status matters (marriage, divorce, inheritance, maintenance, waqf)
4. The **Judicature Act 2011** Section 3 is the statutory foundation for the pluralistic application of law
5. The **Constitution 2010** is supreme — if customary or Islamic law conflicts with it, the Constitution prevails
6. *Koitalel v Samoei* remains a landmark case on customary law and sovereignty (though colonial-era, still cited on customary law of war/conflict)
7. Remember: Kenya has **no pure civil law system** but has codified elements drawn from common law sources
