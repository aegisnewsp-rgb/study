# Topic 6: Contract Law in Tanzania — Indian Contract Act 1872, Sale of Goods Act 1930, and Related Areas

## 🟢 Quick Review

Contract law in Tanzania is primarily governed by the **Indian Contract Act 1872** (received law from British colonial times) and the **Sale of Goods Act 1930** (also received from British legislation). Tanzania does not have its own comprehensive contract statute — it relies on these received laws. Understanding offer and acceptance, consideration, capacity, discharge of contracts, and sale of goods is essential for examination.

---

## 📜 The Indian Contract Act 1872 — Application in Tanzania

The Indian Contract Act 1872 was enacted by the British colonial administration and applies in Tanzania (including Zanzibar, in appropriate cases). It is the **primary source of contract law** on the mainland.

### Key Definitions (Section 2)

- **Proposal (Offer):** When a person signifies his willingness to do or to abstain from doing something, with a view to obtaining the assent of another person to such act or abstinence, he is said to make a proposal (offer)
- **Promise:** When the person to whom the proposal (offer) is made signifies his assent, the proposal becomes a promise
- **Agreement:** An agreement is every promise and every set of promises forming consideration for each other
- **Contract:** An agreement enforceable by law is a contract

### Formation of a Contract: Key Elements

#### 1. Offer and Acceptance (Sections 3–9)

**Offer (Proposal):** A proposal/offer must be:
- **Communicated** to the offeree (an offer that is not communicated cannot be accepted)
- **Clear and definite** (vague or indefinite offers may be invalid)
- **Distinguished from an invitation to treat** (e.g., advertisements, display of goods in a shop — these are invitations to treat, not offers)

**Acceptance:** The offeree must accept the offer:
- **Communicate the acceptance** (Section 4 — acceptance must be communicated; silence alone is not acceptance)
- **Acceptance must be unconditional** ( Section 7 — an acceptance with modifications is a rejection and a counter-offer)
- **Must be within the validity period** of the offer
- **Must be by the person to whom the offer was made**

**Revocation:** An offer may be revoked at any time before acceptance is communicated. Under Section 6, an offer lapses if not accepted within the prescribed time or, if no time is prescribed, within a reasonable time.

#### 2. Consideration (Sections 2(d) and 23)

**Definition:** Consideration is when, at the desire of the promisor, the promisee or any other person has done or abstained from doing, or does or abstains from doing, or promises to do or abstain from doing, something such thing — it is the "something" exchanged in a contract.

**Rules on Consideration:**
- **Must move at the desire of the promisor** — consideration given spontaneously without request from the promisor is not valid consideration
- **Must be real** — not illusory (e.g., a promise not to sue is valid consideration; a promise not to commit a crime is not)
- **Must have some value** — even nominal consideration suffices
- **Past consideration is not good consideration** (services already rendered before the promise to pay — Section 18)
- **Consideration must be competent** — if the consideration is something illegal or opposed to public policy, the contract is void

**Contracts without Consideration:** Under Section 25, agreements made on natural love and affection (between family members living together) enforceable without consideration — but this must be in writing and registered.

#### 3. Capacity to Contract (Sections 10–12, 24–33)

**Minors (Section 11):** A minor (person under 18 years in Tanzania) is not competent to contract. Any contract with a minor is **voidable at the option of the minor** — the minor may choose to affirm or avoid the contract upon attaining majority. The other party cannot enforce the contract against the minor.

**Note:** A contract for necessaries of life supplied to a minor or a person of unsound mind **can be enforced** for the value of the necessaries (Section 68) — this is a quasi-contractual obligation.

**Persons of Unsound Mind (Section 12):** A person of unsound mind is not competent to contract. Contracts with persons of unsound mind are voidable at their option.

**Disqualified Persons:** Persons disqualified by law from contracting (e.g., adjudicated bankrupt — but this must be expressly provided by law) cannot enter valid contracts.

#### 4. Free Consent (Sections 13–22)

**Consent** is free when it is not caused by:
- **Coercion** (Section 15) — physical force, threat; includes economic duress in some cases
- **Undue influence** (Section 16) — abuse of a dominant position; relationship of trust and confidence
- **Fraud** (Section 17) — false representation of material fact, knowingly or recklessly, intended to deceive
- **Misrepresentation** (Section 18) — innocent or negligent misstatement of material fact
- **Mistake** (Section 20–22) — mutual mistake of fact (not of law), unilateral mistake

Contracts procured by coercion, undue influence, fraud, or misrepresentation are **voidable** at the option of the deceived party. Contracts caused by mistake are **void** (not merely voidable).

## 📦 Sale of Goods Act 1930 (Cap 33)

The Sale of Goods Act (SGA) governs the sale of goods (movable property) in Tanzania.

### Definition (Section 1)

A contract of sale of goods is a contract whereby the **seller transfers or agrees to transfer the property (ownership) in goods** to the **buyer** for a **money consideration called the price**.

**Sale vs. Agreement to Sell:**
- **Sale:** Property transfers immediately on contract formation — the seller is bound to deliver and the buyer to pay
- **Agreement to Sell:** Property transfers at a future date or upon fulfillment of a condition — this is a contract to sell, not an immediate sale

### Conditions and Warranties (Sections 12–14)

Under the SGA, contractual terms are classified as:

**Conditions (Section 12):**
- Terms that go to the **root of the contract** — breach of a condition gives the innocent party the right to **treat the contract as void** (reject the goods) and claim damages
- Examples: description of goods, merchantable quality, fitness for purpose (implied by statute unless excluded)

**Warranties (Section 13):**
- Terms that are **collateral** to the main purpose of the contract — breach of a warranty gives the right to **damages only**, not to reject the goods
- Examples: quiet possession, free from encumbrances

**Implied Terms (Sections 14–16):**
- **Implied condition of title** (Section 14): the seller has the right to sell the goods; goods are free from encumbrances
- **Implied condition of merchantable quality** (Section 15): goods are of satisfactory quality for their ordinary purpose
- **Implied condition of fitness for purpose** (Section 15): where buyer makes known the particular purpose, goods are fit for that purpose
- **Implied condition of sale by description** (Section 15): where goods are sold by description, they correspond to the description

### Transfer of Property (Sections 18–24)

The key rule is that **property passes when it is intended to pass** (Section 19). Courts look to the parties' intention:
- If goods are **unascertained** — property cannot pass until goods are identified and appropriated
- If goods are **specific** (identified at the time of contract) — property may pass immediately

**Rule of *Dunlop v Selfmix*:** In a sale of unascertained future goods by description, property passes when goods of that description are unconditionally appropriated.

### Rights of Unpaid Seller (Sections 39–47)

An **unpaid seller** (one who has not received the full price) has:
- **Right of lien** — retain possession of goods until payment
- **Right of stoppage in transit** — if goods are in possession of a carrier, the seller may stop delivery if the buyer becomes insolvent
- **Right of resale** — sell the goods to a third party and recover damages from the original buyer

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. **Indian Contract Act 1872** is the primary source of contract law in Tanzania (received law)
2. **Section 2 ICA** — definitions of proposal (offer), promise, agreement, contract
3. **Offer must be communicated** to be valid; **silence is not acceptance** (Section 4)
4. **Consideration** must move at the desire of the promisor, be real, have some value, and not be past consideration
5. **Minors (under 18)** cannot contract — contracts with minors are **voidable at the minor's option**
6. **Free consent** — must not be caused by coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, or mistake
7. **Contracts induced by mistake** are **void**; contracts induced by fraud/misrepresentation are **voidable**
8. **Sale of Goods Act 1930** (Cap 33) — governs sale of goods; distinguishes **sale** (property passes immediately) from **agreement to sell** (property passes later)
9. **Conditions** go to the root of the contract (breach = right to reject); **Warranties** are collateral (breach = damages only)
10. **Implied conditions** under SGA: title (Section 14), merchantable quality (Section 15), fitness for purpose, sale by description
11. **Unpaid seller's rights:** right of lien, right of stoppage in transit, right of resale
12. Property passes when **intended to pass** (Section 19) — courts look to parties' intention; for unascertained goods, property passes when goods are unconditionally appropriated
