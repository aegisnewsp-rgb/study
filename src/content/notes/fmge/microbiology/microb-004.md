## General Virology Principles

### Viral Architecture

Viruses are obligate intracellular parasites comprising genetic material (DNA or RNA, never both) surrounded by a protein capsid. Capsomeres are individual protein subunits forming the icosahedral capsid. Enveloped viruses acquire a lipid bilayer membrane from host cells during budding, studded with viral glycoprotein spikes. Naked viruses lack this envelope and are more resistant to environmental stress.

Size ranges from 20 nm (Parvovirus) to 300 nm (Poxvirus). This tiny scale necessitates electron microscopy for direct visualization.

### Viral Replication Overview

The replication cycle begins with attachment where viral surface proteins bind specific host cell receptors. For HIV, CD4 and CCR5/CXCR4 serve this function. Penetration follows through endocytosis or membrane fusion. Uncoating releases the viral genome into the cytoplasm or nucleus. Viral components synthesize according to the genome type — DNA viruses typically replicate in the nucleus using host polymerases, while RNA viruses replicate in the cytoplasm using viral RNA-dependent RNA polymerase. Assembly packages new viral genomes and proteins. Release occurs through budding (enveloped viruses) or cell lysis (naked viruses).

## DNA Viruses

### Herpesviridae Family

Herpesviruses share key features: dsDNA genome, icosahedral capsid, lipid envelope, and the capacity for latent infection in nerve ganglia. Nuclear inclusion bodies (Cowdry type A) characterize infected cells.

**Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV-1 and HSV-2)** causes vesicular lesions on erythematous bases, consulting in groups. HSV-1 produces orolabial herpes while HSV-2 causes genital herpes. Both show multinucleated giant cells on Tzanck smear preparation, though this cannot differentiate HSV from VZV. The virus replicates in the nucleus and establishes latency in sensory ganglia. Treatment with acyclovir, valacyclovir, or famciclovir inhibits viral DNA polymerase. Acyclovir resistance occurs in immunocompromised patients.

**Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)** produces two clinical forms. Primary infection causes varicella (chickenpox) with the characteristic vesicular rash progressing from macules through papules, vesicles, pustules, and crusts in successive crops. The rash spreads centrifugally from the trunk. Reactivation produces herpes zoster (shingles), a painful dermatomal vesicular eruption following a sensory nerve distribution, often with post-herpetic neuralgia. VZV can cause congenital varicella syndrome with limb hypoplasia and cicatricial scarring.

**Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV)** causes infectious mononucleosis characterized by the classic triad of fever, pharyngitis, and lymphadenopathy. Atypical lymphocytosis with CD8+ T cell predominance occurs. The heterophile antibody test (Monospot/Paul-Bunnell) detects IgM antibodies that agglutinate horse or sheep RBCs. EBV associates with Burkitt lymphoma (endemic African form linked to malaria co-infection), nasopharyngeal carcinoma (endemic in Southern China), and post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorder.

**Cytomegalovirus (CMV)** produces an infectious mononucleosis-like syndrome but heterophile-negative. Congenital CMV represents the most common congenital infection, causing periventricular calcifications, microcephaly, hepatosplenomegaly, and thrombocytopenia manifesting as blueberry muffin rash. It is the leading infectious cause of sensorineural hearing loss in children. In AIDS, CMV retinitis shows characteristic perivascular hemorrhages and exudates (pizza fundus appearance). Owl-eye intranuclear inclusions define CMV infection.

### Other DNA Viruses

**Hepatitis B Virus (HBV)**, a hepadnavirus with partially circular dsDNA, uses reverse transcriptase for replication. The virion contains HBsAg (surface/envelope antigen), HBcAg (core antigen), and HBeAg (secreted protein indicating active replication). HBV serology patterns guide clinical interpretation: HBsAg with anti-HBc IgM indicates acute infection; HBsAg with anti-HBc IgG indicates chronic infection; anti-HBs alone indicates vaccination immunity; anti-HBs with anti-HBc indicates resolved natural infection. HBV infection leads to cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma, a leading cause of cancer death globally. Treatment includes nucleos(t)ide analogues (tenofovir, entecavir) and interferon. The recombinant HBsAg vaccine provides effective prevention.

**Adenovirus** is a non-enveloped dsDNA virus causing respiratory infections (pharyngitis, conjunctivitis, pneumonia), epidemic keratoconjunctivitis, and gastroenteritis. The virus is particularly problematic in military recruits and immunocompromised patients.

**Parvovirus B19** is the smallest DNA virus, a single-stranded ssDNA virus. It causes erythema infectiosum (fifth disease) with slapped cheek appearance in children, aplastic crisis in patients with hemolytic anemia, hydrops fetalis in pregnant women, and pure red cell aplasia in immunocompromised hosts.

## RNA Viruses

### HIV/AIDS

**HIV Structure and Replication:** HIV is a retrovirus with an ssRNA genome that reverse transcribes to DNA using reverse transcriptase. The envelope contains gp120 and gp41 glycoproteins mediating CD4 receptor and CCR5/CXCR4 co-receptor binding. The matrix protein p17 surrounds the conical capsid containing the RNA genome and enzymes (reverse transcriptase, integrase, protease).

The replication cycle involves fusion, uncoating, reverse transcription producing proviral DNA, nuclear integration via integrase, transcription producing viral RNA, translation synthesizing proteins, assembly at the plasma membrane, and budding.

**HIV Infection Stages:** Acute infection presents with mononucleosis-like syndrome 2–4 weeks post-exposure during the period of high viremia. Clinical latency follows with gradual CD4 decline over years. AIDS defines itself when CD4 counts fall below 200 cells/μL or when opportunistic infections and malignancies develop.

**Opportunistic Infections:** Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PCP) presents with subacute onset dyspnea, non-productive cough, and bilateral interstitial infiltrates. Candida esophagitis causes odynophagia. Cryptococcal meningitis presents with headache and meningeal signs. Toxoplasma encephalitis shows ring-enhancing brain lesions. CMV retinitis appears as hemorrhagic perivascular infiltrates. Mycobacterium avium complex causes disseminated disease with fever and weight loss.

**HIV Diagnosis:** Fourth-generation immunoassays detect both antibodies and p24 antigen, reducing the window period to approximately 2 weeks. Reactive screening tests require confirmatory testing with HIV-1/HIV-2 differentiation assay or HIV RNA PCR. Antibody-only tests require longer window periods.

**Antiretroviral Therapy:** Combined antiretroviral therapy (cART) suppresses viral replication, restores immune function, and prevents transmission. Drug classes include nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NRTIs like tenofovir, emtricitabine) as backbone therapy, non-nucleoside RTIs (NNRTIs like efavirenz, nevirapine), integrase inhibitors (INSTIs like dolutegravir, raltegravir) now preferred first-line, protease inhibitors (PIs like ritonavir, atazanavir), and entry inhibitors (maraviroc, enfuvirtide). Standard regimens combine two NRTIs with an INSTI.

PrEP (pre-exposure prophylaxis) with daily tenofovir-emtricitabine prevents HIV acquisition in high-risk individuals. PEP (post-exposure prophylaxis) with 28 days of antiretroviral therapy after occupational or non-occupational exposure reduces transmission risk when initiated promptly.

### Hepatitis Viruses

**Hepatitis A Virus (HAV)** is an ssRNA picornavirus transmitted fecal-orally. It causes acute hepatitis only, never chronic infection. Anti-HAV IgM indicates acute infection while anti-HAV IgG indicates past infection or vaccination. The inactivated vaccine provides effective protection.

**Hepatitis C Virus (HCV)** is an ssRNA flavivirus transmitted primarily through blood. It causes chronic infection in 70–80% of cases, leading to cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma. Diagnosis involves anti-HCV antibody screening followed by HCV RNA PCR for confirmation and genotyping. Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) including sofosbuvir, ledipasvir, and velpatasvir achieve cure rates exceeding 95%.

**Hepatitis D Virus (HDV)** is a defective RNA virus requiring HBV co-infection or superinfection. Co-infection produces more severe acute hepatitis while superinfection on chronic HBV causes accelerated cirrhosis progression.

**Hepatitis E Virus (HEV)** is an ssRNA virus transmitted fecal-orally, causing acute hepatitis similar to HAV. Pregnant women face high mortality rates. It is endemic in developing countries.

### Influenza Virus

Orthomyxovirus contains eight ssRNA segments as its genome. Hemagglutinin (HA) mediates viral attachment and entry while neuraminidase (NA) releases newly formed virions from infected cells. Antigenic drift through point mutations produces seasonal epidemics. Antigenic shift through reassortment of gene segments between different strains generates pandemic strains, as occurred in 2009 with H1N1.

Oseltamivir and zanamivir inhibit neuraminidase. Baloxavir marboxil inhibits cap-dependent endonuclease. M2 inhibitors (amantadine, rimantadine) are no longer recommended due to widespread resistance. Annual vaccination with trivalent or quadrivalent vaccines remains the primary prevention strategy.

### Other Important RNA Viruses

**Poliovirus** is an ssRNA enterovirus of the Picornaviridae family. Three serotypes (1, 2, 3) exist. WPV type 2 was declared eradicated in 2015 and type 3 in 2019. The oral polio vaccine (Sabin, OPV) contains live attenuated virus providing intestinal immunity but rarely causes vaccine-associated paralytic polio. The inactivated polio vaccine (Salk, IPV) is safer for immunocompromised individuals.

**Rabies Virus** is a bullet-shaped rhabdovirus with ssRNA genome. The Negri body — eosinophilic intracytoplasmic inclusion in neurons — is pathognomonic. The incubation period varies from days to months depending on bite distance from the CNS. Furious rabies features hydrophobia, aerophobia, hypersalivation, and seizures. Paralytic rabies shows ascending paralysis. Post-exposure prophylaxis includes wound cleaning, rabies immunoglobulin at the wound site, and the rabies vaccine series.

**Dengue Virus** is an ssRNA flavivirus transmitted by Aedes aegypti. Four serotypes exist, with infection by one serotype providing lifelong immunity to that serotype only. Secondary infection with a different serotype increases severe disease risk through antibody-dependent enhancement. Dengue fever causes high fever, retro-orbital pain, myalgia (breakbone fever), and rash. Dengue hemorrhagic fever features plasma leakage, hemorrhage, and possibly shock. Diagnosis uses NS1 antigen detection, IgM/IgG ELISA, or PCR.

**Japanese Encephalitis Virus** is a flavivirus transmitted by Culex mosquitoes with pigs and birds as amplifying hosts. Endemic across South and Southeast Asia, it causes encephalitis with high mortality and neurological sequelae in survivors. The inactivated Vero cell vaccine provides protection.

**SARS-CoV-2**, a novel coronavirus (Betacoronavirus), emerged in 2019 causing COVID-19. The ssRNA genome encodes spike protein mediating ACE2 receptor binding. Respiratory transmission produces manifestations ranging from asymptomatic infection to severe pneumonia, ARDS, and multi-organ dysfunction. Vaccines targeting the spike protein received emergency authorization.

### Frequently Tested Questions

**Q: Which virus causes Burkitt lymphoma?**
A: Epstein-Barr virus

**Q: Congenital CMV findings:**
A: Periventricular calcifications, sensorineural hearing loss, blueberry muffin rash

**Q: Acute HBV infection serology:**
A: HBsAg positive, anti-HBc IgM positive

**Q: CD4 count defining AIDS:**
A: <200 cells/μL

**Q: HIV entry requires:**
A: CD4 receptor plus CCR5 or CXCR4 co-receptor

**Q: Negri bodies identify:**
A: Rabies virus (eosinophilic intracytoplasmic neuronal inclusions)

**Q: Dengue severe disease mechanism:**
A: Antibody-dependent enhancement on secondary heterotypic infection

**Q: First-line treatment for invasive aspergillosis:**
A: Voriconazole

**Q: Hepatitis with chronic carriage and hepatocellular carcinoma risk:**
A: Hepatitis B and C

**Q: Virus requiring both CD4 and CCR5/CXCR4 for cell entry:**
A: HIV
