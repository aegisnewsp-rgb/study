# Topic 8: Legal Profession in Tanzania — Tanganyika Law Society, Admission Requirements, and Regulation

## 🟢 Quick Review

Tanzania's legal profession is regulated by the **Tanganyika Law Society (TLS)** under the Advocates Act. Unlike Kenya, Tanzania does not have a separate Bar School (the Kenya Law School equivalent). Instead, the legal education system relies on university law degrees followed by a period of practical training (pupilage) under a qualified advocate. Understanding the admission requirements, the TLS's role, and the Advocates Apprenticeship Rules is essential for any law student.

---

## 🏛️ The Tanganyika Law Society (TLS)

### History and Establishment

The **Tanganyika Law Society** was established in 1954 under the repealed Advocates Act (Cap 341, Laws of Tanganyika — now replaced by the current legislation). It is the **statutory regulatory body** for advocates in Tanzania (mainland and, in part, in Zanzibar).

**Legal Basis:** The TLS derives its authority from the **Advocates Act (Cap 341)** and the **Tanganyika Law Society Rules**. It is the equivalent of Kenya's Law Society of Kenya (LSK) and Uganda's Uganda Law Society (ULS).

### Membership

Every person admitted as an advocate of the High Court of Tanzania must be a **member of the TLS**. Membership is mandatory for:
- All practicing advocates
- Legal academics (in some categories)
- Others admitted to the Roll

**Annual Practicing Certificate:** Every TLS member who wishes to practice law must obtain an annual **practicing certificate** from the TLS. Practicing without a certificate is an offence under the Advocates Act.

### Functions of the TLS

1. **Regulation of the Legal Profession** — Setting standards, code of conduct, and professional ethics
2. **Admission Support** — Facilitating the admission process for new advocates (though the High Court admits)
3. **Disciplinary Proceedings** — Receiving complaints against advocates; referring serious matters to the **Disciplinary Committee**
4. **Continuing Legal Education (CLE)** — Organizing training and professional development programs
5. **Advocacy and Public Interest** — Representing the legal profession on law reform, filing amicus briefs, and engaging in public interest litigation
6. **Legal Aid** — Providing legal aid services through TLS panels
7. **Welfare and Member Services** — Supporting members through welfare programs and professional networking

### The Code of Conduct

The TLS enforces the **Code of Professional Ethics for Advocates** (based on the now-repealed Rules of Professional Conduct). Key ethical obligations include:
- **Conflict of interest** — an advocate must not act for two parties with conflicting interests
- **Confidentiality** — advocate-client privilege must be maintained
- **Diligence** — advocates must act with reasonable care and skill
- **Independence** — advocates must not compromise their professional judgment
- **Fee transparency** — advocates must not charge excessive fees

## ⚖️ Admission to the Bar — Becoming an Advocate in Tanzania

### Path to Admission

**Step 1: Obtain an LLB Degree**

A person must hold a **Bachelor of Laws (LLB)** from a recognized university. Recognized universities in Tanzania include:
- **University of Dar es Salaam (UoDSM)** — Faculty of Law (established 1961; Tanzania's premier law school)
- **Mzumbe University** — Faculty of Law
- **St. Augustine University of Tanzania (SAUT)** — Faculty of Law
- **Mzumbe University (Mbeya)** and other accredited institutions

The LLB at UoDSM is a **4-year programme** (for A-Level graduates) or 1-year top-up for those with a prior degree. The curriculum covers:
- Constitutional law
- Criminal law and procedure
- Contract law and commercial law
- Property law (land law, succession)
- Public international law
- Legal ethics and professional responsibility

**Step 2: Complete the Advocates Apprenticeship**

After obtaining the LLB, a graduate must complete a **mandatory period of apprenticeship** (pupilage) under a qualified advocate.

**The Advocates Apprenticeship Rules (Cap 341, Subsidiary Legislation):**
- Duration: **12 months** of practical training under a qualified advocate (principal)
- During apprenticeship, the pupil is **not permitted to appear in court** independently
- The pupil must observe court proceedings, assist in legal drafting, and gain practical experience
- The principal advocate is responsible for supervising and training the pupil

**Step 3: Obtain a Certificate of Completion**

Upon completing the 12-month apprenticeship, the principal advocate issues a **Certificate of Completion** of apprenticeship. This is required for the admission application.

**Step 4: Apply for Admission to the High Court**

The applicant must apply to the **High Court of Tanzania** for admission as an advocate. The application is made by **Motion** (a formal court application) supported by an affidavit stating:
- The applicant's qualifications (LLB certificate, certificate of apprenticeship completion)
- Good conduct
- Payment of admission fees

**The High Court** (single judge sitting) reviews the application and, if satisfied, makes an order admitting the person as an advocate. The applicant's name is entered on the **Roll of Advocates** maintained by the High Court.

### Section 10 of the Advocates Act — Requirements for Admission

An applicant for admission must:
1. Be a **Tanzanian citizen** (or permitted non-citizen under law)
2. Be of **good conduct and character**
3. Hold a **recognized LLB degree** (from an accredited university)
4. Have completed **12 months of apprenticeship** under a qualified advocate
5. Have obtained the **principal's certificate** of completion

## 📜 The Advocates Act and Discipline

### The Disciplinary Committee

The Advocates Act establishes a **Disciplinary Committee** (equivalent of Kenya's Disciplinary Tribunal) to hear complaints against advocates. The Disciplinary Committee has the power to:
- **Strike the advocate off the Roll** (most serious sanction)
- **Suspend** the advocate for a specified period
- **Censure** the advocate (public reprimand)
- **Fine** the advocate
- **Order payment of costs** to the complainant

### Striking Off

**Grounds for striking off include:**
- **Misappropriation of client funds** — the most common serious offence
- **Dishonest conduct** — fraud, deceit, cheating
- **Conviction of a criminal offence involving dishonesty**
- **Persistent inadequate professional services**
- **Breach of advocate-client confidentiality**

### Appeals

Appeals from the Disciplinary Committee lie to the **Court of Appeal** of Tanzania.

## ⚖️ Zanzibar's Legal Profession

### Note on Zanzibar

Zanzibar has a **separate legal profession structure** for advocates practicing in Zanzibar courts on Zanzibar-specific matters. The Zanzibar Bar Association exists alongside the TLS for Zanzibar advocates.

**Zanzibar Court System for Advocates:**
- Advocates wishing to appear in Zanzibar courts (for non-Union matters) may need to be admitted to the Zanzibar Court structure or be enrolled in a separate Zanzibar-specific roll

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. **Tanganyika Law Society (TLS)** — established 1954; statutory regulatory body for advocates in Tanzania (mainland)
2. The **Advocates Act (Cap 341)** is the primary legislation governing admission and discipline of advocates
3. **Path to admission:** LLB (recognized university) → 12-month apprenticeship under a qualified advocate → Certificate of Completion → Application to High Court → Admission to the Roll
4. **No separate Bar School** in Tanzania — legal education is university-based; practical training is through apprenticeship under a principal
5. **TLS membership** is mandatory for all advocates; annual **practicing certificate** required to practice
6. **Disciplinary Committee** — has power to strike off, suspend, or censure advocates
7. **Misappropriation of client funds** is the most common ground for striking off
8. Appeals from the Disciplinary Committee go to the **Court of Appeal**
9. **Code of Professional Ethics** — conflicts of interest, confidentiality, diligence, fee transparency
10. Zanzibar has its own **Zanzibar Bar Association** for advocates practicing in Zanzibar courts on Zanzibar matters
11. The **High Court** formally admits advocates; the applicant's name is entered on the **Roll of Advocates**
12. **Continuing Legal Education (CLE)** — the TLS organizes ongoing professional development programs for members
