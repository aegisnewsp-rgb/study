**Moving Charges** — JEE Main / Advanced Study Guide

When a charged particle moves through a region containing both electric and magnetic fields, it experiences the **Lorentz force**: **F = q(E + v × B)**. The magnetic component F = q(v × B) acts perpendicular to both the velocity and the magnetic field direction, never changing the particle's speed — only its path. This is why magnetic fields are used in **velocity selectors** and **mass spectrometers**.

**Magnetic force on a current-carrying conductor**: For a straight wire of length L carrying current I in a magnetic field B, the force is **F = IL × B** (vector cross-product). The direction is given by Fleming's left-hand rule — thumb = motion, forefinger = field, middle finger = current.

**Biot–Savart Law** gives the magnetic field dB at a point P due to a small current element Idl: **dB = (μ₀/4π) · I dl × r̂ / r²**. For a long straight wire, this yields **B = (μ₀I)/(2πr)**, where r is the perpendicular distance from the wire. The direction is given by the right-hand thumb rule (thumb = current, curled fingers = field direction).

**Motion of a charged particle in a uniform B field**: The path is circular with radius **r = mv/(qB)** and time period **T = 2πm/(qB)** (cyclotron frequency ω = qB/m). If velocity has a component parallel to B, the path becomes helical — combining circular motion in the perpendicular plane with uniform drift along the field direction.

**Solved Example 1**: An electron (q = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C, m = 9.1 × 10⁻³¹ kg) moves at 10⁶ m/s perpendicular to a 0.01 T magnetic field. Find the radius of the circular path.
- r = mv/(qB) = (9.1 × 10⁻³¹ × 10⁶) / (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 0.01) = 9.1 × 10⁻⁵ / 1.6 × 10⁻²¹ ≈ **5.69 × 10⁻⁴ m = 0.569 mm**

**Solved Example 2**: A wire of length 0.5 m carrying 2 A current is placed at 30° to a 0.4 T magnetic field. Find the force.
- Using F = ILB sinθ: F = 2 × 0.5 × 0.4 × sin30° = 0.4 × 0.5 = **0.2 N** (direction perpendicular to both wire and field, found using Fleming's left-hand rule).

**Moving Charges** — Comprehensive JEE Notes

**Magnetic force does no work**: Because F = q(v × B) is always perpendicular to velocity v, the dot product F · v = 0 — magnetic force changes direction but not speed, hence does zero work. This has a key corollary: **kinetic energy is conserved** in pure magnetic fields; energy change requires electric fields (Faraday's law) or specially configured magnetic fields (adiabatic invariants).

**Biot–Savart Law from relativistic perspective**: At low speeds (v ≪ c), the Biot–Savart law is accurate. At relativistic speeds, the exact expression is given by the **Lienard–Wiechert potentials**, which reduce to the Biot–Savart form for v/c → 0. The magnetic field of a moving point charge is **B = (μ₀/4π) · q(v × r̂)/r²** for non-relativistic velocities, and becomes increasingly Lorentz-contracted as v → c.

**Torque on a current loop**: A rectangular loop of sides a and b carrying current I in a uniform magnetic field B experiences a torque **τ = I A B sinθ**, where A = ab is the loop area and θ is the angle between the magnetic field and the loop's normal. The **magnetic dipole moment** is **μ = IA** (vector normal to the plane), and the torque can be written compactly as **τ = μ × B**. This is the principle behind **moving-coil galvanometers** and **electric motors**.

**Advanced solved example — Hall effect**: A conductor of width d and thickness t carries current I in a perpendicular magnetic field B. The Hall voltage V_H across the width is given by **V_H = BI/(nqt)**, where n is the charge carrier density. For a semiconductor with n = 10²⁰ m⁻³, t = 1 mm, I = 1 A, B = 0.1 T: V_H = (0.1 × 1)/(10²⁰ × 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 10⁻³) = 0.1/(2.56 × 10⁻²) ≈ **3.9 mV**. The sign of V_H determines whether the charge carriers are electrons (negative) or holes (positive) — a key diagnostic in semiconductor physics.

**Historical note**: Oersted discovered magnetism from current in 1820; Ampere formulated the law of force between current elements a month later. Maxwell later showed Ampere's law must be modified to include the displacement current for consistency with charge conservation (Maxwell's correction ∮ B·dl = μ₀(I + ε₀ dΦ_E/dt)).
