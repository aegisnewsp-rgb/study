**Asphyxia — FMGE Study Guide**

## Definition and Mechanism

Asphyxia is a condition of oxygen deficiency in the blood (hypoxemia) and tissues (hypoxia), leading to increased carbon dioxide levels (hypercapnia). This triggers a cascade: initial hyperventilation → loss of consciousness → convulsions → paralysis → death. The mechanism involves compression of air passages or interference with oxygenation of blood.

## Types of Asphyxia

### 1. Hanging

Hanging is the suspension of the body by a ligature that encircles the neck, with the weight of the body acting as the constricting force. The ligature may be applied to a fixed point above (complete hanging) or with body partially supported (incomplete hanging).

**Mechanism of death:**
- Compression of carotid arteries → cerebral anoxia
- Compression of jugular veins → venous congestion → cerebral oedema
- Compression of airway → airway obstruction
- Compression of carotid sinus → vagal inhibition → cardiac arrest

**Types by ligature position:**
- **Typical hanging:** Ligature passes over the anterior midline of neck (most common — about 80%)
- **Atypical hanging:** Ligature passes on one side or occiput
- **Partial hanging:** Body not fully suspended; feet or knees may touch ground

**Postmortem findings:**
- **Ligature mark:** Oblique, V-shaped; highest point at knot; interrupted at knot position
- **Ligature mark characteristics:** Parchment-like, dry, brownish; width corresponds to ligature
- **Face:** Congested, cyanotic, with petechial haemorrhages
- **Tongue:** May be protruded, bitten, or dark-coloured
- **Neck:** Soft tissues — bruising of sternocleidomastoid, strap muscles; thyroid cartilage fractures possible (especially in hanging >100 kg body weight)
- **Hands and feet:** May show postmortem lividity in dependent parts
- **Signs of struggle:** Usually absent — hanging is typically quick-acting
- **Foreign materials:** Under fingernails may indicate struggle (if not typical hanging)

**Differentiating antemortem from postmortem hanging:**
- Antemortem: Ligature mark with vital reaction (haemorrhage in subcutaneous tissue), petechiae, congestion
- Postmortem: No vital reaction in ligature mark; body may show signs of alternative cause

### 2. Strangulation

Strangulation is external compression of the neck by application of force other than the body weight. Unlike hanging, the constricting force is applied manually or by a ligature without the body being suspended.

**Types:**

**A. Manual strangulation (Throttling):**
- Compression by hands, forearm, or elbow
- Usually homicidal
- External findings: Fingernail abrasions, contusions on neck, crescentic nail mark abrasions
- Internal findings: Fractures of hyoid bone (especially greater cornua in elderly), thyroid cartilage, cricoid cartilage
- Petechiae are prominent due to sustained compression

**B. Ligature strangulation:**
- Loop of rope/cloth/electrical cord tightened around neck
- Ligature mark: Horizontal, encircles neck at uniform level, continuous (not broken at knot — unlike hanging)
- Prominent petechiae
- May show imprint of ligature material
- Respiratory and feeding difficulties if survival period exists

**C. Bansdola:**
- Strangulation using a stick twisted into a cloth around the neck (Southeast Asian method)
- Found in India; named after the device used

### 3. Smothering

Obstruction of the external respiratory orifices (nose and mouth) preventing air from entering the lungs.

**Causes:**
- Hand or palm pressed over nose/mouth
- Occlusion by soft object (pillow, cloth, plastic bag)
- Overlaying (adult sleeping on infant)
- Burking (person sits or kneels on chest of victim while covering face)

**Postmortem findings:**
- No neck findings (unlike hanging/strangulation)
- May show external injuries around nose/mouth (abrasions, contusions)
-殷 Blood-tinged froth from nose/mouth
- Congestion of face, petechiae
- Non-specific internal findings (pulmonary oedema, congestion)

### 4. Choking (Tracheal Obstruction)

Blockage of the internal air passages (larynx, trachea, bronchi) by a foreign body.

**Common causes:**
- Food bolus (meat, fish bone, betel nut)
- Vomitus (especially in unconscious patients)
- Dentures, coins (children)
- Foreign bodies in industrial accidents

**Postmortem findings:**
- Obstruction visible at larynx/trachea
- Congestion and emphysema of lungs
- May show咽喉部 petechiae
- Foreign body must be retrieved and preserved

### 5. Traumatic Asphyxia (Crush Asphyxia)

Occurs when chest and abdomen are compressed, preventing respiratory movements.

**Mechanism:** Forced expiration against closed glottis → extreme intrathoracic pressure → rupture of capillaries → upper body congestion and petechiae

**Classic findings:**
- **Cyanosis of head and neck** (congestion of upper body)
- **Petechiae** in conjunctiva, face, upper body (below clavicle)
- **Subconjunctival haemorrhages**
- **Swelling/cyanosis of face**
- Associated chest/abdominal injuries

**Seen in:** Stampede victims, crowd crush, excavation collapses, heavy object压在胸口

### 6. Drowning (Submersion Asphyxia)

Liquid (usually water) enters the respiratory passages, preventing air exchange.

**Types:**
- **Wet drowning:** Water enters lungs (90% of cases); classical
- **Dry drowning:** Laryngospasm prevents water entry; small amount of water in sinuses/hypopharynx
- **Secondary drowning:** Victim appears to recover but dies hours later due to pulmonary oedema

**Postmortem findings in drowning:**
- **Washerwoman's hands/feet:** Pale, wrinkled, waterlogged skin (after 1–2 hours in water)
- **Cutis anserina (goose skin):** Due to contraction of arrectores pilorum muscles (NOT reliable — may occur after death from other causes in cold water)
- **Fine froth (cup froth):** White/pink stable froth at nose/mouth; persists on wiping
- **Water in stomach:** 90% of drowning victims have water in stomach; indicates water was inhaled
- **Water in middle ear:** Contraversal — may indicate antemortem immersion
- **Lung findings:** Overinflation, pale pink, frothy fluid, subpleural haemorrhages (Paltauf's spots)
- **Haemodilution:** In left heart and pulmonary vessels (though this finding is controversial)

**Freshwater vs Saltwater drowning:**
- Freshwater: Rapid absorption → haemodilution → hypervolaemia → haemolysis → cardiac arrhythmias
- Saltwater: Osmotic gradient draws fluid into lungs → pulmonary oedema → hypovolaemia

**Medicolegal aspects:**
- **Differentiating antemortem from postmortem drowning:** Diatoms in bone marrow (antemortem) — most reliable; putty kidney (long-standing immersion); proof of water in lungs
- In India, drowning is often seen in cases of homicide, suicide, and accident; rarely in infanticide

## Signs Common to All Vital Asphyxial Deaths

1. **Cyanosis** of face and nails — due to venous congestion
2. **Petechial haemorrhages (Tardieu's spots):** Small capillary haemorrhages in skin and viscera (conjunctiva, epicardium, pleura, thymus); caused by rupture of capillaries due to increased venous pressure
3. **Congestion** of face, lips, and neck
4. **Fluid froth** from nose and mouth
5. **Haemorrhages in deeper tissues** of neck

## Manner of Death in Asphyxia

| Type | Usually |
|---|---|
| Hanging | Suicide (most common) or homicide |
| Strangulation | Homicide (usually) |
| Smothering | Homicide (infants: overlay/overlaying) |
| Choking | Accident (often in elderly, children) |
| Traumatic asphyxia | Accident (stampede) |
| Drowning | Suicide, accident, rarely homicide |

- **Section 302 IPC:** Punishment for murder — if death is caused with intention
- **Section 304 IPC:** Culpable homicide not amounting to murder
- **Section 325 IPC:** Voluntarily causing grievous hurt
- **Section 326 IPC:** Grievous hurt by dangerous weapons

**Asphyxia — Comprehensive FMGE Notes**

## Pathophysiology of Asphyxia

Understanding the sequence of events during asphyxia is critical for reconstructing the timeline and manner of death:

**Stage 1 — Distress/Hyperventilation (30 seconds–1 minute):**
- Hypoxia triggers respiratory centre in medulla
- Victim gasps, attempts to breathe
- Blood pressure rises, heart rate increases
- Victim may become combative

**Stage 2 — Loss of Consciousness (1–2 minutes):**
- Cerebral oxygen supply critically reduced
- Consciousness lost
- Convulsions may occur
- Pupils dilate

**Stage 3 — Respiratory Failure (2–3 minutes):**
- Respiratory centre fails
- Terminal gasps (agonal breathing) may occur briefly
- Heart continues briefly but cardiac output fails

**Stage 4 — Death:**
- Irreversible hypoxic damage
- Respiratory and cardiac arrest

**In hanging:** The process may be more rapid due to compression of carotid arteries and carotid sinus stimulation (vagal inhibition). Deaths can occur within seconds to minutes.

## Forensic Pathology of Ligature Marks

The ligature mark provides crucial information about the weapon used and the position of the victim:

**Factors affecting ligature mark:**
- Nature of ligature (soft vs hard)
- Width of ligature
- Force applied
- Duration of suspension
- Weight of body

**Ligature mark interpretation:**
- **Single ligature:** One mark — typical of most suicidal hanging
- **Multiple ligatures:** Multiple parallel marks — suggests repeated attempts or staged scene
- **Patterned ligature:** Shows the weave/pattern of rope/cloth
- **Interrupted mark:** Gap at knot position (hanging) vs continuous (strangulation)

**Histological examination of ligature mark:**
- Haemorrhage in subcutaneous tissue (antemortem)
- Inflammatory cell infiltration (time-dependent)
- Fibroblastic proliferation (if survivable period exists)
- Scar formation (if survival is prolonged)

## Cervical Spine and Soft Tissue in Strangulation

Fractures of the laryngeal framework are important findings:

**Hyoid bone fractures:**
- Most common in strangulation (manual or ligature)
- Greater cornua are most commonly fractured
- Rare in hanging (except in elderly with ossified hyoid)
- Age factor: Hyoid remains cartilaginous until age 20–30; ossifies with age and becomes more brittle

**Thyroid cartilage fractures:**
- Superior horns commonly fractured in strangulation
- Not typically seen in hanging

**Cricoid cartilage fractures:**
- Less common but significant when present
- Indicates significant force

**Important:** Absence of fractures does NOT rule out strangulation — especially in younger individuals where cartilages are more pliable.

## Petechial Haemorrhages — Detailed Analysis

Tardieu's spots are petechial haemorrhages seen in vital asphyxial deaths. They are found in:
- **Skin:** Face, neck, upper chest; sparing lower body
- **Conjunctivae** (most reliable external sign)
- **Epicardiym** and endocardium
- **Pleura** (subpleural haemorrhages)
- **Thymus** (particularly in infants)
- **Brain** (white matter petechiae)

**Mechanism:** Increased venous pressure → capillary rupture → petechiae

**Differential diagnosis (non-asphyxial petechiae):**
- Forceful vomiting
- Coughing paroxysms
- Strangulation
- Tight clothing
- Certain infections (typhus, dengue)
- Thrombocytopenia
- Vasculitis

## Sexual Asphyxia (Autoerotic Death)

Autoerotic asphyxia involves self-strangulation or suffocation during sexual activity to enhance sexual gratification. Features suggesting this:
- Young male (most common)
- Isolated location (bedroom, locked room)
- Presence of pornographic material
- Protective padding around neck to prevent injury
- No suicide note
- Similar cases previously deceased (history of risky behaviour)

**Distinguishing from suicide:** More elaborate setup, evidence of repeated practice, protective padding, presence of pornography, scene consistent with solitary activity.

## Special Considerations for Drowning

**Drowning in other liquids:**
- **Hot water:** Thermal burns + drowning
- **Petrol/benzene:** Aspiration + toxicity
- **Sewage water:** Severe infection risk

**Deaths in bathtubs:**
- Common in elderly (syncope while bathing)
- Sudden cardiac death (arrhythmias triggered by cold water immersion)
- Epilepsy patients may drown during seizure
- Infants: accidental submersion or intentional immersion (infanticide)

**Immersion syndrome (cold water shock):**
- Sudden cardiac death on immersion in cold water
- Vagal stimulation → cardiac arrest
- Surface vital reflex — may survive if rapidly rescued

**Putty kidney (saponification):**
- Long-standing immersion in water → adipocere formation
- Kidneys appear greyish-white, putty-like
- Indicates prolonged immersion (>3 weeks)

## Legal Medicine Considerations

**Inquest procedure:**
- Section 174 CrPC: Police inquest for deaths in custody, unnatural deaths, deaths with suspicious circumstances
- Magistrate inquest for differently classified deaths
- Body must be identified and examined by doctor

**Exhumation:**
- When buried body needs examination
- Requires magistrate order
- Body should be examined within 24 hours of exhumation
- Multiple bodies may be exhumed in case of mass disasters

**Preservation of evidence:**
- Ligature material
- Fingernail scrapings
- Blood and tissue samples
- Foreign fibres
- Water samples from drowning site
