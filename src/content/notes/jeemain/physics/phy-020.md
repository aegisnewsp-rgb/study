**EMI** — JEE Main / Advanced Study Guide

**Electromagnetic induction** occurs when the magnetic flux through a closed circuit changes, inducing an **electromotive force (emf)**. **Faraday's Law** states that the induced emf is proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux: **emf = −N dΦ/dt**, where N is the number of turns. The negative sign encodes **Lenz's Law** — the induced current flows in a direction that opposes the change in flux that produced it.

**Motional emf**: A conducting rod of length L moving with velocity v perpendicular to a magnetic field B develops an emf **ε = BLv**. When this rod forms part of a circuit with resistance R, the induced current I = ε/R creates a magnetic force ILB opposing motion — the basis of **eddy current braking**.

**Self-induction**: A coil of inductance L resists changes in current through it. The back emf induced when the current changes at rate dI/dt is **ε = −L dI/dt**. The energy stored in an inductor is **U = ½LI²**, stored in the magnetic field.

**Mutual induction**: When the current in one coil changes, it induces an emf in a nearby coil: **ε₂ = −M dI₁/dt**, where M is the mutual inductance. This is the principle behind **transformers** and **inductive coupling**.

**Solved Example 1**: A coil of 200 turns encloses a magnetic flux that decreases uniformly from 5 × 10⁻³ Wb to 2 × 10⁻³ Wb in 0.01 s. Find the induced emf.
- |ε| = N |ΔΦ/Δt| = 200 × (5−2)×10⁻³/0.01 = 200 × 3×10⁻³/0.01 = 200 × 0.3 = **60 V**

**Solved Example 2**: A 10 cm long conductor moves at 5 m/s perpendicular to a 0.2 T magnetic field. Find the motional emf.
- ε = BLv = 0.2 × 0.1 × 5 = **1 V** (direction given by Fleming's right-hand rule — use right hand because this is a generator effect).

### 🔴 Extended — Deep Study (3mo+)
> Comprehensive coverage for students on a longer timeline.

**EMI** — Comprehensive JEE Notes

**Lenz's Law and energy conservation**: The minus sign in Faraday's law (emf = −dΦ/dt) is not a convention — it is a direct consequence of **energy conservation**. When a magnet moves toward a coil, the induced current creates a magnetic field that repels the magnet (north faces north). The work done by the magnet against this repulsion is the source of the electrical energy in the coil. This interplay satisfies **Lehv's rule** — also known as the flux rule — and underlies the operation of electric generators and transformers.

**Eddy currents**: When a bulk conductor experiences a changing magnetic field, induced circulating currents (eddy currents) flow within the material. These produce **magnetic damping** — used in eddy current brakes on trains and roller coasters, and in induction heating. However, eddy currents also cause energy loss in transformer cores, mitigated by laminating the core (thin insulated sheets) to increase effective resistance.

**Self-inductance of a solenoid**: For a long solenoid of length l with N turns and cross-sectional area A carrying current I, the magnetic field B = μ₀NI/l. The flux Φ = NBA = μ₀N²IA/l. Hence self-inductance **L = μ₀N²A/l** — proportional to the square of turns, independent of current. Energy stored: U = ½LI² = (μ₀N²A/2l)I², giving magnetic energy density u = B²/(2μ₀) in the core.

**Advanced solved example — LR circuit transients**: An LR circuit with R = 10 Ω and L = 1 H has a battery of emf 5 V switched on at t = 0. The current grows as I(t) = (V/R)(1 − e⁻ᴿᵗ/ᴸ). The time constant τ = L/R = 0.1 s. After t = τ, current reaches 63.2% of its final value (≈ 0.316 A). The voltage across the inductor decays as V_L = V e⁻ᵗ/τ. This transient behavior is critical in designing relay circuits and suppression of voltage spikes in inductive loads.

**Historical note**: Michael Faraday discovered electromagnetic induction in 1831 using a iron ring with two coils wound on opposite sides — one coil connected to a battery, the other to a galvanometer. When the battery was switched on/off, the galvanometer showed a brief deflection — a changing current in one coil induced a current in the other. This discovery paved the way for electric generators, transformers, and the entire electrical power grid.

- Key numerical value: µ0 = 4π×10^-7 H/m (permeability of free space).

- Most tested concept: Lenz's law direction of induced emf; using right-hand rule.

- Common mistake: Forgetting the negative sign in Faraday's law, leading to wrong direction of induced current.

- Exam tip: When asked for direction of induced current, always first determine the direction of change of flux, then apply Lenz's law.
