# Topic 1: Constitution of Tanzania 1977 (as Amended) — Structure and Key Provisions

## 🟢 Quick Review

The Constitution of Tanzania 1977 is the foundational legal document of the United Republic of Tanzania. Unlike Kenya's 2010 Constitution, Tanzania's constitution has been amended piecemeal rather than completely replaced. It establishes a **unitary state** with a unique structure — a union of mainland Tanzania and Zanzibar — and contains detailed provisions on fundamental rights, the structure of government, and the economy. Understanding its structure and key provisions is essential for any Tanzania law student.

---

## 📐 Structure of the Constitution of Tanzania

The Constitution of Tanzania (as amended) has **XIII Parts** organized into chapters and articles. The numbering system uses articles. Key structural elements:

- **Part I** — The Union and its Territory (Articles 1–4)
- **Part II** — The People and the State (Articles 5–8)
- **Part III** — The President and the Vice President (Articles 9–35)
- **Part IV** — The Parliament (National Assembly) (Articles 36–71)
- **Part V** — The Union Government (Articles 72–94)
- **Part VI** — Zanzibar Government (Articles 95–108)
- **Part VII** — The Judicature (Articles 109–139)
- **Part VIII** — The Revolutionary Government of Zanzibar (Articles 110–125, additional provisions)
- **Part IX** — fundamental Rights and Duties (Articles 129–147)
- **Part X** — Financial Matters (Articles 148–158)
- **Part XI** — Local Government (Articles 159–163)
- **Part XII** — Miscellaneous and Transitional Provisions (Articles 164–198)

---

## 🔷 The Union State (Articles 1–4)

### The Nature of the Union

Tanzania is officially **"The United Republic of Tanzania"** — a union formed on 26 April 1964 between Tanganyika (the mainland) and Zanzibar. Article 1 of the Constitution establishes this union.

**Article 4** — The territory of the United Republic consists of:
- Mainland Tanzania (formerly Tanganyika)
- Zanzibar (comprising the islands of Unguja and Pemba)

### Union vs. Non-Union Matters

This is a critical distinction tested frequently. The Constitution divides powers into:

**Union Matters (Article 4(2)):**
These are exclusively managed by the Union Government:
- Foreign affairs and diplomacy
- Defence and national security
- Police and internal security (the Tanzania Police Force)
- Emergency powers and civil defence
- Citizenship and immigration
- External trade and borrowing
- Mainland revenue and customs
- Higher education and university policy
- The Tanzania shilling currency and monetary policy
- Public debt management
- Registration of births, deaths, and marriages (on mainland; Zanzibar has its own registration system)
- Meteorology and seismology

**Non-Union Matters (Zanzibar's Exclusive Competence):**
Zanzibar has its own government (the Revolutionary Government of Zanzibar) with exclusive jurisdiction over:
- Zanzibar's internal affairs
- Zanzibar House of Representatives legislation on non-union matters
- Zanzibar's own Constitution (the Revolutionary Government's Constitution of 1984)
- Zanzibar-specific taxation (within limits)
- Land and agricultural policy in Zanzibar
- Zanzibar's commercial and industrial regulation
- Tourism in Zanzibar
- Islamic religious affairs (Zanzibar has a unique Muslim demographic)

**The Concurrent Power Problem:** Where a matter is not exclusively union or exclusively Zanzibar's, there is a grey area. Disputes on the classification of matters can arise.

## 🏛️ The Executive (Articles 9–35)

### The President

**Article 9** — The President is the Head of State, Head of Government, and Commander-in-Chief of the Defence Forces. The President is also the Chairman of the Cabinet.

**Election of the President:**
- Elected for a **5-year term** in a **first-past-the-post** system
- Must receive a **simple majority** of votes cast (no 50%+1 requirement like Kenya)
- If no candidate receives a majority, a **run-off** is held between the two highest candidates (within 30 days)

**Qualifications of the President (Article 34):**
- Must be a Tanzanian citizen by birth
- Must have attained 40 years of age
- Must be a member of a political party ( Tanzania is effectively a one-party state under CCM — Chama Cha Mapinduzi)
- Must be nominated by a political party

**Term Limits:**
- The President serves a maximum of **two 5-year terms**
- The 2015 amendment (Fifth Schedule) introduced this limit after President John Magufuli served, limiting future presidents

### Vice President

**Article 10** — The Vice President must be elected together with the President on the same ticket (from the same political party). The Vice President acts as President when the President is absent from Tanzania or unable to discharge duties.

## 🏛️ The Parliament — National Assembly (Articles 36–71)

The National Assembly (Bunge) is the legislative organ of the Union. It has:
- **324 members** (as of current composition): elected constituency members, women representatives (30% quota), nominated members, the Attorney General, and the Chief Secretary (ex-officio)
- **Functions:** Legislation, approval of the budget, oversight of the executive, ratification of treaties

### The Speaker

The National Assembly is presided over by a **Speaker** (not the President).

### Legislative Process

A bill becomes law upon:
1. Being passed by the National Assembly
2. Being assented to by the President (within 21 days; the President may return it for reconsideration, but if passed again it becomes law without presidential assent)

## 🏛️ The Judicature (Articles 109–139)

The Constitution establishes:
- **Court of Appeal** — Highest court (below the Supreme Court of Tanzania)
- **High Court** — Superior court with full jurisdiction
- **District Courts, Resident Magistrate Courts, Primary Courts** — Subordinate courts

**Important Note:** Unlike Kenya, Tanzania does **not** have a Supreme Court separate from the Court of Appeal. The Court of Appeal IS the highest court for most matters. A new **Supreme Court** was proposed under the Constitutional Amendments but the full establishment has faced practical challenges.

## 📜 Fundamental Rights and Duties (Articles 129–147)

Tanzania's Bill of Rights is contained in Part IX. Key rights include:

**Right to Life (Article 129):** No person shall be deprived of the right to life intentionally. The death penalty remains lawful for serious offences (unlike Kenya post-2016).

**Right to Personal Liberty (Article 130):** A person arrested must be brought before a court within 24 hours.

**Freedom from Torture and Inhuman Treatment (Article 131):** Absolute protection.

**Right to a Fair Trial (Article 133):** Every accused person is presumed innocent until proven guilty.

**Right to Property (Article 134):** No person shall be deprived of property save under statutory authority and with compensation.

**Right to Privacy (Article 135):** Protection against search, home intrusion, and surveillance.

**Freedom of Expression (Article 137):** Every citizen has freedom of expression and access to information, but this may be restricted by law in the interest of national security.

### Notable Difference from Kenya

Tanzania's Bill of Rights does **not** include socio-economic rights (housing, health, education) as fundamental rights in the same way Kenya's does. Tanzania's constitution is more classical liberal in its rights framework, focusing on civil and political rights.

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. Tanzania is a **United Republic** — a union of mainland Tanzania and Zanzibar (formed April 1964)
2. **Union matters** (Article 4(2)) are exclusively managed by the national government; Zanzibar manages its **non-union** matters
3. The President is elected by **simple majority** (unlike Kenya's 50%+1 requirement); run-off within 30 days if no majority
4. The **Vice President** is elected on the same party ticket as the President
5. **Tanzania is a one-party state** under CCM (Chama Cha Mapinduzi); opposition parties exist but CCM dominates
6. The National Assembly has approximately **324 members** including women representatives, nominated members, the AG, and Chief Secretary
7. Tanzania does **not** have a separate Supreme Court — the **Court of Appeal is the highest court** (proposals for a Supreme Court have not been fully implemented)
8. Tanzania's Bill of Rights does **not** include socio-economic rights as justiciable constitutional rights (unlike Kenya's Article 43)
9. Zanzibar has its **own Constitution** (1984) and its own House of Representatives for non-union matters
10. The **death penalty** remains lawful in Tanzania for serious offences (e.g., treason, aggravated murder)
