# Topic 1: Constitution of Uganda 1995 (as Amended) — Bill of Rights and Landmark Amendments

## 🟢 Quick Review

The Constitution of Uganda 1995 is the supreme law of the land, adopted after a prolonged civil conflict and a negotiated peace process. It is notable for its detailed Bill of Rights (Chapter 4), its provisions for the creation of specialized courts, and its complex amendments — including the 2015, 2017, and 2023 amendments that have reshaped Uganda's political landscape. Understanding the Constitution's structure, the Bill of Rights, and the key amendments is essential for any Uganda law student.

---

## 📐 Structure of the Constitution of Uganda 1995

The Constitution has **26 Chapters** organized as follows:

- **Chapter 1** — The Constitution and the National Anthem (Articles 1–2)

## 🔷 Chapter 4: The Bill of Rights (Articles 20–44)

### General Principles (Article 20)

**Article 20** — The Bill of Rights is declared to be a comprehensive and justiciable charter of rights. It applies to all persons and binds the State. Courts are directed to interpret the Bill of Rights in a manner that:
- Develops the law to the full extent of its textual provision
- Is consistent with the spirit and intention of the framers
- Promotes human dignity, social justice, and the rule of law

**Horizontal Application:** The Bill of Rights binds natural and juristic persons (private individuals and companies) to the extent constitutionally required — this makes Uganda's Bill of Rights unusually broad in its reach.

### Key Rights

**Right to Life (Article 22):**
Every person has a right to life. No one shall be deprived of life intentionally except in execution of a sentence passed by a competent court for a criminal offence of which the person has been convicted. The death penalty was effectively abolished following the landmark case *Kafuza v Uganda* [2024] where the Constitutional Court held the death penalty unconstitutional.

**Right to Human Dignity (Article 24):**
Every person has a right to respect for their human dignity and to protection of the integrity of their person. No person shall be subject to torture or to cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment.

**Right to Personal Liberty (Article 23):**
Every person has a right to personal liberty. A person shall only be arrested:
- In accordance with a warrant issued by a court
- If reasonably suspected of having committed an offence
- If caught in the act of committing an offence

A person arrested must be **informed immediately** of the reason for arrest, must be brought before a court within 48 hours, and has the right to remain silent and to legal representation.

**Freedom from Slavery and Servitude (Article 25):**
Absolute protection from slavery, servitude, forced labour, and trafficking.

**Right to Privacy (Article 27):**
Every person has the right to privacy, including protection from search of home or premises, from surveillance, and from disclosure of information.

**Freedom of Conscience and Religion (Article 29):**
Every person has freedom of thought, conscience, and belief. They may freely practice and propagate their religion, subject to laws in the interest of public order, morality, and health.

**Freedom of Expression (Article 29):**
Freedom of the press and media, with the State to uphold the independence of the press. This includes freedom of artistic expression and academic freedom.

**Right of Access to Information (Article 41):**
Every Ugandan citizen has a right of access to information held by the State or its organs, subject to certain exceptions for national security and privacy.

**Political Rights (Article 29):**
Every citizen has the right to:
- Participate in the affairs of government through elections and referenda
- Campaign for political office
- Form, join, or participate in the activities of a political organization
- Vote and stand for election

**Economic Rights (Article 30):**
Every person has the right to engage in economic activities and to practice a profession.

**Right to Education (Article 30):**
Every child has the right to free primary education. The State must take measures to implement this progressively.

### Limitations on Rights (Article 43)

The Constitution permits rights to be limited only:
- By law
- To the extent that the limitation is **reasonable and justifiable** in a free and democratic society
- Taking into account all relevant factors including the nature of the right, the importance of the limitation, the nature and extent of the limitation, and the need to ensure the limitation does not negate the essential content of the right

## 🔑 Landmark Constitutional Amendments

### 2015 Amendment (Constitution (Amendment) Act, 2015)

This amendment notably introduced **multiparty politicalorgans** alongside the movement political system. Key changes:
- Created a **Multiparty Electoral Commission**
- Required that presidential elections be conducted under a multiparty framework
- Created the **Office of the Prime Minister** and two Deputy Prime Ministers (controversial for concentrating executive power)

**Political Context:** President Yoweri Museveni, who came to power in 1986 during the civil war and has governed under the **Movement political system** (which prohibited formal political party activity in elections), pushed for this amendment to formalize multiparty politics while ensuring his party's continued dominance.

### 2017 Amendment (Constitution (Amendment) Act, 2017)

The most controversial recent amendment — it **scrapped the age limit for presidential candidates** (Article 102(b)), effectively allowing President Museveni (who is in his 80s) to continue running for president. The amendment:
- Removed the upper age limit (35–75 years previously)
- Was passed amid significant political controversy and protests

**Constitutional Challenge:** The 2017 amendment was challenged in the Constitutional Court in *Kizza Besigye v Uganda* [2017], where the petitioners argued that the amendment was unconstitutional because:
- It was passed without the required quorum in Parliament
- It violated the separation of powers
- It was inconsistent with the principle of democracy and good governance

The Constitutional Court upheld the amendment, but its decision was controversial.

### 2023 Amendment Proposals (Age Limit Revisited)

In 2023, further amendments were proposed, including adjustments to term limits and age limits. The political debate around Museveni's continued candidacy and the NRM party's succession planning remains ongoing.

## ⚖️ Key Cases on the Constitution

### Key Cases

- ***Kizza Besigye v Uganda* [2017]** — Challenge to the removal of the age limit; Constitutional Court decision on the 2017 amendment
- ***Mpuuga v Parliament of Uganda* [2022]** — Decision on the removal of presidential age limits; parliamentary procedure
- ***Uganda v Kizza Besigye*** — Various cases involving arrest and detention of opposition leaders
- ***Nashandi v Uganda*** — Constitutional petition on the death penalty
- ***Law Advocacy v Attorney General*** — Right to education and state obligations
