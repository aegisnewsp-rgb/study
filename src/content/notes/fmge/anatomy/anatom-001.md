# Upper Limb — Shoulder, Arm & Forearm

### 🟢 Lite — Quick Review (1h–1d)

**Shoulder Joint (Glenohumeral Joint)**
- Most mobile and least stable joint in the body
- Ball-and-socket joint: head of humerus + glenoid cavity of scapula
- Fibrous capsule attached to anatomical neck of humerus and glenoid rim
- **Rotator cuff muscles** (SITS): Supraspinatus, Infraspinatus, Teres minor, Subscapularis — common site of injury in overhead athletes
- Coracoacromial arch protects the joint superiorly
- **Clinical:** Dislocation most commonly anteroinferior; axillary nerve damage causes deltoid paralysis and sensory loss over lateral shoulder (regimental badge area)

**Axilla**
- Pyramidal space between arm and chest wall
- Contents: axillary artery (divided into 3 parts by pectoralis minor), brachial plexus, brachial vein, axillary vein, lymph nodes (apical, central, lateral, anterior, posterior groups)
- **Clinical:** Axillary lymph node enlargement seen in breast carcinoma — important for staging

**Brachial Plexus**
- Formed from C5–T1 roots
- Trunks: upper (C5–C6), middle (C7), lower (C8–T1)
- Divisions (behind clavicle), cords (lateral, medial, posterior), then terminal branches
- Important branches: musculocutaneous nerve (lateral forearm sensation), median nerve, ulnar nerve, radial nerve, axillary nerve
- **Clinical:** Erb's palsy (C5–C6 injury) → "waiter's tip" position; Klumpke's palsy (C8–T1) → claw hand

### 🟡 Standard — Regular Study (2d–2mo)

**Bony Anatomy of Upper Limb**

**Humerus:**
- Head (articular), anatomical neck, greater & lesser tubercles (separated by bicipital groove), surgical neck (common fracture site in elderly), deltoid tuberosity, radial groove
- Radial nerve runs in the spiral groove — vulnerable in mid-shaft fractures
- Median nerve injured in supracondylar fractures → "finger to palm" sign

**Radius & Ulna:**
- Radius: head (articulates with capitulum and radial notch), radial tuberosity (biceps insertion), ulnar notch
- Ulna: olecranon (forms point of elbow), coronoid process, trochlear notch
- Interosseous membrane connects the two; anterior oblique bundle is strongest part
- **Proximal radioulnar joint:** pivot joint allowing pronation/supination
- **Clinical:** Colles' fracture = distal radius fracture with dorsal displacement (fall on outstretched hand); "dinner fork" deformity

**Joints Around Shoulder Complex**

**Sternoclavicular Joint:**
- Only bony connection between upper limb and axial skeleton
- Articulation: medial end of clavicle + manubrium + first costal cartilage
- **Clinical:** Dislocation rare but life-threatening if posterior → compression of mediastinal structures

**Acromioclavicular Joint:**
- Gliding joint between acromion and clavicle
- **Clinical:** Separated by fall on shoulder; Rockwood classification types I–VI

**Scapulothoracic Movement:**
- Scapula glides on chest wall; elevation/depression, protraction/retraction, rotation
- Important for shoulder abduction beyond 90° (scapulothoracic rhythm)

**Muscles of Arm**

**Anterior compartment (flexors):** biceps brachii (short and long heads), brachialis, coracobrachialis
- All supplied by musculocutaneous nerve
- Biceps: powerful supinator; tendon rupture → "Popeye deformity"

**Posterior compartment (extensors):** triceps brachii (three heads)
- Supplied by radial nerve
- **Clinical:** radial nerve injury in spiral groove → wrist drop

### 🔴 Extended — Deep Study (3mo+)

**Shoulder — Detailed Clinical and Surgical Anatomy**

**Rotator Cuff: Detailed Attachments & Functions**
- **Supraspinatus:** origin from supraspinous fossa → inserts on greater tubercle (middle facet). Initiates first 15° of abduction. Most commonly torn rotator cuff muscle.
- **Infraspinatus:** infraspinous fossa → greater tubercle (posterior facet). External rotation of shoulder.
- **Teres minor:** superior part of lateral border of scapula → greater tubercle (inferior facet). External rotation.
- **Subscapularis:** subscapular fossa → lesser tubercle. Internal rotation; forms anterior wall of rotator cuff interval.

**Coracoacromial Arch:**
- Formed by coracoid process, acromion, and coracoacromial ligament
- Subacromial bursa lies beneath this arch
- **Clinical:** Impingement syndrome — friction between undersurface of acromion and greater tubercle; Neer classification (Stage I: edema/hemorrhage, Stage II: fibrosis, Stage III: bone spurs and tendon tears)

**Shoulder Joint Capsule & Ligaments:**
- Glenohumeral ligaments (superior, middle, inferior) — thickenings of capsule; anterior stabilizers
- Coracohumeral ligament — strengthens superior capsule
- Transverse humeral ligament — holds biceps long head tendon in bicipital groove
- **Bankart lesion:** avulsion of inferior glenohumeral ligament from glenoid rim → recurrent anterior dislocation
- **Hill-Sachs lesion:** compression fracture of posterolateral humeral head

**Venous & Lymphatic Drainage of Upper Limb**
- Superficial veins: cephalic vein (lateral) and basilic vein (medial) — connected by median cubital vein at elbow
- **Clinical:** basilic vein used for peripheral IV access; cephalic vein cutdown for cardiac catheterization
- Deep veins follow arteries (paired brachial veins)
- Lymphatics: superficial follow superficial veins; deep follow brachial artery → axillary lymph nodes

**Cubital Fossa**
- Boundaries: lateral (brachioradialis), medial (pronator teres), superior (interepicondylar line)
- Floor: brachialis (anterior) and supinator (posterior)
- Contents (lateral to medial): radial nerve, brachial artery, biceps tendon, median nerve
- **Clinical:** median cubital vein used for venepuncture; median nerve block at elbow

**Elbow Joint**
- Compound joint: humeroulnar, humeroradial, and proximal radioulnar joints
- Humeroulnar joint is hinge joint; allow flexion/extension
- **Ligaments:** ulnar collateral ligament (medial — anterior, posterior, transverse bundles) and radial collateral ligament (lateral)
- **Clinical:** Tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis) — extensor carpi radialis brevis origin; Golfer's elbow (medial epicondylitis) — flexor pronator origin

**Important Anatomical Spaces**
- **Carpal tunnel:** bounded by carpal bones (floor, sides) and flexor retinaculum (roof). Contains median nerve and 9 tendons. **Carpal tunnel syndrome** — median nerve compression causing thenar muscle weakness and sensory loss in median nerve distribution.
- ** Anatomical snuffbox:** extensor pollicis longus (medial), abductor pollicis longus and extensor pollicis brevis (lateral). Contains radial artery and scaphoid bone. **Scaphoid fracture** — tender in anatomical snuffbox; avascular necrosis risk due to retrograde blood supply.
- **Quadrangular space:** boundaries (subscapularis above, teres major below, long head of triceps medially, humeral shaft laterally). Transmission of axillary nerve and posterior circumflex humeral artery.
