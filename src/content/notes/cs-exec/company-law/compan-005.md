# Winding Up & Corporate Insolvency

### 🟢 Lite — Quick Review (1h–1d)
> Rapid summary for last-minute revision before your exam.

**Winding Up & Corporate Insolvency** — Key Facts for CS Executive
Modes of winding up: (1) Compulsory — by NCLT order under Section 272, (2) Voluntary — members' or creditors' voluntary winding up under Sections 304-316, (3) Subject to supervision of NCLT — rarely used hybrid
NCLT benches: Principal bench in Delhi + regional benches; winding up petition filed where registered office is situated; Grounds: inability to pay debts (proved by statutory demand or 3 unpaid judgments), just and equitable, special resolution
Priority of payments on winding up: (1) Secured creditors (from sale of secured assets), (2) Liquidation costs (liquidator fees, legal costs), (3) Workmen's dues (up to 12 months), (4) Government dues (up to 2 years), (5) Preferential creditors, (6) Unsecured creditors (rateably), (7) Equity holders
⚡ Exam tip: Under IBC 2016, the Insolvency Resolution Process (IRP) suspends all recovery proceedings — even secured creditors cannot enforce security during moratorium period (Section 14) — this has largely replaced winding up for companies that can be revived!

### 🟡 Standard — Regular Study (2d–2mo)
> Standard content for students with a few days to months.

**Winding Up & Corporate Insolvency** — CS Executive Study Guide
Inability to pay debts: Company deemed unable to pay if — (a) debt >₹50,000 unpaid for 21 days after written demand (Section 271(1)(e)), (b) execution decree returned unsatisfied, (c) NCLT is satisfied company is unable to pay its debts
Compulsory winding up process: (1) Petition filed by creditor/member/company/government, (2) NCLT admits petition, (3) Official Liquidator appointed, (4) Statement of Affairs filed, (5) Public examination of promoters (if fraud suspected), (6) Realization of assets, (7) Distribution per priority rules, (8) Dissolution
Voluntary winding up: Members' voluntary — directors declare company is solvent (Form F with CA declaration), 3/4 shareholders approve; Creditors' voluntary — company is insolvent, creditors' committee oversees; neither requires NCLT
Fraudulent winding up: If company wound up with intent to defraud creditors or for fraudulent purpose — every officer/promoter can be held personally liable; prosecution under Section 212 IPC
Oppression & Mismanagement (Section 241-246): Minority shareholders can approach NCLT if company affairs are being conducted in — (a) oppressive manner to members, (b) prejudicial to public interest, or (c) in disregard of minority interests; NCLT can pass any order including winding up (last resort) or buyout of minority shares
⚡ Exam tip: Under Section 271(2), even if a just and equitable winding up petition is filed, NCLT may instead give relief under Sections 241-246 (oppression) if appropriate — NCLT rarely orders winding up unless company is genuinely defunct!

### 🔴 Extended — Deep Study (3mo+)
> Comprehensive coverage for students on a longer study timeline.

**Winding Up & Corporate Insolvency** — Comprehensive CS Executive Notes
IBC 2016 vs Companies Act winding up: IBC (Insolvency & Bankruptcy Code) is now the primary route for corporate insolvency — handles companies that are unable to pay debts ≥₹1 crore; Resolution Professional (RP) manages company during CIRP; 180-day resolution window (extendable to 330 days); if no resolution plan approved, company goes to liquidation under IBC
Liquidator duties: Take custody of company assets, call meetings of creditors/contributories, investigate conduct of directors, realize all assets, distribute proceeds per priority, file annual reports with NCLT, apply for dissolution after all assets realized
Contributories: Present and past members liability — if company's assets are insufficient, past members who ceased membership within 1 year before winding up can be called upon to contribute; liability limited to amount unpaid on shares
Avoidance of transactions (Sections 49, 328-342): Transactions that can be set aside in winding up — (1) Unfair preference (Section 304) — if debtor acted in manner putting creditor in better position, (2) Undervalue transactions (Section 305) — transfers at undervalue, (3) Extortionate credit transactions (Section 306), (4) Fraudulent preference (old Section 332 — now under IBC)
Liability of directors in winding up: Directors not automatically liable — liable only if: (1) contributed to insolvency through negligence/misfeasance, (2) misapplied assets, (3) carried on business with intent to defraud creditors, (4) made false entries or omitted entries from books
Dissolution: After all assets realized and distributed, liquidator files dissolution application with NCLT; NCLT passes dissolution order; company ceases to exist; RoC is notified and company struck off register
 oppression jurisdiction vs winding up: Oppression remedy is MEDIUM — NCLT can: appoint new directors, regulate management, declare dividend, order buyout at fair value; Winding up is LAST RESORT — only when company genuinely cannot function or majority has lost all confidence
Cross-border insolvency: IBC 2016 Section 234 allows recognition of foreign proceedings; India is a member of UNCITRAL Model Law; coordination between Indian and foreign liquidators
