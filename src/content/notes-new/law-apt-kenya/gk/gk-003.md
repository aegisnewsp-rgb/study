# Topic 3: Kenya's Court System — Structure, Jurisdiction, and Key Features

## 🟢 Quick Review

Kenya's court system is hierarchy-based, established under Chapter 10 of the Constitution of Kenya 2010. From the Supreme Court at the apex to Magistrate Courts at the base, each level has defined jurisdiction. The Constitution (Articles 159–175) establishes the courts and guarantees judicial independence.

---

## 🏛️ The Court Hierarchy

### 1. Supreme Court (Article 163)

The Supreme Court is the **highest court in Kenya**. Established under Article 163 of the Constitution, it sits in Nairobi.

**Composition:**
- Chief Justice (Chairperson)
- Deputy Chief Justice
- 5 other Supreme Court judges
- Total: 7 judges

**Exclusive Jurisdiction:**
- Appeals from the Court of Appeal on any matter, at its own discretion (certiorari/lack of judgment)
- Final determination of disputes on **constitutional matters** (original jurisdiction under Article 165(3)(d))
- Interpretation of the Constitution (any court may refer, but Supreme Court has final say)
- Certification that a matter is of great public importance justifying Supreme Court consideration

**Key Feature:** The Supreme Court may hear appeals on matters of public importance without leave. Its decisions are binding on all other courts.

**Constitutional Cases:** Article 163(4) — The Supreme Court has exclusive original jurisdiction to hear disputes arising from presidential elections (after 2017 amendment following the 2017 election crisis — *Raila Odinga v IEBC* case that led to the 2017 amendment).

**Notable Supreme Court Decisions:**
- *Samura Omary v Republic* — Sentencing, murder
- *GP v Republic* — Right to bail
- *Trusted Society of Alliance v Chief Justice* — Removal of judicial officers

---

### 2. Court of Appeal (Article 164)

The Court of Appeal is the **second highest court** and the final appellate court for most matters (short of the Supreme Court).

**Composition:**
- President of the Court of Appeal (a Court of Appeal judge)
- Not fewer than 12 judges (as per constitutional provision, currently more)

**Jurisdiction:**
- Appeals from the High Court
- Appellate jurisdiction on questions of law, fact, or mixed law and fact
- May affirm, reverse, or vary the decision of the High Court
- May order a retrial or refer back to the High Court

**Sittings:** The Court of Appeal sits in divisions (e.g., Nairobi, Mombasa, Kisumu) and at least 3 judges must sit for an appeal.

**Rules:** Governed by the Rules of the Court of Appeal (Cap 9, subsidiary legislation).

---

### 3. High Court (Article 165)

The High Court is a **superior court of record** with unlimited jurisdiction in civil and criminal matters (subject to constitutional and statutory limitations).

**Composition:**
- Chief Justice
- Deputy Chief Justice
- Not fewer than 50 judges (as per constitutional provision, currently over 100)

**Jurisdiction:**

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Civil | All civil matters without monetary limit |
| Criminal | All criminal offences (capital offences tried first in High Court) |
| Constitutional | Original jurisdiction under Art. 165(3)(d) on fundamental rights enforcement |
| Judicial Review | Certiorari, mandamus, prohibition, habeas corpus |
| Admiralty | Maritime law disputes |
| Testamentary | Wills, probate, administration of estates |
| Adoption | Adoption under the Adoption Act |
| Family | Divorces, child custody, maintenance |
| Commercial | Business disputes (Commercial Court division) |

**Constitutional Jurisdiction (Article 165(3)):**

The High Court has jurisdiction to hear:
- Questions of whether a fundamental right has been violated
- Questions of whether any law is constitutional
- Questions of whether presidential election disputes exist (though this was shifted to Supreme Court post-2017)
- Any question arising from the enforcement of the Bill of Rights

**The Commercial Court Division:**
Established in 1999 as a specialized division of the High Court. Handles:
- Banking and financial disputes
- Negotiable instruments
- Insurance and reinsurance
- Corporate disputes (shareholder disputes, winding up)
- Intellectual property
- Foreign exchange transactions
- Admiralty (maritime law)

---

### 4. Environment and Land Court (Article 162)

Article 162(2) establishes the Environment and Land Court (ELC) as a **specialized division of the High Court** with exclusive jurisdiction over:
- Environmental disputes and conservation
- Land disputes (ownership, boundaries, easements, adverse possession)
- Compulsory acquisition disputes
- Land administration

**Composition:** ELC judges are High Court judges specifically designated for this division, appointed through the same process as other High Court judges.

**Land Dispute Process:** The ELC has exclusive jurisdiction over land matters — no other court (including subordinate courts) may hear land disputes. Appeals from the ELC go to the Court of Appeal.

**Notable Cases:**
- *Muis v Registered Trustees of the Kenya Conference of Seventh Day Adventist* — Land rights
- *Green v Minister for Lands* — Land adjudication
- Various cases on community land under Article 63 (community land)

---

### 5. Magistrate Courts (Article 169)

Magistrate Courts are **subordinate courts** established under the Independence Constitution (retained in the 2010 Constitution). They are NOT superior courts of record.

**Structure of Magistrate Courts:**
- **Chief Magistrate** (head of the nationwide magistrate court system)
- **Senior Resident Magistrates**
- **Resident Magistrates**
- **Magistrates** (formerly District Magistrates)

**Jurisdiction:**

| Court Level | Criminal Jurisdiction | Civil Jurisdiction |
|-------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Chief Magistrate | Up to life imprisonment (in practice, capital offences go to High Court) | Unbounded or high limits |
| Senior Resident Magistrate | Up to 15 years imprisonment | Up to KES 1,000,000 or more depending on scale |
| Resident Magistrate | Up to 10 years imprisonment | Up to KES 500,000 |
| Magistrate | Up to 5 years imprisonment | Up to KES 100,000 |

**Appellate Jurisdiction:** From the Senior Citizens and Vulnerable Persons Courts and other lower tribunals.

**Note:** Magistrate courts do **NOT** have jurisdiction over:
- Land matters (exclusive to ELC)
- Admiralty (exclusive to High Court)
- Constitutional matters (original jurisdiction to High Court)
- Questions of constitutional validity

## ⚖️ Key Judicial Principles

### Judicial Independence (Article 160)

- Judges are independent in the exercise of their judicial authority
- They are subject only to the Constitution and the law
- No person or institution may interfere with judicial decisions
- The State must provide adequate resources for the judiciary
- Judges' remuneration is a charge on the Consolidated Fund (Art. 162)

### Security of Tenure (Article 168)

- Judges hold office until they: reach the mandatory retirement age (70 for Supreme Court/High Court judges), resign, are removed for incapacity or misconduct
- Removal requires a petition supported by at least 50 members of the public, followed by a tribunal and parliamentary approval

### Access to Justice (Article 48)

The State must ensure access to justice for all persons, including free legal aid for indigent persons.

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. **Supreme Court** has 7 judges (Chief Justice + Deputy + 5); Court of Appeal has at least 12
2. **Environment and Land Court** is a division of the High Court (not a separate court level)
3. **Magistrate courts** are subordinate — they CANNOT hear land disputes, constitutional matters, or admiralty
4. The **High Court** has unlimited civil and criminal jurisdiction (subject to some statutory limitations)
5. Appeals go: Magistrate → High Court → Court of Appeal → Supreme Court (for constitutional matters or certified important questions)
6. **Constitutional matters** — High Court (original jurisdiction) → Supreme Court (final). Court of Appeal does not handle constitutional original jurisdiction
7. The **Commercial Court** is a specialized division of the High Court — it is NOT a separate court
8. Remember the 2017 amendment shifting presidential election disputes from High Court to Supreme Court (*Article 163(4)(a)* as amended)
