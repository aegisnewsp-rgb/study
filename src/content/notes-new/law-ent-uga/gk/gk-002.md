# Topic 2: Uganda Court Structure — Supreme Court, Court of Appeal, High Court, Magistrate Courts, and LC Courts

## 🟢 Quick Review

Uganda's court system is established under Chapter 13 of the Constitution of Uganda 1995. It runs from the Supreme Court at the apex through the Court of Appeal and High Court to Magistrate Courts and Local Council (LC) courts at the base. Specialized courts and tribunals exist within this framework. Understanding the jurisdiction, composition, and distinctive features of each level is essential for the law examination.

---

## 🏛️ The Court Hierarchy

### 1. Supreme Court (Articles 128–135)

The **Supreme Court** is the **highest court in Uganda** and the final court of appeal on all matters.

**Composition:**
- **Chief Justice** (Chairperson)
- **Deputy Chief Justice**
- **9 Supreme Court Justices** (including the Chief Justice and Deputy)
- Total: 11 judges

**Jurisdiction:**
- Appeals from the **Court of Appeal** on any matter (civil, criminal, constitutional)
- **Original jurisdiction** on questions of constitutional interpretation referred by the Court of Appeal or High Court
- Final appellate authority on questions of law, fact, or mixed law and fact
- Reviews of its own decisions (limited)

**Constitutional Reference:** Article 137 — Questions of constitutional interpretation may be referred to the Supreme Court by any party, but the Supreme Court has original jurisdiction to determine them.

**Key Feature:** Decisions of the Supreme Court are binding on all lower courts. A decision of the Supreme Court Constitution Bench (sitting with at least 5 judges) is binding on all courts.

---

### 2. Court of Appeal (Articles 136–142)

The **Court of Appeal** is the **second highest court** and the main appellate court from the High Court.

**Composition:**
- **President of the Court of Appeal** (who is also a judge of the Court of Appeal)
- Such number of judges as may be prescribed (at least 5)

**Jurisdiction:**
- Appeals from the **High Court** (civil and criminal)
- Questions of law referred by the High Court
- May sit as a **Constitutional Court** under Article 137 to determine questions of constitutional interpretation
- May review its own decisions

**Constitutional Court Function:** Under Article 137, when a question of constitutional interpretation arises in any court (other than the Supreme Court), that court must refer the question to the Court of Appeal sitting as a Constitutional Court. The Constitutional Court consists of **5 judges** of the Court of Appeal.

**Note:** The Court of Appeal also hears appeals from the **Industrial Court** (which handles labour disputes).

---

### 3. High Court (Articles 139–152)

The **High Court** is a **superior court of record** with unlimited original jurisdiction in civil and criminal matters.

**Composition:**
- **Chief Justice** (who is also head of the High Court and all courts)
- **Deputy Chief Justice**
- **Judges of the High Court** (at least 50 as per constitutional provision)

**Jurisdiction:**

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Civil | All civil matters without monetary limit |
| Criminal | All criminal offences including capital offences |
| Constitutional | Questions of constitutional rights (original jurisdiction under Art. 137) |
| Judicial Review | Certiorari, mandamus, prohibition, habeas corpus |
| Family | Divorce, custody, maintenance |
| Probate | Wills, administration of estates |
| Commercial | Corporate, banking, insurance disputes |
| Land | Land disputes (Land Courts division) |
| Admiralty | Maritime law disputes |

**Specialized Divisions:**
The High Court has established specialized divisions:
- **Commercial Court** — Banking, insurance, corporate, and intellectual property disputes
- **Family Law Division** — Divorce, child custody, adoption
- **Land Division** — Land ownership, boundaries, and registration disputes
- **Criminal Division** — Serious criminal offences (capital offences, complex crimes)

**Original Constitutional Jurisdiction (Article 137):**
A person may apply to the High Court directly to enforce the Bill of Rights. The High Court has jurisdiction to hear and determine questions of constitutional interpretation.

---

### 4. Magistrate Courts (Chapter 14, Articles 250–255)

Magistrate Courts are **subordinate courts** presided over by magistrates appointed under the Judicature Act. They are NOT superior courts of record.

**Structure:**

| Court Level | Criminal Jurisdiction | Civil Jurisdiction |
|-------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Chief Magistrate** | Up to life imprisonment | Up to UGX 100,000,000 |
| **Principal Magistrate** | Up to 15 years imprisonment | Up to UGX 50,000,000 |
| **Senior Magistrate** | Up to 10 years imprisonment | Up to UGX 20,000,000 |
| **Magistrate Grade I** | Up to 5 years imprisonment | Up to UGX 10,000,000 |
| **Magistrate Grade II** | Up to 3 years imprisonment | Up to UGX 3,000,000 |

**Note:** Uganda's magistrate courts have **monetary limits** set by the Magisterial Divisions Act. Cases exceeding the monetary limit must be referred to the High Court.

**Appellate Jurisdiction:** Magistrate courts hear appeals from lower tribunals and LC courts.

---

### 5. Local Council (LC) Courts (Article 129, Fourth Schedule)

**LC courts** are the **lowest level courts** in Uganda, established under the **Local Government Act 1997** and the Fourth Schedule of the Constitution.

**Jurisdiction:**
- Minor criminal offences (disputes, assaults, theft of small value)
- Civil disputes (small claims involving land, inheritance, family matters at community level)
- Offences against local bylaws

**Structure:**
- **LC 1** — Village level (lowest)
- **LC 2** — Parish level
- **LC 3** — Sub-County/Municipality level
- **LC 4** — County level
- **LC 5** — District level

**Note:** LC courts are primarily dispute resolution mechanisms at the community level. They are not courts of record and are not superior courts.

**Procedure:** LC courts follow **customary law and procedure** rather than formal rules of evidence. They are designed to provide accessible, community-level justice.

**Appeals:** From LC courts, appeals go to the **Magistrate Court** at the appropriate level.

## ⚖️ Key Judicial Principles

### Judicial Independence (Article 128)

- The judiciary is independent in the exercise of its judicial authority
- Judges are subject only to the Constitution and the law
- No person or institution may interfere with judicial decisions
- The State must provide adequate resources for the judiciary

### Security of Tenure (Article 142)

- Judges hold office until **mandatory retirement age** (60 years for most judges; possible extension to 70 for Supreme Court and Court of Appeal judges)
- Judges may only be removed for **incapacity or misconduct** following a tribunal recommendation
- The removal process requires parliamentary approval

### Access to Justice (Article 28)

The Constitution guarantees every person the right to **have their case heard and determined within a reasonable time** by a court or other tribunal. This is the right to a fair and expeditious trial.

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. **Supreme Court** — 11 judges (Chief Justice + Deputy Chief Justice + 9 others); highest court; final appellate authority
2. **Court of Appeal** — second highest; sits as Constitutional Court under Article 137 when constitutional questions arise
3. **High Court** — unlimited civil and criminal jurisdiction; superior court of record
4. **Magistrate courts** — subordinate courts; have **monetary limits** on civil jurisdiction; criminal jurisdiction up to life imprisonment at Chief Magistrate level
5. **LC courts** — community-level dispute resolution; operate under customary procedure; not superior courts of record
6. **Article 137** — High Court has original constitutional jurisdiction; Court of Appeal sits as Constitutional Court (5 judges) for questions of constitutional interpretation
7. **Supreme Court decisions** are binding on all lower courts
8. **Judicial independence** — Article 128 guarantees independence; Article 142 provides security of tenure
9. Judges hold office until **60 years** (general) or **70 years** (Supreme Court/Court of Appeal)
10. The right to a fair trial within a **reasonable time** is guaranteed under Article 28
