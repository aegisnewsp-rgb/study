# Nutrition in Plants and Animals

## Nutrition: An Overview

Nutrition is the process by which organisms obtain and utilize food for energy, growth, and maintenance. Organisms can be classified based on how they obtain nutrition:

- **Autotrophs:** Organisms that synthesize their own food from inorganic substances (e.g., green plants — photoautotrophs; chemosynthetic bacteria — chemoautotrophs)
- **Heterotrophs:** Organisms that cannot synthesize their own food and depend on other organisms (e.g., animals, fungi, non-green plants like mushroom)

### Mode of Nutrition in Plants

**Photosynthesis** is the process by which green plants manufacture food using sunlight, carbon dioxide, and water. It occurs in the **chloroplasts** containing chlorophyll pigment.

**Chemical Equation:**
> 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ (in presence of sunlight and chlorophyll)

**Process of Photosynthesis:**

1. **Light Reaction (Photochemical phase):** Occurs in the thylakoid membranes of grana. Chlorophyll absorbs light energy and splits water molecules (photolysis), releasing oxygen and producing ATP and NADPH.
   - **Products:** ATP, NADPH, O₂
   - **Location:** Grana of chloroplast
   - **Byproduct:** O₂ is released as a waste product

2. **Dark Reaction (Biosynthetic phase / Calvin Cycle):** Occurs in the stroma of chloroplast. CO₂ from the atmosphere is fixed using ATP and NADPH to produce glucose. This does not directly require light but uses products of light reaction.
   - **Location:** Stroma of chloroplast
   - **Key enzyme:** RuBisCO (Ribulose bisphosphate carboxylase)
   - **End product:** Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆)

**Key CTET Fact:** The raw materials for photosynthesis are **carbon dioxide** and **water**. Sunlight provides energy. The by-product is **oxygen**, which is released into the atmosphere — essential for survival of most living organisms.

**Other Modes of Nutrition in Plants:**
- **Saprophytic:** Decomposers like mushrooms obtain food from dead and decaying organic matter
- **Parasitic:** Plants like Cuscuta (dodder) absorb prepared food from the host plant
- **Insectivorous:** Plants like Venus flytrap and pitcher plant digest insects to supplement nitrogen deficiency

### Human Digestive System

The human digestive system is a complete **alimentary canal** (mouth to anus) associated with digestive glands. It consists of:

**Mouth → Pharynx → Oesophagus → Stomach → Small intestine → Large intestine → Rectum → Anus**

**Associated glands:** Salivary glands (3 pairs), Liver, Pancreas

#### Process of Digestion

| Part | Secretion | Function |
|------|-----------|----------|
| **Mouth** | Saliva (amylase/ptyalin) | Begins starch digestion (starch → maltose) |
| **Stomach** | Gastric juice (HCl + pepsin + rennin) | Protein digestion (pepsinogen → pepsin); HCl creates acidic pH (~1.5–2) |
| **Small intestine** | Pancreatic juice (trypsin, lipase, amylase); Bile (from liver — emulsifies fats); Intestinal juice (maltase, peptidases) | Complete digestion of carbohydrates, proteins, and fats |
| **Large intestine** | Mucus | Water absorption, formation of feces |

**Final absorption:** The inner surface of the small intestine has **villi** (finger-like projections) that increase surface area for absorption. Nutrients diffuse into blood capillaries and lymph vessels of the villi.

**Key enzymes and their functions:**
- **Amylase:** Breaks starch into maltose (salivary and pancreatic)
- **Pepsin:** Breaks proteins into peptones (stomach)
- **Trypsin:** Breaks proteins into peptides (pancreas)
- **Lipase:** Breaks fats into fatty acids and glycerol
- **Maltase:** Breaks maltose into glucose

### Balanced Diet and Nutrition

A balanced diet contains adequate amounts of **carbohydrates** (energy), **proteins** (growth and repair), **fats** (energy and insulation), **vitamins** (regulatory functions), **minerals** (structural and regulatory), and **water** (transport and metabolic functions).

**Deficiency diseases:**
- **Vitamin A** → Night blindness, Xerophthalmia
- **Vitamin B1 (Thiamine)** → Beriberi
- **Vitamin C** → Scurvy
- **Vitamin D** → Rickets (bone deformity in children)
- **Iron** → Anaemia
- **Iodine** → Goitre

### Plant Nutrition: Minerals and Fertilizers

Plants require **essential mineral elements** for healthy growth. These are classified as:

**Macronutrients** (required in larger quantities): N, P, K, Ca, Mg, S
**Micronutrients** (required in trace amounts): Fe, Mn, Zn, Cu, B, Mo, Cl, Ni

**Important functions:**
- **Nitrogen (N):** Promotes leaf growth (vegetative growth). Deficiency causes yellowing of leaves (chlorosis).
- **Phosphorus (P):** Promotes root development and flowering. Deficiency causes purple coloration in leaves.
- **Potassium (K):** Strengthens stems, increases disease resistance.
- **Iron (Fe):** Essential for chlorophyll synthesis.
- **Magnesium (Mg):** Central atom in chlorophyll molecule.

**Fertilizers:**
- **NPK fertilizers** are the most commonly used (Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Potassium)
- **Urea** is the most common nitrogenous fertilizer
- **DAP (Diammonium phosphate)** is a common phosphorus fertilizer
- **Potassium chloride** and **Potassium sulphate** are potassium fertilizers
- Organic fertilizers include **farmyard manure**, **compost**, and **vermicompost**

⚡ **CTET Exam Focus:** Students must know the difference between macronutrients and micronutrients, deficiency symptoms of major nutrients, and the role of NPK fertilizers. The light and dark reactions of photosynthesis are frequently asked.
