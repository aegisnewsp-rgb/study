# Abdomen and Pelvis — GI Organs, Peritoneum, Kidneys, Pelvic Viscera and Nerves

### 🟢 Lite — Quick Review (1h–1d)
> Rapid summary for last-minute revision before your exam.

Abdominal and pelvic anatomy are frequently tested in INI CET, especially the peritoneal relations of abdominal organs, blood supply to GI organs, and the course of the ureters. Focus on the portocaval system, the three layers of the abdominal wall, and the anatomy of the pelvis.

**High-Yield Facts for INI CET:**
- Peritoneum: Parietal (lines wall) vs visceral (covers organs); peritoneum forms mesenteries, omenta, and ligaments
- Lesser sac (omental bursa): Posterior to stomach and lesser omentum; communicates with greater sac via omental (Epiploic) foramen (of Winslow)
- Liver: Right lobe (larger), left lobe (smaller), caudate and quadrate lobes; portal triad (hepatic portal vein, hepatic artery, bile duct); supplied by hepatic artery (25%) and portal vein (75%); venous drainage by hepatic veins to IVC
- Kidneys: Retroperitoneal; right kidney lower (liver); left kidney higher (protected by spleen); cortex + medulla (pyramids); renal sinus; ureter descends to bladder

⚡ **Exam tip:** Stomach has lesser curvature (supplied by left gastric artery) and greater curvature (supplied by short gastric and left gastro-omental artery). Pancreas: head (uncinate process touching IVC), neck, body, tail; crosses anatomical structures at L1-L2 — the "submarines" behind the stomach.

### 🟡 Standard — Regular Study (2d–2mo)
> Standard content for students with a few days to months.

**Abdominal Wall and Regions:**

#### Quadrants and Regions
- 9 regions: Right hypochondrium, epigastrium, left hypochondrium; right lumbar, umbilical, left lumbar; right iliac fossa, suprapubic, left iliac fossa
- McBurney's point: 1/3 distance from ASIS to umbilicus — marks the surface projection of the appendix; incision for appendicectomy
- Costal margin: Formed by costal cartilages of ribs 7-12; contains: Right hypochondrium (liver, gallbladder, right kidney), epigastrium (stomach, pancreas, aorta), left hypochondrium (spleen, left kidney, stomach tail)

#### Anterior Abdominal Wall
- Layers: Skin → Camper's fascia (fat) → Scarpa's fascia (membranous) → external oblique aponeurosis → internal oblique → transversus abdominis → transversalis fascia → extraperitoneal fat → parietal peritoneum
- **External oblique:** Fibres inferomedial ("hands in pockets"); forms inguinal ligament (Poupart's ligament) at its lower free border; also forms lacunar ligament (Gimbernat's) at medial attachment
- **Internal oblique:** Fibres superomedial; cremaster muscle (from internal oblique) covers spermatic cord
- **Transversus abdominis:** Deepest; transverse fibres; forms conjoint tendon (shared aponeurosis with internal oblique) medial to rectus

**Rectus abdominis:** Paired muscle in rectus sheath; segmented by tendinous intersections; stabilises pelvis + flexes trunk; supplied by superior epigastric artery (continuation of internal thoracic) and inferior epigastric artery (from external iliac); drains to internal thoracic vein
- **Rectus sheath:** Formed by aponeuroses of external oblique, internal oblique, and transversus; above arcuate line (halfway between umbilicus and pubic symphysis): anterior wall formed by external oblique aponeurosis + half of internal oblique aponeurosis; posterior wall formed by rest of internal oblique aponeurosis + transversus aponeurosis; below arcuate line: all three aponeuroses go to anterior wall; posterior wall has only transversalis fascia

**Inguinal Canal:**
- Site of potential weakness; 4cm long; runs from deep (internal) inguinal ring (midpoint of inguinal ligament) to superficial (external) inguinal ring (aperture in external oblique aponeurosis)
- Contains: Spermatic cord in males, round ligament in females; ilioinguinal nerve (C1 — emerges from superficial ring)
- **Deep inguinal ring:** Lateral to inferior epigastric vessels; lateral to rectus abdominis; congenital origin of indirect inguinal hernia
- **Superficial ring:** Above pubic tubercle; medial to inferior epigastric vessels
- Direct inguinal hernia: Medial to inferior epigastric vessels; acquired (weakness in Hesselbach triangle); covered by transversalis fascia
- Indirect inguinal hernia: Lateral to inferior epigastric vessels; congenital (patent processus vaginalis); passes through deep ring, canal, and may exit superficial ring; covered by all three layers (external spermatic fascia from external oblique, cremasteric from internal oblique, internal spermatic fascia from transversalis)
- Hesselbach triangle: Medial border = lateral edge of rectus abdominis; Lateral border = inferior epigastric vessels; Inferior border = inguinal ligament; direct hernia exits here

### 🔴 Extended — Deep Study (3mo+)
> Comprehensive coverage for students on a longer study timeline.

**Peritoneum and GI Organs:**

#### Peritoneum

**Parietal peritoneum:** Lines abdominal and pelvic walls; sensitive to pain (somatic) — parietal peritoneal irritation causes localised, well-defined pain; irritation of central diaphragm (phrenic nerve irritation → shoulder tip pain — referred pain)

**Visceral peritoneum:** Covers abdominal organs; sensitive only to stretch — deep visceral pain is poorly localised, often referred to T5-L1 dermatomes

**Intraperitoneal vs retroperitoneal:**
- **Intraperitoneal:** Covered by visceral peritoneum; has mesentery connecting to posterior abdominal wall; mobile (except during inflammation); examples: stomach, jejunum, ileum, transverse colon, sigmoid colon, liver, spleen, appendix
- **Retroperitoneal:** Only partially covered by peritoneum (anterior surface); not mobile; examples: duodenum (except first part), pancreas (except tail), ascending and descending colon, kidneys, adrenal glands, aorta, IVC

**Peritoneal folds:**
- **Greater omentum:** Double layer of peritoneum from greater curvature of stomach → drapes over transverse colon and small intestine; contains fat (omental apron); has immune function (contains macrophages); surgical barrier; can be used to wrap anastomosis
- **Lesser omentum:** Connects lesser curvature of stomach and proximal duodenum to liver; contains hepatic portal triad (portal vein, hepatic artery, bile duct); divided into: hepatogastric ligament + hepatoduodenal ligament (forms anterior boundary of omental foramen)
- **Mesenteries:** Double-layered peritoneum connecting intraperitoneal organs to posterior abdominal wall: mesentery of small intestine (jejunoileum), transverse mesocolon (transverse colon), sigmoid mesocolon (sigmoid colon), mesoappendix (appendix), falciform ligament (liver to anterior wall)
- **Omental (Epiploic) foramen (of Winslow):** Entry to lesser sac; bounded anteriorly by hepatoduodenal ligament (portal vein, hepatic artery, bile duct), posteriorly by IVC, superiorly by caudate lobe of liver, inferiorly by duodenum

**Lesser sac (omental bursa):** Behind stomach and lesser omentum; communicates with greater sac via epiploic foramen; bounded anteriorly by stomach and lesser omentum; posteriorly by pancreas, aorta, left kidney, left adrenal; superiorly by liver; inferiorly by transverse mesocolon

**Mesenteric circulation:**
- **Ventral mesentery derivatives:** Lesser omentum, falciform ligament, mesentery of foregut
- **Dorsal mesentery:** Greater omentum, mesenteries of midgut and hindgut

#### Stomach

**Surfaces and borders:**
- Lesser curvature (right border) — supplied by left gastric artery (from coeliac trunk)
- Greater curvature (left border) — supplied by short gastric + left gastro-omental artery (from splenic artery)
- Anterior surface and posterior surface
- Cardiac opening (oesophagogastric junction — T10/11), pylorus (GIT smooth muscle, T12-L1)

**Regions:** Cardia, fundus (above oesophageal opening), body, pyloric antrum and canal
**Innervation:** Vagal trunks (parasympathetic) — anterior (left) and posterior (right) vagal trunks; sympathetics from coeliac plexus (T6-T10)

**Blood supply:**
- Left gastric (coeliac): Lesser curvature
- Right gastric (proper hepatic): Lesser curvature
- Short gastric (splenic): Fundus
- Left gastro-omental (splenic): Greater curvature
- Right gastro-omental (gastroduodenal): Greater curvature

#### Duodenum

**Four parts (C-shaped around head of pancreas):**
- D1 (superior): Short, intraperitoneal; first part of foregut; attached to lesser omentum; contains entry of common bile duct and pancreatic duct (hepatopancreatic ampulla of Vater)
- D2 (descending): Retroperitoneal; second part of foregut; contains major duodenal papilla (entry of bile + pancreatic duct); second part of midgut
- D3 (horizontal): Retroperitoneal; crosses IVC and aorta; second part of midgut
- D4 (ascending): Retroperitoneal; third part of midgut; ascends to duodojejunal flexure; suspended by ligament of Treitz (suspensory muscle of duodenum — bands of smooth muscle + CT from right crus of diaphragm)

**Pancreas**

**Head (in C-shaped curve of duodenum):** Uncinate process extends behind superior mesenteric vessels
- Neck: Anterior to portal vein; between head and body
- Body: Crosses L1; posterior to stomach; tail reaches splenic hilum
- Pancreatic duct: Runs through tail to head → joins bile duct at hepatopancreatic ampulla (Vater's); sphincter of Oddi controls entry
- **Exocrine function:** Digestive enzymes — trypsin, chymotrypsin, carboxypeptidase (all secreted as inactive proenzymes), amylase, lipase, nucleases
- **Endocrine function:** Islets of Langerhans — alpha (glucagon), beta (insulin), delta (somatostatin), PP (pancreatic polypeptide)

**Accessory pancreatic duct (Santorini):** Drains separately at minor duodenal papilla in D1

**Liver and Biliary System:**

**Liver anatomy:**
- Anatomical: Right lobe (larger), left lobe (smaller), caudate (dorsal), quadrate (ventral) — though functionally right lobe is actually divided by Cantlie's line
- Functional: Right lobe divided into anterior and posterior segments by right hepatic vein; left into medial and lateral by falciform ligament; 8 segments by Couinaud classification (based on portal triad distribution)
- **Porta hepatis:** Entry of hepatic portal vein and hepatic artery, exit of hepatic ducts; lies between caudate and quadrate lobes
- **Gallbladder:** Attached to liver by cystic duct; Hartmann's pouch (constriction at neck — common site of gallstone impaction); fundus (rounded, projects beyond liver edge); body; neck (narrow, connects to cystic duct); Riedel's lobe (anatomical variant — elongated right lobe)
- **Biliary tree:** Right hepatic duct (from right lobe) + left hepatic duct (from left lobe) → common hepatic duct → joins cystic duct → common bile duct (CBD) → runs in lesser omentum → behind D1 → through head of pancreas → hepatopancreatic ampulla of Vater → major duodenal papilla (D2)

**Portal Venous System:**
- Splenic vein + superior mesenteric vein → hepatic portal vein → liver
- Portal vein: Supplies 75% of liver blood supply (75-80mL/100g/min); hepatic artery supplies remaining 25%
- Portocaval anastomoses (sites where portal and systemic venous systems connect — portal hypertension leads to dilation and bleeding):
  1. Oesophageal varices: Oesophageal veins (tributary of azygos) ↔ left gastric (portal)
  2. Rectal varices: Superior rectal (portal) ↔ middle and inferior rectal (systemic)
  3. Paraumbilical: Paraumbilical veins (portal) ↔ superficial epigastric (systemic) → caput medusae
  4. Retroperitoneal: Colic veins (portal) ↔ renal and lumbar veins (systemic)

**Spleen:**
- Intraperitoneal (attached to greater omentum); largest lymphoid organ
- Functions: Filters blood, removes old RBCs, immune function, blood reservoir
- Surface anatomy: 9th-11th ribs, between midaxillary and posterior axillary lines; weighs ~150g; 12cm long
- **Not palpable unless enlarged** (splenomegaly) — then palpable below left costal margin; causes: malaria, CLD, leukaemia, lymphoma, haemolytic anaemia
- **White pulp:** Lymphoid follicles with central arterioles — immune function
- **Red pulp:** Splenic cords + venous sinuses — filters blood
- Blood supply: Splenic artery (from coeliac); splenic vein → IMV → portal vein

#### Large Intestine

**Key features of large intestine:** Taeniae coli (three longitudinal muscle bands — haustra between them), omental appendices (fatty tags), epiploic appendices

**Caecum and appendix:**
- Caecum: Intraperitoneal; ascending colon begins here; vermiform appendix attaches at posteromedial wall at McBurney's point; ileocaecal valve (ileum opens into caecum)
- Appendix: Mesoappendix (contains appendicular artery — branch of ileocolic artery); different positions (retrocaecal most common, then pelvic, then subcaecal, then pre-ileal); McBurney's point = surface marking for appendicitis; no function (vestigial)
- Blood supply: Ileocolic artery (from SMA) → appendicular artery

**Colon:**
- Ascending colon: Retroperitoneal; hepatic flexure (right colic flexure) at liver; blood supply from SMA (ileocolic → right colic)
- Transverse colon: Intraperitoneal; suspended by transverse mesocolon; splenic flexure (left colic flexure) at spleen; blood supply from SMA (middle colic) and IMA (left colic)
- Descending colon: Retroperitoneal; left colic flexure to sigmoid; blood supply from IMA (left colic → marginal artery → sigmoid branches)
- Sigmoid colon: Intraperitoneal; S-shaped; blood supply from IMA (sigmoid arteries → marginal artery); sigmoid mesocolon attaches to posterior abdominal wall
- Blood supply: SMA supplies midgut derivatives (duodenum D1 to proximal 2/3 transverse colon); IMA supplies hindgut derivatives (distal 1/3 transverse to upper rectum)

**Rectum:** 12cm long; begins at S3 level;有三个横向皱襞 (Houston's valves — used as landmark for digital examination); peritoneum only on anterior surface; supplied by superior (IMA), middle and inferior (internal iliac) rectal arteries; middle and inferior rectal veins → internal iliac → common iliac → IVC (portocaval anastomosis at upper rectum); portal hypertension → superior rectal varices

#### Kidneys

**Location and structure:**
- Retroperitoneal; T12-L3; right slightly lower (liver); left slightly higher (spleen)
- Each kidney ~11cm; weighs 120-170g; surrounded by perirenal fat and renal fascia (of Gerota)
- Capsule: Smooth, fibrous; can be stripped
- Cortex: Outer, contains glomeruli; extends as medullary rays into medulla
- Medulla: 8-12 pyramids; each pyramid has base (cortical side) and papilla (apex pointing to minor calyx); papillae open into minor calyces → major calyces → renal pelvis → ureter
- Hilum: Entry/exit for renal vessels, ureter, nerves; hilum of right faces forward (IVC); left faces forward (aorta)

**Blood supply:**
- Renal artery (from aorta at L1-L2) → segmental arteries → interlobar → arcuate → interlobular → afferent arterioles → glomerulus → efferent arteriole → peritubular capillaries → interlobular → arcuate → renal vein → IVC
- Each kidney has dual blood supply: Large volume low-pressure portal system (renal artery) + small volume high-pressure venous drainage

**Ureter:**
- 25cm long; has three constrictions: UPJ (where renal pelvis narrows to ureter), pelvic brim (crosses bifurcation of common iliac), UVJ (where ureter enters bladder trigone — posterolateral)
- Three layers: Mucosa, muscularis (circular + longitudinal), adventitia; no serosa
- Blood supply: Upper: Renal artery; Middle: Gonadal, aorta; Lower: Superior vesical (from umbilical) + uterine and vaginal arteries (in females)

**Suprarenal Glands:**
- Retroperitoneal; right triangular (pyramidal) on right kidney; left crescentic on left kidney
- Cortex (from mesoderm): 3 zones — zona glomerulosa (mineralocorticoids — aldosterone), zona fasciculata (glucocorticoids — cortisol), zona reticularis (androgens)
- Medulla (from ectoderm): Chromaffin cells; secretes adrenaline and noradrenaline; innervated by sympathetic preganglionic fibres (splanchnic nerve)
- Blood supply: Superior suprarenal (from inferior phrenic), middle suprarenal (from aorta), inferior suprarenal (from renal artery)

**Pelvis and Pelvic viscera:**

#### Pelvic Walls
- Pelvic brim divides pelvis into true (below brim) and false (above brim) pelvis
- Bones: Two hip bones (ilium, ischium, pubis), sacrum, coccyx; form bony pelvis with sacroiliac, pubic symphysis, sacrococcygeal joints
- Pelvic floor: Pelvic diaphragm — levator ani (pubococcygeus, iliococcygeus, puborectalis) + coccygeus; supports pelvic organs; has urogenital hiatus (urethra in both, vagina in females, rectum)
- Obturator internus muscle + membrane → forms obturator canal (carries obturator vessels and nerve); used in nerve block for hip surgery

#### Male Pelvic Organs

**Urinary Bladder:**
- Extraperitoneal; stores urine; capacity ~500mL; trigone (smooth triangle between ureteric orifices and internal urethral orifice) — common site of infection
- Relations (male): Superior (peritoneum and intestines); posterior (rectum and seminal vesicles); neck (prostate)
- Blood supply: Superior vesical (from umbilical), inferior vesical (from internal iliac), middle rectal (from internal iliac); venous drainage to internal iliac
- Musculature: Detrusor muscle (circular, involuntary) + internal urethral sphincter (involuntary, sympathetic) + external urethral sphincter (voluntary, pudendal nerve S2-S4)

**Prostate:**
- In pelvis: Below bladder; surrounds prostatic urethra; 4 lobes (anterior — no glandular tissue, lateral, median, posterior); base sits on urogenital diaphragm
- Contains ejaculatory ducts (from seminal vesicles and vas deferens) which open into prostatic urethra at verumontanum
- Blood supply: Prostatic arterial branches from internal pudendal and inferior gluteal; venous plexus (of Santorini) drains to internal iliac
- **BPH:** Causes urinary frequency, nocturia, hesitancy, weak stream; arises from transitional zone (periurethral); PSA elevation; treated with 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (finasteride), alpha blockers (tamsulosin), surgery (TURP)
- **Prostate cancer:** Arises from peripheral zone (70%); PSA, digital rectal examination (DRE), transrectal ultrasound (TRUS), biopsy; spreads to bone (osteoblastic secondaries)

**Seminal Vesicles:** Lie above prostate; produce 70% of seminal fluid; contain fructose; ducts join vas deferens to form ejaculatory duct; not present in males who have had vasectomy

**Testes:**
- Develop in abdomen, descend into scrotum via inguinal canal (guided by gubernaculum); covered by tunica vaginalis (processus vaginalis — peritoneum that closes)
- Blood supply: Testicular artery (from aorta), cremasteric artery (from inferior epigastric), artery to vas deferens (from superior vesical)
- Venous drainage: Pampiniform plexus → testicular vein; right → IVC; left → left renal vein (angle of left testicular vein to left renal vein makes it prone to reflux → varicocele)

#### Female Pelvic Organs

**Uterus:**
- Retroverted and anteflexed (normal); supported by broad ligament, round ligament (runs through inguinal canal), cardinal ligament, uterosacral ligaments
- Parts: Fundus (above cornua), body, isthmus, cervix (partially intraperitoneal)
- Layers: Perimetrium (serosa), myometrium (thick smooth muscle — contracts during labour), endometrium (functional and basal layers — menstruates if no implantation)
- Blood supply: Uterine artery (from internal iliac) → arcuate arteries → radial arteries → spiral arteries (functional layer — respond to oestrogen/progesterone)
- Lymphatics: Fundus → para-aortic; body → external iliac; cervix → internal iliac, external iliac, sacral
- **Hysterectomy:** Types — total (cervix removed), subtotal (cervix left), radical ( parametrium removed, ovaries preserved if possible)

**Ovaries:**
- Intraperitoneal; attached to uterus by ovarian ligament, to pelvic wall by suspensory ligament (contains ovarian vessels)
- Blood supply: Ovarian artery (from aorta at L2); ovarian vein → IVC (right) and left renal vein (left)
- **Polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS):** Hirsutism, oligomenorrhoea, obesity, insulin resistance, unruptured follicles on ultrasound
- **Ovarian cysts:** Follicular, corpus luteum, chocolate (endometriotic), dermoid (mature teratoma)

**Uterosacral ligaments:** Contain autonomic nerves — important in spread of cervical cancer

**Vagina:** 8cm; connections: Cervix above, vestibular glands laterally; fornices (anterior, posterior, lateral); blood supply from vaginal artery (from internal iliac), uterine, middle rectal; innervation from pudendal nerve (S2-S4) — lower 2/3 only; upper 1/3 is intraperitoneal (spans pelvic). Contains vaginal flora (Lactobacillus — produces lactic acid → pH 4); no glands — mucus from cervical glands

**Urethra:**
- Female: 4cm short; straight; opening between clitoris and vagina; prone to UTIs; sphincter at bladder neck
- Male: 20cm; prostatic (3cm, widest), membranous (1cm, narrowest, through urogenital diaphragm), penile/spongy (15cm)
