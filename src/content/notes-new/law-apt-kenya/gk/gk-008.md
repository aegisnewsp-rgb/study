# Topic 8: Notable Kenyan Legal Cases and Landmark Statutes

## 🟢 Quick Review

Kenya's jurisprudence has been shaped by landmark cases addressing constitutional rights, criminal law, land disputes, and governance. Equally important are the foundational statutes that form the backbone of Kenya's legal system. This topic covers the most significant cases and statutes that every Kenya law student must know.

---

## ⚖️ Landmark Kenyan Cases

### 1. Koitalel v Samoei [1900] (Court of Inquiry)

**Citation:** Koitalel v Samoei (1900) (Colonial Court)

**Facts:** This case arose during British colonial rule in Kenya. Koitalel, a Nandi chief (Orkoiyot), led resistance against British forces in the early 1900s. The British attempted to arrest Koitalel, claiming he had breached customary law of war by attacking a British party. Koitalel was killed during the encounter.

**Issue:** The key legal issue was whether customary international law (laws of war) applied to the conduct of a local chief and whether the British colonial authority had jurisdiction.

**Significance:** This is one of the earliest recorded cases on the intersection of customary law and colonial law in Kenya. It established that colonial courts had authority over disputes involving local rulers and customary law of warfare. It is frequently cited in discussions of sovereignty, customary law, and the limits of colonial authority.

**Modern Relevance:** Cited in modern cases on customary law of war, sovereignty of indigenous communities, and the historical basis of British authority in Kenya.

---

### 2. GM v Criminal Reviews and Appeals Board [2013]

**Citation:** GM v Criminal Reviews and Appeals Board & Another [2013]

**Issue:** The applicant challenged the composition and procedures of the Criminal Reviews and Appeals Board (CRAB), which had refused to review their conviction. The case raised issues of constitutional rights, fair trial, and access to justice.

**Significance:** The Court held that the right to a fair hearing under Article 50 of the Constitution was engaged and that administrative bodies exercising quasi-judicial functions must comply with principles of natural justice — *audi alteram partem* (right to be heard) and *nemo judex in causa sua* (no one can be judge in their own cause).

---

### 3. Re Mumo [2013]

**Citation:** In the Matter of the Application by Benjah Omingal Mumo [2013]

**Facts:** This constitutional reference involved the retirement and replacement of judges following the implementation of the Constitution of Kenya 2010. Questions arose about whether retired judges could continue to serve and whether the appointment process complied with Chapter 9 of the Constitution.

**Issue:** Judicial independence, security of tenure, and compliance with constitutional provisions on the appointment of judges.

**Significance:** The Court reaffirmed the principle of judicial independence and held that any appointment process that compromises judicial independence is unconstitutional. It emphasized that Chapter 9 of the Constitution establishes a clear framework for judicial appointments and that the **Judicial Service Commission (JSC)** must be properly constituted and independent.

---

### 4. Trusted Society of Alliance v Chief Justice [2012]

**Citation:** Trusted Society of Alliance v The Chief Justice and Another [2012]

**Issue:** A public interest petition challenging the integrity of the judicial appointment process for the Chief Justice, alleging that the JSC had not followed proper constitutional procedures in recommending candidates.

**Significance:** The Court addressed the **removal of judicial officers** under Article 168 of the Constitution — holding that the removal process must be procedurally fair, transparent, and free from executive interference. This case reinforced the constitutional framework for judicial accountability and independence.

---

### 5. Muru Kunguru and 5 Others v Republic [2016]

**Citation:** Muru Kunguru and 5 Others v Republic [2016]

**Facts:** The applicants were sentenced to death following conviction for murder. They challenged the constitutionality of the death penalty provisions under the Constitution of Kenya 2010.

**Issue:** Whether the death penalty, as applied, was inconsistent with the Constitution's provisions on the right to life (Article 26), dignity (Article 28), and freedom from torture and degrading treatment (Article 29).

**Held:** The Court declared that the **mandatory death penalty** (imposed without judicial discretion to consider mitigating factors) was unconstitutional under Articles 26, 28, and 30 of the Constitution. The death penalty provisions in the Penal Code were struck down as inconsistent with the Constitution.

**Impact:** This decision effectively abolished the mandatory death penalty in Kenya and paved the way for resentencing of death row inmates. Courts now have discretion to consider mitigating circumstances before imposing sentence.

---

### 6. Raila Odinga v IEBC [2017]

**Citation:** Raila Odinga v Independent Electoral and Boundaries Commission [2017]

**Facts:** Following the 2017 presidential election, Raila Odinga challenged the declaration of Uhuru Kenyatta as President. The petition alleged that the IEBC had not properly verified the results before declaring them, and that there were irregularities in the tallying process.

**Issues:**
- Whether the IEBC's declaration of results was lawful
- Whether the irregularities were substantial enough to affect the outcome
- Whether the Supreme Court had jurisdiction to nullify a presidential election

**Held (4-2 majority):** The Supreme Court, in a landmark decision, nullified the election result, holding that the IEBC had failed to verify the results as required by law. The Court ordered a fresh presidential election within 60 days.

**Significance:** This was the **first presidential election ever nullified by a court in Africa**. It established important principles on electoral accountability, the duty of electoral commissions to verify results, and the power of the courts to nullify elections. It led to constitutional amendments (17th Amendment) and significant reforms to the IEBC.

## 📜 Landmark Kenyan Statutes

### 1. Constitution of Kenya 2010

The supreme law of the land. It is the source of all legislative authority and fundamental rights. It established devolution (Chapter 11), the Bill of Rights (Chapter 4), and a new court structure (Chapter 10). All other laws derive their authority from the Constitution and must be consistent with it.

### 2. The Judicature Act 2011

This Act (which replaced the old Judicature Act, Cap 8) establishes the jurisdiction of the courts and governs the application of law in Kenya. Section 3 is particularly important — it provides that the courts shall be guided by common law and equity, African customary law, and Islamic law in appropriate cases, subject to the Constitution and written law.

### 3. The Advocates Act (Cap 16)

The principal statute governing the legal profession in Kenya. It provides for:
- Admission to the bar (Section 10)
- Registration of advocates
- Annual practicing certificates
- Discipline and striking off

### 4. The Penal Code (Cap 63)

Kenya's principal criminal statute, originally based on the Indian Penal Code 1860 (itself derived from the common law tradition). It defines offences against persons (homicide, assault, sexual offences), property offences (theft, robbery, burglary), and public order.

Key provisions:
- **Section 203** — Definition of murder
- **Section 204** — Culpable homicide
- **Section 240** — Theft
- **Section 275** — Obtaining by false pretence
- Sexual offences provisions were substantially updated by the **Sexual Offences Act 2006**

### 5. The Criminal Procedure Code (Cap 75)

Governs criminal procedure from investigation through to trial and appeal. Key provisions:
- Powers of arrest (Sections 27–35)
- Bail and recognizance (Sections 81–125)
- Trial procedure in the subordinate courts and High Court
- Appeals procedure

### 6. The Evidence Act (Cap 80)

Governs the admissibility of evidence in Kenyan courts. Based on the Evidence Act of 1872 (Indian origin). Key principles:
- Burden of proof (Section 108 — on the prosecution in criminal cases)
- Admissibility of confessions (Section 25 — must be voluntary)
- Opinion evidence (Section 47)
- Competence and compellability of witnesses

### 7. The Land Control Act (Cap 282)

Governs the transfer and dealing in land, particularly agricultural land. It restricts transactions in land in designated Land Control Areas and requires the consent of the Land Control Board for any transaction.

### 8. The Marriage Act 2014

Unified the law on marriage in Kenya, recognizing:
- Civil marriages (under the Act)
- Christian marriages
- Hindu marriages
- Islamic marriages (nikah)
- Customary marriages (for purposes of registration)

### 9. The Sexual Offences Act 2006

Comprehensive legislation addressing sexual offences in Kenya, including:
- Rape (Section 3) — insertion of penis into vagina without consent
- Attempted rape
- Indecent assault
- Sexual offences against children (defilement)
- Provisions on protection of victims

### 10. The Prevention of Torture Act 2017

Gave effect to the UN Convention Against Torture (CAT). Criminalized torture, made it non-bailable, and established remedies for victims of torture by state officers.

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. **Koitalel v Samoei** [1900] — the earliest landmark case on customary law, sovereignty, and colonial authority in Kenya
2. **Muru Kunguru v Republic** [2016] — mandatory death penalty declared unconstitutional; courts must now consider mitigating factors
3. **Raila Odinga v IEBC** [2017] — first presidential election nullified in Africa; established electoral accountability of the IEBC
4. **Trusted Society v Chief Justice** [2012] — judicial removal must be procedurally fair; JSC must be independent
5. **Re Mumo** [2013] — judicial independence under Chapter 9 of the Constitution
6. **Judicature Act 2011 Section 3** — the pluralistic foundation: common law + equity + customary law + Islamic law (all subject to Constitution)
7. **The Constitution of Kenya 2010** is supreme — all other statutes must conform
8. **Sexual Offences Act 2006** replaced and updated the rape provisions of the Penal Code
9. **The Evidence Act (Cap 80)** — burden of proof on prosecution in criminal cases; confessions must be voluntary
10. **The Marriage Act 2014** — unified Kenya's fragmented marriage law across all faiths and traditions
