# Respiration and Circulation

## Respiration

Respiration is the process by which living organisms release energy from food (glucose). It involves the breakdown of organic compounds (primarily glucose) in the presence of oxygen to release energy, carbon dioxide, and water.

**Aerobic Respiration** (complete oxidation in presence of O₂):
> C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Energy (ATP)
> Energy released: 38 ATP per glucose molecule

**Anaerobic Respiration** (incomplete oxidation in absence of O₂):

**In plants and yeast:**
> C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂ + Energy (2 ATP)
> (Alcoholic fermentation)

**In muscles (during heavy exercise when O₂ supply is insufficient):**
> C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₃H₆O₃ (Lactic acid) + Energy

**Important CTET facts:**
- Anaerobic respiration produces much less ATP (2 ATP) compared to aerobic respiration (38 ATP)
- Lactic acid accumulation in muscles causes **muscle fatigue**
- Alcoholic fermentation by yeast is used in **brewing** and **bread-making**

### Human Respiratory System

The respiratory system consists of:

**Nasal cavity → Pharynx → Larynx (voice box) → Trachea (windpipe) → Bronchi → Bronchioles → Alveoli (air sacs)**

**Key features:**
- **Alveoli:** Thin-walled, balloon-like structures (~300 million in human lungs) providing large surface area for gas exchange. Walls are just one cell thick and surrounded by a dense network of blood capillaries.
- **Diaphragm:** Dome-shaped muscular partition separating thorax from abdomen; its contraction and relaxation facilitates breathing.
- **Pleural membranes:** Surround and protect the lungs.

**Mechanism of Breathing:**

| Phase | Action | Diaphragm | Rib cage |
|-------|--------|-----------|----------|
| **Inhalation (inspiration)** | Air enters lungs | Contracts and flattens | Lifts upward and outward |
| **Exhalation (expiration)** | Air leaves lungs | Relaxes and domes upward | Lowers downward and inward |

**Lung volumes:**
- **Tidal Volume (~500 mL):** Normal breathing
- **Vital Capacity (~4000 mL):** Maximum forceful exhalation after maximum inhalation
- **Residual Volume (~1500 mL):** Air remaining after maximum exhalation

**Exchange of gases:** O₂ diffuses from alveoli into blood capillaries (into RBCs combining with haemoglobin to form **oxyhaemoglobin**). CO₂ diffuses from blood capillaries into alveoli to be exhaled.

### Respiratory Disorders

- **Asthma:** Chronic inflammation of airways causing wheezing and breathlessness (triggered by allergens, pollution)
- **Bronchitis:** Inflammation of bronchial tubes
- **Pneumonia:** Infection causing inflammation in alveoli (filled with fluid/pus)
- **Tuberculosis (TB):** Caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* affecting lungs

## Circulation

### Blood and Blood Components

Blood consists of:

| Component | Description | Function |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Plasma (55%)** | Pale yellow fluid; 90% water | Transport nutrients, hormones, waste products; maintains pH |
| **RBCs (Erythrocytes)** | Biconcave, no nucleus; contain haemoglobin | O₂ transport (oxyhaemoglobin); ~5 million/mm³ |
| **WBCs (Leukocytes)** | Larger than RBCs; with nucleus | Immune defence (phagocytosis, antibody production) |
| **Platelets (Thrombocytes)** | Small, non-nucleated fragments | Blood clotting (release thromboplastin → fibrinogen → fibrin) |

**Clotting mechanism:** When blood vessels are injured, platelets release thromboplastin → converts prothrombin (inactive) to **thrombin** (active) → thrombin converts fibrinogen (soluble) to **fibrin** (insoluble mesh) → clot formation.

### Blood Groups

**ABO Blood Group System:**

| Blood Group | Antigens on RBCs | Antibodies in Plasma | Can Donate To | Can Receive From |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|---------------|------------------|
| **A** | A antigen | Anti-B antibody | A, AB | A, O |
| **B** | B antigen | Anti-A antibody | B, AB | B, O |
| **AB** | A and B antigens | No antibodies (universal recipient) | AB only | A, B, AB, O |
| **O** | No antigens | Anti-A and Anti-B antibodies (universal donor) | A, B, AB, O | O only |

**Rh Factor:**
- **Rh+ (Rh positive):** Rh antigen present on RBCs (≈85% Indians)
- **Rh− (Rh negative):** Rh antigen absent
- **Compatibility:** Rh− mother should not carry Rh+ baby (causes **haemolytic disease of newborn** in subsequent pregnancies due to anti-Rh antibodies produced in mother's blood)

### Double Circulation

In mammals, blood passes through the heart **twice** in one complete circuit — hence the term **double circulation**:

1. **Pulmonary circulation:** Right ventricle → Pulmonary artery → Lungs → Pulmonary vein → Left atrium
2. **Systemic circulation:** Left ventricle → Aorta → Body tissues → Vena cava → Right atrium

**Significance:** Double circulation ensures efficient oxygenation of blood. In the lungs, blood picks up O₂ and releases CO₂. The oxygenated blood is then pumped to all body parts. This separation of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood allows more efficient oxygen delivery to tissues.

**Comparison with fish (single circulation):** Fish have a single circuit — heart → gills → body → heart. Blood is oxygenated only at gills, making it less efficient for active animals.

### Heart Structure and Function

- **Four chambers:** Right atrium, Right ventricle, Left atrium, Left ventricle
- **Pace of heart:** **Sino-atrial node (SA node)** in the right atrium acts as the **pacemaker**, setting the rhythm (~72 beats/minute)
- **Cardiac cycle:** Alternating contraction (**systole**) and relaxation (**diastole**)

**Heart beat stages:** Atrial contraction → Ventricular contraction (pumping blood out) → Complete rest (brief) → Next cycle begins.

⚡ **CTET Exam Focus:** Double circulation is a frequently asked concept. Students must remember that oxygenated and deoxygenated blood remains separate in mammals and birds, while single circulation exists in fish.
