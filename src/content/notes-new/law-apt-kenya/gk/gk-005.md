# Topic 5: Human Rights in Kenya — International Instruments and Enforcement Mechanisms

## 🟢 Quick Review

Human rights law is a cornerstone of Kenya's constitutional framework. Kenya has ratified key international human rights instruments including the ICCPR and ACHPR, and has incorporated human rights protections into the Constitution of Kenya 2010. Understanding the content of these instruments and the enforcement mechanisms available is essential for examination.

---

## 🌍 International Human Rights Instruments

### 1. International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) — 1966

Kenya ratified the ICCPR in 1972. The ICCPR is one of the two foundational UN human rights covenants (along with ICESCR).

**Key Rights Under ICCPR:**
- Right to self-determination (Article 1)
- Freedom from arbitrary deprivation of life (Article 6)
- Freedom from torture and cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment (Article 7)
- Freedom from slavery and forced labour (Article 8)
- Liberty and security of person (Article 9)
- Rights of persons deprived of liberty (Article 10)
- Freedom of movement (Article 12)
- Expulsion of aliens (Article 13)
- Equality before courts and fair trial (Article 14)
- Freedom of thought, conscience, and religion (Article 18)
- Freedom of opinion and expression (Article 19)
- Prohibition of propaganda for war and incitement to discrimination (Article 20)
- Right to privacy (Article 17)
- Protection of minority rights (Article 27)

**Optional Protocols:**
- First Optional Protocol (1966): Individual complaints mechanism (Kenya has NOT ratified)
- Second Optional Protocol (1989): Abolition of death penalty (Kenya has NOT ratified)

### 2. African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights (ACHPR) — 1981

Kenya ratified the ACHPR in 1992. The ACHPR is Africa's primary human rights instrument, administered by the African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights (sitting in Banjul, The Gambia).

**Unique Features of ACHPR:**
- **Peoples' rights** — Distinct from individual rights; includes rights of peoples to existence, self-determination, development, and a satisfactory environment
- **Duties** — Article 27 imposes duties on individuals toward their family, community, and the state (a unique feature not found in Western human rights instruments)
- **No formal list of reservations** — States parties may not ratify with reservations (unlike ICCPR which permits certain reservations)

**Key Rights Under ACHPR:**
- Right to recognition as a person before the law (Article 3)
- Right to equality before the law (Article 3)
- Right to life and integrity (Article 4)
- Freedom from torture and degrading treatment (Article 5)
- Right to personal liberty (Article 6)
- Right to a fair trial (Article 7)
- Freedom from exploitation and degradation (Article 28)

**Protocol on the Rights of Women (Maputo Protocol, 2003):**
Kenya signed in 2004 and ratified in 2010. Key provisions include:
- Elimination of discrimination against women
- Right to dignity
- Right to life
- Protection from harmful practices (e.g., FGM)
- Right to peace in the family
- The Maputo Protocol has been incorporated into Kenyan law through various statutes (e.g., the Prohibition of Female Genital Mutilation Act 2011)

**Protocol on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (2006):** Kenya ratified in 2008.

### 3. The UN Charter and Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR) — 1948

While the UDHR is not a treaty and is not binding as such, it is:
- Recognized as **customary international law** and **general principles of law** (sources of international law under Article 38(1) of the ICJ Statute)
- Influences constitutional interpretation in Kenya — courts may refer to international instruments when interpreting rights under Article 21(2) of the Constitution

### 4. Convention Against Torture (CAT) — 1984

Kenya ratified in 1997. The CAT prohibits torture in all circumstances. Kenya incorporated CAT through the **Prevention of Torture Act 2017** which criminalized torture and made it non-bailable.

## 📜 Kenya's Bill of Rights and Human Rights Framework

### Constitutional Recognition

**Article 21** — State obligations:
- All State organs and public officers must observe, respect, and protect human rights
- The State must promote human rights through legislation, policies, and programmes
- The State is obligated to fulfill progressively the socio-economic rights

**Article 22** — Enforcement:
- Any person may petition a court to enforce the Bill of Rights
- The petition may be filed in the High Court (original jurisdiction under Article 165(3)(d)) or directly in the Supreme Court on constitutional matters
- Courts may grant appropriate relief including declarations, orders, or compensation

### The Kenya National Human Rights Institution (KNHRI)

**Article 59** establishes the Kenya National Human Rights Commission (KNHRC), which:
- Investigates alleged human rights violations
- Reports on human rights compliance
- Reviews legislation for consistency with human rights standards
- Operates independently from government
- Has quasi-judicial powers to recommend remedies

**The Commission on Administrative Justice (CAJ)** — Also known as the Office of the Ombudsman — addresses maladministration and administrative injustice by state organs.

## ⚖️ Enforcement Mechanisms

### 1. Judicial Enforcement

**High Court (Article 165(3)(d)):**
The High Court has original jurisdiction to hear questions of whether a fundamental right has been violated. This includes:
- **Compensation** (monetary remedy)
- **Declarations** (court declares a law or action unconstitutional)
- **Injunctions** (stop ongoing violations)
- **Orders of mandamus** (compelling the State to act)

**Supreme Court:**
On constitutional matters, the Supreme Court may hear and determine human rights cases, especially matters of great public importance or fundamental constitutional significance.

### 2. The African Commission and Court on Human and Peoples' Rights

**African Commission (Banjul, The Gambia):**
- Receives state reports (Kenya submits periodic reports)
- Inter-state complaints (one state vs. another)
- Individual communications (complaints by individuals or NGOs against states)

**African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights (Arusha, Tanzania):**
- A judgment of the African Court is final and binding on states
- Kenya has accepted the **individual complaints jurisdiction** — this means individuals in Kenya can directly petition the African Court (only states that have made a declaration accepting this jurisdiction can be petitioned)

### 3. United Nations Human Rights Treaty Bodies

Kenya is state party to:
- Human Rights Committee (ICCPR monitoring) — receives state reports; individual complaints for ICCPR where Kenya has ratified Optional Protocol
- Committee Against Torture (CAT monitoring)
- Committee on the Elimination of Racial Discrimination (CERD)
- Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW)
- Committee on the Rights of the Child (CRC)

Each treaty body receives **periodic reports** from Kenya and issues **Concluding Observations** with recommendations.

### 4. National Campaign and Advocacy

NGOs such as:
- **Kenya National Commission on Human Rights (KNHRI)** — monitoring and reporting
- **Kenya Human Rights Commission (KHRC)** — strategic litigation
- **International Commission of Jurists Kenya (ICJ Kenya)** — legal reform and advocacy

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. **ICCPR** (ratified 1972) vs **ACHPR** (ratified 1992) — know which instruments Kenya has ratified and when
2. **ACHPR** is unique in recognizing *peoples' rights* (not just individual rights) AND in imposing *duties* on individuals (Article 27)
3. The **Maputo Protocol** (Women's Rights) — Kenya ratified in 2010; incorporated into Kenyan law through statutes
4. **Article 22** of the Constitution gives *any person* the right to petition for enforcement of fundamental rights
5. The **Kenya National Human Rights Commission (KNHRC)** is established under Article 59 — it is a constitutional body
6. **Enforcement hierarchy:** Individual (court petition under Art. 22) → High Court (Art. 165(3)(d)) → Supreme Court (constitutional matters) → African Commission → African Court (individual complaints jurisdiction accepted by Kenya) → UN Treaty Bodies
7. Kenya has **NOT ratified** the First Optional Protocol to ICCPR (individual complaints) — this means Kenyan individuals cannot directly petition the UN Human Rights Committee against Kenya
8. *Muru Kunguru and 5 Others v Republic* [2016] was a landmark case where the Court declared the death penalty unconstitutional under Articles 26, 28, and 30 of the Constitution
