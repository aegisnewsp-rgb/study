# Topic 7: Current Affairs & Governance in Tanzania — CCM, Elections, and Local Government

## 🟢 Quick Review

Tanzania is effectively a **dominant-party state** under **Chama Cha Mapinduzi (CCM)** — the Party of the Revolution, which has governed since independence in 1961. Understanding the political structure, electoral system, local government framework, and the legacy of **Ujamaa** (African socialism) is essential for understanding Tanzania's governance context.

---

## 🏛️ Chama Cha Mapinduzi (CCM) — The Revolutionary Party

### Historical Background

**Chama Cha Mapinduzi** (CCM — "Party of the Revolution") was formed in 1977 through the merger of:
- **TANU** (Tanganyika African National Union — the party that led independence, led by Julius Nyerere)
- **AFRO** (Umma Party — Zanzibar's party)

**Julius Nyerere** (Mwalimu — "Teacher") was Tanzania's first President (1964–1985) and the architect of **Ujamaa African Socialism**. Under Nyerere, Tanzania pursued a policy of:
- **Ujamaa** (familyhood/community) — African socialism based on collective ownership, self-reliance, and communal values
- **Villagization** — relocating rural populations into organized villages for better service delivery and social organization
- **State-led development** — the government played a dominant role in the economy

**Post-Nyerere Era:** Tanzania moved toward **economic liberalization** in the 1980s and 1990s under President Ali Hassan Mwinyi (1985–1995) and Frederick Sumaye (1995–2005). However, CCM remained dominant.

### CCM's Dominance

Tanzania operates as a **de facto one-party state** — while opposition parties exist (CUF — Civic United Front, CHADEMA — Chadema, and others), CCM has won every presidential and parliamentary election since 1995. Key reasons include:
- **Political legacy** — Nyerere's popularity created deep CCM loyalty
- **State resources** — CCM benefits from access to state resources and media
- **Electoral framework** — some aspects of the electoral laws have been criticized for disadvantaging opposition parties

### The Second Multi-Party Election (1995)

Tanzania held its first multi-party elections in 1995 after constitutional reforms introducing multi-party politics. CCM won the presidency and a large majority in the National Assembly. This marked the formal end of the single-party system (though CCM had effectively been in power continuously).

## 🗳️ Elections in Tanzania

### Presidential Election

**Electoral System:** First-past-the-post (simple majority) in a **single national constituency** — the candidate with the most votes wins the entire presidency.

**Term:** 5 years. Maximum of **two terms** (2015 constitutional amendment introduced term limits for the presidency after President Magufuli).

**Qualifications:** Same as those for Union President (Article 34): Tanzanian citizen by birth, 40+ years, member of a political party.

**Election Date:** Held on the **last Sunday of October** every 5 years.

### Parliamentary Election (National Assembly)

The National Assembly has approximately **324 seats**:
- **239 constituency seats** — directly elected
- **Women representatives** — 30% quota (women elected from each region)
- **Nominated members** — appointed by the President
- **Ex-officio members** — Chief Secretary, Attorney General

**Qualifications for MPs:** Must be a Tanzanian citizen, 21+ years, and able to speak and write in Kiswahili (or English with competence).

### Zanzibar Elections

Zanzibar holds **separate elections** for:
- **President of Zanzibar** (Mwenyekiti)
- **House of Representatives (Baraza la Wawakilishi)** — approximately 85 seats

Zanzibar elections operate under the same first-past-the-post system. The ** CCM vs. CUF** contest in Zanzibar has been particularly competitive — CUF (Civic United Front) is strong in Pemba and has disputed several Zanzibar election results.

### Electoral Commission

The **National Electoral Commission (NEC)** oversees presidential and parliamentary elections. At the regional and district level, **Regional and District Electoral Commissions** manage the process.

**Key Functions:**
- Voter registration
- Nomination of candidates
- Conducting the election
- Counting and tallying results
- Announcing results

**Criticism:** Tanzania's electoral system has faced criticism for:
- alleged bias in the NEC toward CCM
- restrictions on opposition party activities
- challenges in accessing state media for opposition parties

## 🏡 Local Government in Tanzania

### Constitutional Basis (Articles 159–163, Fourth Schedule)

Tanzania has a **three-tier local government system**:
1. **Regional Level** — Regional Administration (Regional Commissioner)
2. **District Level** — District Councils
3. **Village/Ward Level** — Village Councils and Township Authorities

### District Councils

**District Councils** are the primary units of local government. They are responsible for:
- Local planning and development
- Primary education (management of primary schools)
- Local health services (dispensaries, health centres)
- Local road maintenance
- Agricultural extension services
- Social welfare

**Composition:** Councilors elected from wards, plus appointed officials.

### Village Councils

Under the **Local Government (Village Councils) Act**, each registered village has a **Village Council** (Baraza la Vijiji) elected for a 5-year term.

**Village Council Responsibilities:**
- Managing village land (under the Village Land Act)
- Local development planning
- Registering births, deaths, marriages (non-Union)
- Managing local schools
- Managing community water sources

**Village Assemblies:** The Village Assembly is the broadest local democratic body — all adult residents of the village are members. It approves the Village Council's plans and budgets.

### Ward Development Committees

Between the village and district levels, **Ward Development Committees** coordinate local development activities and channel resources to villages.

## 🇹🇿 Ujamaa — The Heritage of African Socialism

### Origins

**Ujamaa** (Swahili for "familyhood" or "brotherhood") was Nyerere's vision of African socialism, articulated in his 1967 Arusha Declaration. The core principles were:
- Every person is a member of a family/community and has obligations to that family
- Land belongs to the community (not private individuals)
- Development should be collective, not based on individual accumulation
- Self-reliance — Tanzania should not depend on foreign aid

### Villagization (Ujamaa Villages)

Between 1973 and 1976, Nyerere's government implemented **villagization** — relocating rural populations into newly established Ujamaa villages. This was intended to:
- Concentrate populations for easier service delivery (health, education)
- Promote collective agriculture
- Enable government planning and administration

**Criticisms of Villagization:**
- Forced relocation without adequate consultation
- Loss of traditional settlement patterns and agricultural practices
- Some resistance in certain communities

### Legacy of Ujamaa Today

While Tanzania has liberalized economically since the 1980s, Ujamaa's **communal values** influence governance and land policy:
- The **Village Land Act** (1999) reflects the Ujamaa principle that village land belongs to the community
- Collective village governance structures (Village Councils) derive from the Ujamaa village concept
- Political rhetoric often invokes Nyerere's legacy and Ujamaa values even by parties that support economic liberalization
