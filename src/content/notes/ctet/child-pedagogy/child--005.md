# Intelligence and Multiple Intelligences

## What is Intelligence?

Intelligence is the ability to learn, understand, reason, and adapt to new situations. However, different psychologists have proposed very different models of intelligence — CTET questions frequently test these competing theories.

## Early Models

### Charles Spearman (1904) — General Intelligence (g Factor)
Spearman observed that people who performed well on one cognitive task tended to perform well on others. He proposed a single underlying general intelligence factor (**g**) that influences performance across all mental tasks. A smaller **specific intelligence (s)** factor accounts for performance in particular areas.

> g factor: Innate general cognitive ability | s factor: Specific to particular tasks

### Louis Thurstone (1938) — Primary Mental Abilities
Thurstone challenged Spearman, identifying seven independent primary mental abilities:
1. Verbal comprehension (V)
2. Numerical ability (N)
3. Spatial visualization (S)
4. Memory (M)
5. Perceptual speed (P)
6. Reasoning (R)
7. Word fluency (W)

> Instead of one "g", Thurstone proposed multiple independent abilities — intelligence is multidimensional.

### J.P. Guilford (1967) — Structure of Intellect (SOI)
Guilford proposed the most complex early model: intelligence has three dimensions (operations, content, products) yielding up to 150 different abilities. This model influenced later work on multiple intelligences.

## Howard Gardner's Multiple Intelligences (1983)

Gardner's theory, which is most relevant for CTET, proposes **8 independent intelligences** that are relatively independent of each other. No single "g" factor — each intelligence operates on its own pathway.

### The 8 Intelligences

| # | Intelligence | Core capacity | Career examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Linguistic | Sensitivity to sounds, rhythms, meanings of words | Writer, poet, lawyer, teacher |
| 2 | Logical-Mathematical | Ability to handle long chains of reasoning, numbers | Mathematician, scientist, accountant |
| 3 | Spatial | Ability to perceive visual-spatial world accurately | Architect, artist, surgeon, navigator |
| 4 | Musical | Sensitivity to rhythm, pitch, melody, timbre | Musician, composer |
| 5 | Bodily-Kinesthetic | Skill in controlling one's own body movements | Athlete, dancer, actor, craftsman |
| 6 | Interpersonal | Ability to understand and respond to other people's moods, intentions | Teacher, counselor, politician, HR |
| 7 | Intrapersonal | Self-awareness, understanding one's own feelings, motivations | Philosopher, psychologist |
| 8 | Naturalistic | Ability to recognize and categorize plants, animals, natural phenomena | Botanist, farmer, ecologist |

> **For CTET**: The naturalist intelligence was added later (1999). Remember all 8 and their key features.

### Implications for Teaching

- Each student has a dominant intelligence profile
- A "weak" intelligence doesn't mean "stupid" — different kinds of strengths
- Teachers should use multiple entry points: verbal explanations alone won't reach spatial or kinesthetic learners
- Assessment should use diverse methods, not just written tests

**Classroom strategy**: If you have an interpersonal learner, use group work. If you have a bodily-kinesthetic learner, incorporate movement and hands-on activities. Don't force everyone into the same learning box.

## Emotional Intelligence (Goleman, 1995)

EI is the ability to perceive, understand, manage, and use emotions effectively. It includes:

1. **Self-awareness**: Recognizing your own emotions
2. **Self-management**: Controlling impulsive feelings
3. **Social awareness**: Empathy for others
4. **Relationship management**: Handling interpersonal interactions

> **For CTET**: EI is not one of Gardner's 8 intelligences — it's a separate construct. Some questions test both.

## Intelligence Quotient (IQ)

IQ = (Mental Age ÷ Chronological Age) × 100

- Average IQ = 100
- Gifted: IQ > 130
- Intellectually disabled: IQ < 70

**Limitations of IQ**:
- Does not measure creativity
- Does not measure emotional or social intelligence
- Can be influenced by education and environment
- Cultural bias in test items
