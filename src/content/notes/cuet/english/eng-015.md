# Essay Writing

### 🟢 Lite — Quick Review (1h–1d)
> Rapid summary for last-minute revision.

**Essay Writing** — Key Facts for CUET
• Use the PEEL method: Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link—keeps paragraphs structured and focused
• Each paragraph should contain one main idea supporting your thesis statement
• Connect paragraphs with transition words: "However," "Furthermore," "In contrast," "Consequently"
• Avoid first-person pronouns (I, me, my) in formal essays—maintain an objective tone
• Keep vocabulary precise—use domain-specific terms appropriately rather than overly complex words
• Time allocation: spend 2 minutes brainstorming, 5 minutes outlining, 10 minutes writing, 3 minutes reviewing
⚡ Exam tip: CUET essays are scored on content relevance, organization, vocabulary, and grammar—focus on clarity over complexity

**Essay Writing** — CUET English Study Guide

Essay writing in CUET evaluates your ability to present ideas coherently under time pressure. Begin by analyzing the prompt carefully—identify directive words like "discuss," "analyze," "explain," or "evaluate." Your thesis statement should directly address the question and appear in the introduction. Structure your essay with an introduction (2-3 sentences), body paragraphs (2-3 paragraphs), and a conclusion (2-3 sentences). Each body paragraph must support your thesis with a clear topic sentence, evidence or examples, and explanation of how the evidence supports your point.

Vocabulary building is essential—read newspaper editorials and academic articles to absorb sophisticated language patterns. However, avoid using words whose meanings you are uncertain about; misplaced vocabulary can weaken your argument. Grammar accuracy matters—ensure subject-verb agreement, proper article usage, and consistent tense throughout. CUET examiners look for logical flow, so use transitional phrases to guide readers between ideas. Common errors include going off-topic, exceeding word limits, and neglecting to address all parts of a multi-part question.

Example 1: Prompt: "Technology has improved education." Write an essay. Key approach: Take a clear position, support with specific examples (online platforms, interactive whiteboards), conclude with broader implications.

Example 2: Prompt: "Discuss the impact of social media on youth." Include both positive effects (connectivity, awareness) and negative effects (addiction, cyberbullying) before stating your informed opinion.

**Essay Writing** — Comprehensive CUET English Notes

Advanced essay writing demands nuanced argumentation and sophisticated language control. Move beyond simple PEEL by incorporating counterarguments and rebuttals to demonstrate critical thinking. For instance, after presenting a point, acknowledge potential objections before explaining why your perspective holds stronger merit. This technique showcases depth of analysis and aligns with higher scoring criteria.

Grammar mastery at this level includes proper use of subjunctive constructions ("It is imperative that he submit the report"), passive voice for objectivity, and conditional sentences for hypothetical scenarios. Vocabulary should reflect academic register—words like "paradigm," "dichotomy," "synergy," and "implications" demonstrate sophistication when used accurately. However, avoid lexical overkill; clarity remains paramount.

Cross-topic preparation strengthens essay skills. Reading comprehension exposes you to structured arguments and advanced vocabulary in context. Grammar exercises reinforce correct usage under pressure. General knowledge preparation provides ammunition for evidence and examples. CUET Advanced essays may present abstract prompts requiring you to synthesize multiple perspectives—such as "Evaluate the tension between tradition and modernity in contemporary society." Practice timed writing to build stamina and refine your ability to produce well-organized essays within 20 minutes.
