# Topic 5: Criminal Law in Tanzania — Penal Code Provisions and Procedure

## 🟢 Quick Review

Tanzania's criminal law is primarily codified in the **Penal Code (Cap 16, Laws of Tanzania)** — a statute of general application across the mainland. The Criminal Procedure Code (Cap 20) governs procedure. Understanding the elements of key offences (homicide, theft, sexual offences), criminal procedure from arrest to trial, and the role of courts in criminal matters is essential for any law student.

---

## 📜 The Penal Code (Cap 16)

### Overview

The Penal Code (Cap 16) is Tanzania's principal criminal statute, organized into sections covering:
- Part I — General provisions (definitions, interpretation)
- Part II — Offences against public order
- Part III — Offences against the person (including homicide, assault, sexual offences)
- Part IV — Offences against property (theft, robbery, burglary, criminal destruction)
- Part V — Defamation, currency offences, and miscellaneous offences

The Penal Code was originally based on the **Indian Penal Code 1860** (like Kenya's Penal Code), but has been substantially amended.

### Homicide — The Key Offences

#### Culpable Homicide (Section 195)

A person commits culpable homicide who causes death:
- By an unlawful act or omission
- By negligence or omission of duty
- By causing bodily injury that leads to death

**Punishment:** Culpable homicide not amounting to murder is punishable with **imprisonment for life** or a lesser term.

#### Murder (Section 196)

A person commits murder who commits culpable homicide with **malice aforethought**, which is defined as:
- The intention to cause death
- The intention to cause serious bodily injury
- Knowledge that the act is likely to cause death (reckless indifference)

**Aggravated Murder:** Murder committed in the course of certain serious crimes (e.g., robbery with violence, sexual offences) may attract enhanced penalties or mandatory sentencing.

**Death Penalty:** Murder remains a **capital offence** in Tanzania (unlike Kenya where it has been effectively abolished by *Muru Kunguru*). The death penalty is prescribed for aggravated murder, treason, and certain terrorism-related offences.

**Note on Infanticide (Section 197):** A mother who causes the death of her newborn child under the influence of disturbed mind caused by childbirth or lactation may be convicted of infanticide (a lesser offence than murder).

## 📦 Key Property Offences

### Theft (Section 265)

A person steals who dishonestly:
- Takes movable property belonging to another
- With intent to permanently deprive the owner

**Key Elements:**
- **Actus reus:** Taking property (caption and asportation)
- **Mens rea:** Dishonesty + intent to permanently deprive

**Punishment:** Theft is punishable with imprisonment for **7 years** (simple theft) or **10 years** (aggravated theft, e.g., theft by servant, theft of firearms, or theft from a building).

### Robbery (Section 285)

Robbery is theft with **violence or putting in fear** — the offender uses force or threatens force to steal. Key aggravated form: **armed robbery** (using firearms or offensive weapons) carries enhanced penalties.

**Note:** Robbery with violence is a serious offence potentially punishable by **life imprisonment**.

### Burglary and Housebreaking (Sections 294–299)

- **Burglary:** Breaking and entering a building at night with intent to commit an indictable offence
- **Housebreaking:** Entering a building with intent to commit an indictable offence
- **Housebreaking with intent to steal:** Entering a building and stealing or attempting to steal

### Criminal Damage (Section 315)

Destruction or damage to property belonging to another — punishable upon conviction. This includes arson (destroying property by fire) which carries aggravated penalties.

## 🚨 Sexual Offences

The Penal Code contains provisions on sexual offences, though Tanzania has also enacted the **Sexual Offences (Prohibition) Act 1998** (as amended) which substantially updated the law on sexual offences.

### Key Sexual Offences

**Rape (Section 130A of the Penal Code as amended by the Sexual Offences Prohibition Act):**
- Sexual intercourse without consent
- Against the order of nature (anal intercourse)
- With a child (defilement)
- By a person in authority against their subordinate

**Key Elements:**
- **Penile-vaginal penetration** without consent (rape)
- **Other forms of penetration** may constitute sexual assault
- The offence applies to both male and female victims

**Defilement (Sexual Offences Prohibition Act):**
- Sexual intercourse with a child (under 18 years — the age of consent is 18 in Tanzania)
- Strict liability offence — no defence of reasonable belief as to age (in some contexts)

**Sexual Assault:** Any sexual act without consent, including indecent assault.

**Note on Marital Rape:** In Tanzania, marital rape is not specifically criminalized under the Penal Code (unlike some jurisdictions). This has been the subject of advocacy for law reform.

## ⚖️ Criminal Procedure (Criminal Procedure Code Cap 20)

### Investigation

**Arrest:** A police officer may arrest without a warrant if reasonably suspecting the person has committed an offence (Section 24 CPC). An arrest must be:
- Based on reasonable suspicion
- Effected with minimum force necessary
- Followed by informing the arrested person of the reason for arrest

**Custody and Investigation:** The police may hold a suspect in custody for investigation — maximum periods vary. Detention beyond 24 hours without bringing the suspect before a court requires justification.

**Right to Legal Representation:** Every arrested person has the right to contact a lawyer of their choice before making any statement.

### First Appearance Before Court

**Section 33–36 CPC:** An arrested person must be **brought before a court** within **24 hours** of arrest (excluding public holidays). If not charged within this period, the person must be released or further detained with judicial authorization.

### Bail and Remand

**Bail:** The CPC provides for bail at various stages:
- **Police bail** — at the police station for minor offences (Section 36)
- **Court bail** — at the first court appearance
- **Refusal of bail** — for serious offences (e.g., murder, treason), the court may deny bail pending trial

**Recognizance:** A surety (guarantor) may be required to pledge money or property as security for the accused's attendance at trial.

### Trial Procedure

**Criminal trial in Tanzania follows an adversarial model** — prosecution presents evidence, accused responds.

1. **Charge reading** — accused is informed of the charge
2. **Plea** — accused pleads guilty or not guilty
3. **Prosecution case** — witnesses testify, evidence is led
4. **Defence case** — accused may testify or remain silent, may call witnesses
5. **Closing arguments**
6. **Judgment** — verdict (guilty or not guilty)
7. **Sentencing** — if guilty

### Sentencing

**Available Sentences:**
- **Death penalty** — for murder (aggravated), treason, terrorism
- **Imprisonment** — for various terms; life imprisonment for serious offences
- **Fine** — monetary penalty
- **Probation** — suspended sentence conditional on good behaviour
- **Community service** — in some courts for minor offences

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. **Penal Code Cap 16** — Tanzania's primary criminal statute, organized by offences against persons, property, and public order
2. **Culpable homicide (Section 195)** — unlawful killing without malice aforethought; distinguishes from murder
3. **Murder (Section 196)** — requires **malice aforethought** (intent to kill or knowledge that death is likely)
4. **The death penalty** remains lawful in Tanzania (unlike Kenya) for aggravated murder and treason
5. **Theft (Section 265)** — dishonest taking of movable property with intent to permanently deprive; 7 years imprisonment
6. **Robbery (Section 285)** — theft with violence; life imprisonment for armed robbery
7. **Sexual Offences Prohibition Act 1998** — updated rape and defilement law; age of consent is **18 years**
8. **Criminal Procedure Code (Cap 20)** — governs procedure from arrest to trial
9. **Right to bail** — police bail for minor offences; court may refuse bail for serious offences (murder, treason)
10. Accused must be **brought before court within 24 hours** of arrest (excluding public holidays)
11. Trial is **adversarial** — prosecution leads evidence, accused responds; burden of proof is on the prosecution (beyond reasonable doubt)
