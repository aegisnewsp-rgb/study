# Topic 7: International Law Basics — Treaty Law, Vienna Convention, and Sources of International Law

## 🟢 Quick Review

International law governs relations between states and international organizations. Kenya is a significant participant in international law — it has ratified numerous treaties and is bound by customary international law. The Constitution of Kenya 2010 (Articles 2(5) and (6)) expressly incorporates international law into Kenya's legal system. Understanding treaty law, the Vienna Convention, and the sources of international law is foundational for any law student.

---

## 🌍 Sources of International Law (Article 38, ICJ Statute)

The International Court of Justice (ICJ) Statute, Article 38(1), lists the recognized sources of international law — courts and tribunals apply these in resolving disputes.

### 1. International Conventions (Treaties)

Treaties are written agreements between states (or between states and international organizations) that create binding legal obligations. They are the **primary source** of international law.

In Kenya, treaties form part of the law of the land under **Article 2(6) of the Constitution** upon ratification. Once ratified, a treaty is incorporated without further domestic legislation (though courts have sometimes required enabling legislation for the treaty to be directly enforceable in domestic courts).

### 2. Customary International Law

Customary international law (CIL) arises from **consistent state practice** accompanied by **opinio juris** (the belief that the practice is legally obligatory, not merely a matter of courtesy or convenience).

**Characteristics of CIL:**
- Arises from widespread and consistent state practice over time
- Requires *opinio juris* — states must practice it because they believe they are legally required to
- Binding on all states, including those who did not participate in creating the custom (except for **persistent objectors** who consistently objected during formation)
- Can be codified in treaties (e.g., the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS) codified customary law on maritime zones)

**Examples of CIL rules:**
- Freedom of the high seas
- Sovereign immunity of foreign heads of state
- Prohibition on the use of force except in self-defence
- Rule against *ex post facto* criminalization
- Diplomatic immunity (Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations 1961)

### 3. General Principles of Law Recognized by Civilized Nations

These are principles common to major legal systems (both common law and civil law) that are applied internationally. Examples include:
- **Principle of good faith** (*pacta sunt servanda* — treaties must be performed in good faith)
- **Principle of natural justice** — audi alteram partem (right to be heard), *nemo judex in causa sua* (no one can be judge in their own cause)
- **Principle against unjust enrichment**
- **Doctrines of res judicata** and **lis pendens**

### 4. Judicial Decisions and Scholarly Opinion (Subsidiary Means)

- **Judicial decisions** of international tribunals (e.g., ICJ, ICC, ITLOS) are *subsidiary* sources — they do not create binding precedent but are highly persuasive
- **Decisions of the Permanent Court of International Justice (PCIJ)** — the ICJ's predecessor (ceased in 1946)
- **Scholarly writings** — highly respected treatises and academic opinions may be cited to assist courts in determining what international law requires

### 5. Jus Cogens (Peremptory Norms)

**Jus cogens** (Latin: "compelling law") refers to norms from which no derogation is permitted. These are **absolute and non-derogable** — no treaty, custom, or state practice can override them.

**Recognized jus cogens norms include:**
- Prohibition on genocide
- Prohibition on slavery and slave trade
- Prohibition on torture and cruel treatment
- Prohibition on aggression
- Right to self-determination of peoples
- Rules of international humanitarian law (IHL) in armed conflict

**Consequences of Jus Cogens:** Any treaty or state action contrary to jus cogens is **void** to the extent of the conflict. States cannot claim sovereignty or state immunity to avoid jus cogens violations (e.g., the ICJ in the *Jurisdictional Immunities of States* case Germany v Italy).

## 📜 Treaty Law and the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (1969)

### The Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (VCLT)

The VCLT is the foundational treaty on international treaty law. Kenya is a signatory (signature in 1969) and ratified it in **1972**. The VCLT is widely regarded as **codifying customary international law** on treaties.

### Key VCLT Provisions

**Article 2** — Definition: A treaty is "an international agreement concluded between States in written form and governed by international law."

**Article 6** — Every state has the capacity to conclude treaties.

**Article 7** — Full Powers: Only persons with "full powers" (authorization by the state) may negotiate, sign, or authenticate a treaty.

**Article 8** — A person who participated in the conclusion of a treaty without authorization cannot have their act invalidated, but they must have had authority.

**Article 11** — Consent to be bound: A state may express consent through:
- Signature
- Exchange of instruments
- Ratification
- Acceptance, approval, or accession (where a state signs a treaty after it has entered into force)

**Article 12** — Consent expressed by signature (where the treaty provides, or the parties agree, that signature shall constitute consent)

**Article 14** — Consent expressed by ratification. After signature, the state must ratify the treaty through its domestic constitutional process (in Kenya, ratification requires parliamentary approval under Article 218 of the Constitution — the Treaty Making and Ratification Act 2012 governs this).

**Article 18** — Obligation not to defeat the object and purpose of a treaty prior to entry into force:
> "A State is obliged to refrain from acts which would defeat the object and purpose of a treaty when: (a) it has signed the treaty... subject to ratification... or (b) it has expressed its consent to be bound by the treaty."

**Article 19** — Reservations (with exceptions for jus cogens):
- A state may make a reservation when signing, ratifying, accepting, or acceding to a treaty
- The reservation must not be prohibited by the treaty, nor be incompatible with the object's and purpose of the treaty
- A state that objects to a reservation is not considered a party unless the treaty indicates otherwise

**Article 26** — *Pacta sunt servanda*: Every treaty in force is binding on the parties and must be performed in good faith.

**Article 27** — Internal law and treaty compliance: A party may not invoke internal law as justification for failure to comply with a treaty.

**Article 31** — General Rule of Interpretation:
- Treaties must be interpreted **in good faith** in accordance with the **ordinary meaning** of the terms
- In **context** (including preamble and annexes)
- In light of **object and purpose**
- **Supplementary means** (travaux préparatoires — preparatory works) may be used where interpretation under Article 31 leaves meaning ambiguous

**Article 32** — Supplementary means of interpretation (travaux préparatoires, circumstances of conclusion)

**Article 33** — Interpretation of treaties authenticated in two or more languages: The different language versions are equally authoritative, but in case of discrepancy, the meaning that best reconciles the texts is adopted.

**Article 53** — Jus cogens: A treaty is void if, at the time of its conclusion, it conflicts with a **peremptory norm of general international law** (jus cogens).

**Article 60** — Material breach: A material breach of a bilateral treaty may give the injured party the right to suspend or terminate the treaty. A material breach of a multilateral treaty may authorize suspension or exclusion of the breaching party.

**Article 62** — Fundamental change of circumstances (rebus sic stantibus): This doctrine allows termination or suspension of a treaty if circumstances have fundamentally changed, BUT this does not apply to: treaties establishing a boundary, or where the change was caused by a breach.

## ⚖️ Kenya's Treaty-Making Process

### Constitutional Framework

**Article 2(6) of the Constitution:** "Any treaty or convention ratified by Kenya shall form part of the law of Kenya under this Constitution."

**Article 218** and the **Treaty Making and Ratification Act 2012** set out the process:
1. The Cabinet Secretary (Foreign Affairs) may negotiate and sign treaties
2. Treaties must be laid before Parliament for **approval** before ratification
3. Upon Parliamentary approval, the President (or designated Cabinet Secretary) ratifies the treaty
4. The treaty then becomes part of Kenyan law

**Case Example:** Kenya's ratification of the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court (2002) — Parliament approved and Kenya ratified. Kenya later signed (but did not ratify) the Malabo Protocol for the African Criminal Court (though this has not been operationalized).

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. **Article 38(1) ICJ Statute** — four sources: treaties, custom, general principles, judicial decisions and scholarly writings. Subsidiary: judicial decisions and writings.
2. **Vienna Convention 1969** — Kenya ratified in 1972; codified customary treaty law
3. **Treaty making process in Kenya:** Negotiation → Signature → Parliamentary approval → Ratification → Treaty becomes law under Article 2(6)
4. **Pacta sunt servanda** (Article 26 VCLT) — treaties bind in good faith
5. **Article 27 VCLT** — no internal law can justify non-compliance with a treaty (a state cannot invoke its own constitution to avoid treaty obligations)
6. **Jus cogens** — peremptory norms (no derogation permitted): prohibition on genocide, torture, slavery, aggression
7. **Customary international law** — requires state practice + opinio juris; binding on all states (except persistent objectors)
8. **Article 2(5) and (6) of Kenya's Constitution** — international law is incorporated: treaties upon ratification; customary international law directly applicable
9. *Obligations erga omnes* — obligations owed to the international community as a whole (e.g., prohibition of genocide, right to self-determination)
10. **Treaty interpretation** — use Article 31 VCLT ordinary meaning + context + object and purpose; supplementary means (travaux) under Article 32 only when ambiguous
