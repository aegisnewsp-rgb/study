# Fill in the Blanks

### 🟢 Lite — Quick Review (1h–1d)
> Rapid summary for last-minute revision before your exam.

Fill in the Blanks questions test your vocabulary, grammar, and contextual understanding. In MAT, single-blank sentences are most common, though double-blank questions occasionally appear. The key is to identify the contextual clue that narrows down the answer.

**Key strategies:**
- Always read the complete sentence before looking at options
- Identify the tone (positive/negative), register (formal/informal), and logical relationship (cause-effect, contrast, addition)
- Eliminate options that don't fit grammatically or contextually
- Two-step elimination is faster than finding the "right" answer

**High-frequency patterns:**
- Contrast markers: but, however, although, yet, nevertheless, nonetheless
- Addition markers: and, also, moreover, furthermore, in addition
- Cause-effect: therefore, hence, consequently, thus, as a result

**Exam tip:** If you're unsure between two options, choose the one that is more precise and less vague.

#### Types of Fill in the Blank Questions

**1. Vocabulary-Based Fill in the Blanks:**
These test whether you know the precise meaning of words. The blank requires a specific word that conveys a particular shade of meaning.

Example: "The company's decision to _______ the merger was met with mixed reactions from stakeholders."
Options: (A) proceed with (B) abandon (C) defer (D) revisit

The word "met with mixed reactions" signals caution — so defer or revisit fits better than proceed with or abandon.

**Vocabulary strategies:**
- Learn words in context, not in isolation — know the company a word keeps
- Watch for prefixes and suffixes: benevolent (well-meaning), malevolent (ill-meaning); amateur (Latin amare = to love), professional (expertise)
- Connotations matter: "cheap" vs "economical" vs "frugal" — all relate to cost but carry different evaluative tones
- Watch for "academic" vocabulary in formal passages — words like "cogent," "salient," "tenuous," "robust"

**2. Grammar-Based Fill in the Blanks:**
These test subject-verb agreement, verb tenses, articles, prepositions, and modifiers.

Example: "Neither the CEO nor the board members _______ aware of the impending restructuring."
Options: (A) was (B) were

"With neither...nor," the verb agrees with the nearer subject — "members" is plural, so "were" is correct.

**Common grammar traps:**
- Subject-verb agreement with collective nouns (jury is/are; team is/are depending on meaning)
- Tense consistency within a sentence
- Parallel structure in comparisons
- Modifiers placed near wrong nouns

**3. Contextual/Logical Fill in the Blanks:**
The blank is determined by the logical flow of the sentence — cause and effect, contrast, or sequence.

Example: "The passage described the artist's work as revolutionary, _______ that it fundamentally altered the trajectory of modern art."
Options: (A) implying (B) suggesting (C) disputing (D) celebrating

"Fundamentally altered" is positive change — implying or suggesting fit. "Disputing" and "celebrating" are too extreme or wrong register.

#### How to Approach Double-Blank Questions

Double-blank questions have two blanks in one sentence. The best strategy:

1. Identify which blank is easier to fill first
2. If one blank can be determined with certainty, the other becomes simpler
3. If options don't all have two valid words, eliminate early

Example: "The scientist's theory, while _______ in its conclusions, was _______ in its methodology."
Options:
- (A) sound — flawed
- (B) questionable — rigorous
- (C) controversial — innovative
- (D) unproven — speculative

The contrast between "while" clauses suggests opposite qualities. "Sound — flawed" and "questionable — rigorous" show contrast. Which fits better depends on context.

#### Common Words Confused in MAT

| Word | Meaning | Common Confusion |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| Affect (verb) | To influence | Effect (noun) = result |
| Complement | To complete | Compliment = praise |
| Principal (adj) | Main, chief | Principle = rule |
| Stationary | Not moving | Stationery = office supplies |
| Disinterested | Unbiased | Uninterested = not interested |
| Fewer | Countable things | Less = uncountable things |
|Than (comparisons) | Used after comparisons | Then = time/sequence |

#### Advanced Fill in the Blank Strategies

**Collocations — Word Partnerships:**

MAT often tests collocations — words that naturally go together in English. Knowing common collocations helps fill blanks without deep comprehension.

*Verb + noun collocations:*
- "Take a decision" (not "make a decision")
- "Pay attention" (not "give attention")
- "Reach a conclusion" (not "arrive at a conclusion" is also acceptable)

*Noun + preposition collocations:*
- "belief in," "trust in," "faith in"
- "compliance with," "adherence to," "conformity with"

*Adjective + preposition collocations:*
- "afraid of," "capable of," "conscious of"
- "similar to," "identical to," "equivalent to"

**Idiomatic Expressions:**

Some blanks require idiomatic phrases:

- "____ and ____": Both elements must be parallel (grammatically and conceptually)
  - "Null and void," " safe and sound," "pick and choose"
- "Either ____ or ____": Parallel structure
- "Neither ____ nor ____": Parallel structure

**Signal Words and Connectors:**

Recognising signal words instantly narrows the blank:

*Adversative (contrast):* but, however, yet, nevertheless, nonetheless, still, although, though, even so, on the contrary, instead, rather

*Additive (addition):* and, also, too, as well, besides, moreover, furthermore, in addition, additionally, equally, likewise, similarly

*Cause/result:* because, since, as, therefore, thus, hence, consequently, so, accordingly

*Condition:* if, unless, provided that, in case, on condition that

*Time:* when, before, after, while, during, until, as soon as, meanwhile

**Practice with Real MAT Examples:**

Q: "Despite the initially promising results, the experiment was ultimately deemed _______ due to methodological inconsistencies."
(A) inconclusive (B) futile (C) redundant (D) superficial

Answer: (A) inconclusive — "Despite" signals contrast with "promising," but the overall negative tone (methodological inconsistencies) supports "inconclusive" rather than stronger words like futile.

Q: "The economist argued that the proposed policy would _______ rather than _______ economic growth."
(A) hinder — stimulate (B) encourage — undermine (C) accelerate — retard (D) promote — hamper

Answer: (A) — "Rather than" signals contrast; economic policies typically aim to stimulate growth, so hindering contrasts with stimulating.

#### Word Root Analysis for Unfamiliar Words

When you encounter an unfamiliar word, use root analysis:

- **mal-** (bad): malice, malnourished, malfunction
- **bene-** (good): benevolent, benefactor, beneficial
- **-ist** (person who): scientist, artist, pianist
- **-tion** (process/state): education, nation, function
- **-able/-ible** (capable of): readable, audible, flexible
- **un-** (not): unhappy, unclear, untie
- **re-** (again/back): redo, return, review

#### MAT Fill in the Blanks — Quick Checklist

1. Read the full sentence first
2. Identify tone (positive/negative/neutral) from surrounding words
3. Look for signal words (but, and, because, although)
4. Check if grammar narrows options (subject-verb agreement, parallelism)
5. Check collocations — does the word pair naturally?
6. Eliminate clearly wrong options
7. Choose the most precise fit, not the vaguest
8. If stuck, try substituting each option into the sentence mentally
