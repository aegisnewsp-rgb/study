# Topic 8: Natural Resources and Environment

## Ecosystem — Biotic and Abiotic Components

An **ecosystem** is a functional unit of nature where **living organisms** interact with each other and with their **physical environment**.

### Biotic Components (Living)
| Category | Examples | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Producers (Autotrophs)** | Green plants, algae, some bacteria | Convert solar energy into food (photosynthesis) |
| **Consumers (Heterotrophs)** | Animals, fungi, non-green plants | Depend on producers for food |
| — Primary consumers (Herbivores) | Rabbit, deer, goat, cow | Eat producers directly |
| — Secondary consumers (Carnivores) | Snake, frog, fox | Eat herbivores |
| — Tertiary consumers (Top carnivores) | Lion, eagle | Eat other carnivores |
| **Decomposers (Saprotrophs)** | Bacteria, fungi, some insects | Break down dead organic matter → recycle nutrients |

### Abiotic Components (Non-living)
- **Physical factors:** Sunlight, temperature, water, soil, air, wind, light
- **Chemical factors:** pH, minerals, salts, dissolved gases (O₂, CO₂)
- **Geographical factors:** Altitude, pressure, topography

> **CTET Key:** Abiotic factors are the non-living components that influence living organisms. A change in abiotic factors affects the entire ecosystem.

### Types of Ecosystems
- **Terrestrial:** Forest, grassland, desert, tundra
- **Aquatic:** Freshwater (pond, lake, river) and Marine (ocean, sea)
- **Man-made:** Crop fields (agro-ecosystem), aquarium, garden

## Food Chain and Food Web

### Food Chain
A **linear sequence** of organisms where each organism is eaten by the next organism in the chain.

**Examples:**
```
Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle
          (Producer)  (1° consumer) (2° consumer) (3° consumer) (4° consumer)
```
```
Phytoplankton → Zooplankton → Small fish → Large fish → Eagle
     (Producer)    (1° consumer)  (2° consumer)  (3° consumer) (4° consumer)
```

### Trophic Levels
- **Trophic Level 1:** Producers (plants)
- **Trophic Level 2:** Primary consumers (herbivores)
- **Trophic Level 3:** Secondary consumers (small carnivores)
- **Trophic Level 4:** Tertiary consumers (top carnivores)

> **Energy Flow:** Only **~10%** of energy is passed from one trophic level to the next (90% is lost as heat). This is why food chains are usually **short** (3–5 levels) — not enough energy reaches higher levels to sustain large populations.

### Food Web
A **network of interconnected food chains** — more realistic and stable than a single food chain.
- If one organism in a food chain disappears, the web provides **alternative pathways**
- Greater stability in an ecosystem due to multiple food sources

### Ecological Pyramids
- **Pyramid of Numbers:** Number of organisms at each trophic level (typically decreases upward)
- **Pyramid of Energy:** Energy at each trophic level (always upright — always decreases upward)
- Note: Pyramid of biomass can be **inverted** (e.g., in a pond, phytoplankton biomass < zooplankton biomass at any given time)

## Nitrogen Cycle

The **nitrogen cycle** describes how nitrogen moves through the ecosystem.

### Steps of the Nitrogen Cycle

1. **Nitrogen Fixation:** Atmospheric N₂ is converted to **ammonia (NH₃)** or nitrates (NO₃⁻)
   - **Biological fixation:** Rhizobium bacteria in legume root nodules (most important natural method)
   - **Lightning:** High temperature converts N₂ → NOₓ → nitrates
   - **Industrial fixation:** Haber-Bosch process (manufacture of fertilisers)

2. **Nitrification:** Ammonia (NH₃) → Nitrites (NO₂⁻) → Nitrates (NO₃⁻)
   - Carried out by **nitrifying bacteria** (Nitrosomonas, Nitrobacter)
   - Nitrates are the form **plants absorb**

3. **Assimilation:** Plants absorb nitrates from soil → incorporate into proteins, DNA, chlorophyll

4. **Ammonification:** Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) break down dead organic matter → release **ammonia** back into soil

5. **Denitrification:** Nitrates → N₂ (atmospheric nitrogen)
   - Carried out by **denitrifying bacteria** (Pseudomonas, Thiobacillus) in anaerobic conditions
   - Returns nitrogen to the atmosphere

> **CTET Focus:** **Rhizobium** is the most commonly asked biological nitrogen fixer. It is found in **root nodules of leguminous plants** (pulses, beans, peas, groundnut).

## Carbon Cycle

The **carbon cycle** describes how carbon moves through the ecosystem.

### Carbon Pools and Pathways

1. **Photosynthesis:** CO₂ from atmosphere → converted to glucose (organic carbon) by producers
2. **Food chain:** Carbon passes from producers → consumers → decomposers
3. **Respiration:** All organisms release CO₂ back to atmosphere (cellular respiration: C₆H₁₂O₆ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + energy)
4. **Combustion:** Burning of fossil fuels (coal, petroleum, natural gas) and wood releases stored CO₂
5. **Decomposition:** Dead organisms → decomposers release CO₂
6. **Ocean:** Oceans dissolve and store large amounts of CO₂ (as carbonates); exchange with atmosphere

### Key Fact
- **Carbon is the backbone** of all organic molecules: carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, DNA
- The **greenhouse effect** is caused by increasing atmospheric CO₂ → global warming
- **Fossil fuels** took millions of years to form from decomposed organisms — they are **non-renewable**

## Water Cycle (Hydrological Cycle)

The continuous movement of water between the atmosphere, land, and oceans.

### Steps of the Water Cycle

1. **Evaporation:** Water from oceans, lakes, rivers evaporates → water vapour rises (powered by solar energy)
2. **Transpiration:** Plants lose water vapour through stomata (adds to atmospheric moisture)
3. **Condensation:** Water vapour cools at higher altitudes → forms tiny water droplets → clouds
4. **Precipitation:** When water droplets in clouds grow large enough → rain, snow, hail falls
5. **Runoff:** Water flows over land surfaces → joins rivers, lakes, oceans
6. **Infiltration/Groundwater:** Water seeps into soil → becomes groundwater → some emerges as springs and wells

> **CTET Fact:** About **97%** of Earth's water is in oceans (too salty for drinking); only **~3%** is freshwater (mostly locked in glaciers and ice caps).

## Environmental Issues

### Air Pollution
**Primary pollutants** (directly harmful):
- **Suspended Particulate Matter (SPM)** — dust, smoke, soot
- **Sulphur dioxide (SO₂)** — from burning fossil fuels; causes acid rain
- **Nitrogen oxides (NOₓ)** — from vehicle exhaust; causes smog
- **Carbon monoxide (CO)** — from incomplete combustion; binds haemoglobin, reduces oxygen-carrying capacity
- **Lead (Pb)** — from petrol additives; damages nervous system

**Secondary pollutants** (formed by chemical reactions):
- **Ozone (O₃)** — formed by NOₓ + VOCs in sunlight; causes eye irritation, respiratory problems

### Ozone Depletion
- **Ozone (O₃)** in the stratosphere forms the **ozone layer** (20–35 km above Earth)
- **Ozone layer absorbs UV radiation** — protects living organisms from harmful UV-B and UV-C rays
- **CFCs (Chlorofluorocarbons)** in refrigerators, ACs, aerosols break down ozone → O₃ → O₂ + O (free radical chain reaction)
- **One CFC molecule can destroy ~100,000 ozone molecules**
- **Ozone hole** (especially over Antarctica) → increased UV-B reaching Earth → skin cancer, cataracts, immune suppression, crop damage
- **Montreal Protocol (1987):** Global treaty to phase out CFCs

### Global Warming
- **Greenhouse gases** (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, CFCs, O₃) trap infrared radiation → warm Earth's surface
- **Enhanced greenhouse effect:** Increased greenhouse gases from human activities → more heat trapped → rising global temperatures
- **Consequences:**
  - Rising sea levels (thermal expansion + melting glaciers)
  - Climate change (more extreme weather — floods, droughts, hurricanes)
  - Loss of biodiversity (species migration, extinction)
  - Threat to agriculture (changing monsoon patterns)

### Acid Rain
- SO₂ and NOₓ from burning fossil fuels react with water vapour in atmosphere → **sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄) and nitric acid (HNO₃)**
- Falls as rain with pH < 5.6 (normal rain pH ~5.6 due to CO₂)
- **Effects:** Damages forests, acidifies lakes (kills aquatic life), corrodes buildings and monuments (Taj Mahal is particularly affected), leaches nutrients from soil

### Water Pollution
- **Sewage** → microbial contamination → water-borne diseases (cholera, typhoid, dysentery)
- **Industrial effluents** → heavy metals (mercury, lead), chemicals → bioaccumulation and biomagnification
- **Agricultural runoff** → fertilisers (nitrates, phosphates) → **eutrophication** (algal bloom → oxygen depletion → fish death)
- **Oil spills** → coat feathers of birds, gills of fish → death

### Bioaccumulation and Biomagnification
- **Bioaccumulation:** Accumulation of a toxin in an organism's body over time (e.g., mercury in fish)
- **Biomagnification:** Concentration of toxin increases at each trophic level as it moves up the food chain (e.g., DDT in food chain: water → plankton → small fish → large fish → birds → highest concentration in top carnivores)

## Biodiversity and Conservation

### What is Biodiversity?
- **Biodiversity** = variety of life at all levels (genes, species, ecosystems)
- **Three components:** Genetic diversity, Species diversity, Ecosystem diversity
- India is one of the **17 mega-diversity countries** in the world

### Threats to Biodiversity
1. **Habitat loss** (deforestation, urbanisation) — **greatest threat**
2. **Poaching and hunting** (illegal wildlife trade)
3. **Pollution** (air, water, soil)
4. **Climate change**
5. **Invasive species** (e.g., water hyacinth, Lantana camera)
6. **Overexploitation** (overfishing, overgrazing)

### Conservation Methods

**In-situ conservation** (conservation within natural habitat):
- **National Parks** (e.g., Kanha, Kaziranga)
- **Wildlife Sanctuaries**
- **Biosphere Reserves** (e.g., Sundarbans, Nilgiris)
- **Sacred Groves** (traditional community-protected forest patches)

**Ex-situ conservation** (conservation outside natural habitat):
- **Zoological parks** ( zoos)
- **Botanical gardens**
- **Seed banks** (Svalbard Global Seed Vault — Norway)
- **Tissue culture** (conserving rare plant species in labs)

### Important International Agreements
- **Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES):** Regulates trade in wildlife
- **Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD):** Commits nations to conserve biodiversity
- **Ramsar Convention:** Wetlands conservation

### ⚡ CTET Exam Tips

- **10% energy rule** in food chains — explains why food chains are short and why carnivores are fewer in number

- **CO₂** is the primary greenhouse gas responsible for global warming (most abundant)

- **Water hyacinth** is an **invasive species** that clogs water bodies and reduces oxygen — frequently mentioned in CTET environment questions
- **Kaziranga National Park** is famous for the **one-horned rhinoceros**; **Sundarbans** for the **Royal Bengal Tiger**
- **Biodiversity hotspot** — regions with very high species richness and high endemism (India has 4: Western Ghats, Eastern Himalayas, Sundarbans, Indo-Burma region)
- **Bioaccumulation vs Biomagnification:** Accumulation in one organism vs concentration increasing up the food chain
