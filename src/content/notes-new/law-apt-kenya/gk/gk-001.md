# Topic 1: Constitution of Kenya 2010 — Bill of Rights, Chapters 2–4

## 🟢 Quick Review

The Constitution of Kenya 2010 is the supreme law of the land. It replaced the repealed Constitution of Kenya (1963) and introduced transformative provisions including devolution, a Bill of Rights, and institutional reforms. Key areas tested include the Bill of Rights (Chapter 4), the sovereignty clause (Chapter 2), and the citizenship provisions (Chapter 3).

---

## 📐 Structure of the Constitution

The 2010 Constitution has 26 Chapters organized as follows:

- **Chapter 1** — Sovereign Republic (Articles 1–2)
- **Chapter 2** — Republic and Sovereignty of the People (Articles 3–4)
- **Chapter 3** — Citizenship (Articles 5–8)
- **Chapter 4** — Bill of Rights (Articles 9–59)
- **Chapters 5–8** — Land and Environment
- **Chapters 9–11** — Leadership and Integrity
- **Chapters 12–14** — Public Finance
- **Chapters 15–17** — Parliament
- **Chapters 18–22** — The Executive and Judiciary
- **Chapters 23–26** — Devolution

---

## 🔷 Chapter 2: Republic and Sovereignty of the People

Article 1 establishes that all sovereign power belongs to the people of Kenya and is exercised in accordance with the Constitution. This is foundational — no person, government, or institution may claim or exercise power contrary to the Constitution.

**Article 2** — Supremacy of the Constitution:
- The Constitution is the supreme law
- Any law inconsistent with it is void to the extent of the inconsistency
- Binding on all State organs, persons, and institutions

**Article 3** — Duty to respect and uphold the Constitution. Every person must respect, uphold, and defend the Constitution.

**Article 4** — Declaration of the Republic: Kenya is a sovereign Republic, democratic, multi-party, and founded on principles of human dignity, equity, social justice, and good governance.

---

## 🔷 Chapter 3: Citizenship (Articles 5–8)

Kenyan citizenship may be acquired by:
1. **Birth** — A person born in Kenya before 12 December 2013 if either parent was a citizen at the time. After 12 December 2013, birth alone is insufficient — at least one parent must be a citizen.
2. **Descent** — A person born outside Kenya where a parent was a citizen at the time of birth
3. **Registration** — For spouses of Kenyan citizens (after 3 years of marriage and continuous residence)
4. **Naturalization** — After residing in Kenya for at least 7 of the preceding 10 years, and meeting other requirements

Citizenship cannot be revoked arbitrarily — Article 17 prohibits deprivation of citizenship arbitrarily.

## 🔷 Chapter 4: The Bill of Rights (Articles 9–59)

This is the most examined chapter. It applies horizontally (between individuals) and vertically (against the State).

### Rights vs. Fundamental Freedoms (Article 20)

The Bill of Rights binds:
- All State organs and public officers at national and county levels
- All persons, including private individuals (horizontally)

Article 20(4) provides that when applying rights, courts must develop relevant jurisprudence to the maximum extent possible, guided by principles of human dignity, equity, non-discrimination, and democracy.

### Categories of Rights

**Civil and Political Rights (Articles 21–39):**
- Equality and freedom from discrimination (Art. 27)
- Life (Art. 26) — The right to life is inviolable. The death penalty was effectively abolished by *Muru Kunguru and 5 Others v Republic* [2016] — courts interpreted the death penalty provisions consistent with the Constitution's dignity framework
- Dignity (Art. 28) — Every person has inherent dignity; the State must observe, respect, and protect it
- Freedom and security of person (Art. 29) — No one shall be subjected to torture, cruel or degrading treatment
- Privacy (Art. 31) — Every person has the right to privacy, including protection from search, surveillance, and disclosure of information
- Freedom of conscience, religion, belief, and opinion (Art. 32)
- Freedom of expression (Art. 33) — Includes freedom of the press and media
- Assembly, demonstration, picketing, and petition (Art. 37)
- Movement and residence (Art. 39)

**Economic and Social Rights (Articles 40–55):**
- Right to property (Art. 40) — No person may be deprived of property except under the Land Control Act or other specific circumstances with compensation
- Right to accessible and adequate housing (Art. 43) — Includes reasonable standards of sanitation
- Right to accessible and adequate health care (Art. 43)
- Right to education (Art. 43)
- Food security (Art. 43)

**Environmental Rights (Article 42):**
Every person has the right to a clean and healthy environment, including the right to have the environment protected for present and future generations.

### Limitation of Rights (Article 24)

Rights may only be limited by law and only to the extent that the limitation is reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society. Courts consider:
- The nature of the right
- The importance and purpose of the limitation
- The nature and extent of the limitation
- The need to ensure the limitation does not negate the essential content of the right

## ⚖️ Key Case Law on Bill of Rights

- **Muru Kunguru and 5 Others v Republic [2016]** — Death penalty declared inconsistent with Articles 26, 28, and 30 of the Constitution
- **Re Mumo [2013]** — Court held that the retired judges matter (Chapter 16 appointments) engaged judicial independence provisions
- **Trusted Society of Alliance v Chief Justice [2012]** — Court addressed the removal of the Chief Justice and judicial independence
- **GM v CRB [2013]** — Right to be heard, audi alteram partem rule in constitutional context

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. The Constitution is supreme — any inconsistent law is void (*Republic v Kenya Revenue Authority ex parte Richa* and others)
2. The Bill of Rights applies both vertically and horizontally (Art. 20)
3. Socio-economic rights (*Ogola v UON* and similar) are justiciable
4. Limitations on rights must pass the Article 24 proportionality test
5. Remember the two key dates for citizenship acquisition: 12 December 2013 (the cut-off for citizenship by birth alone)
6. Articles 26 (life), 27 (equality), 28 (dignity), 31 (privacy), 32 (religion), 33 (expression), 37 (assembly) are the most commonly examined rights
