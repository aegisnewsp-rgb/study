# Topic 6: The Legal Profession in Kenya — Law Society of Kenya, Advocates, Admission, and Discipline

## 🟢 Quick Review

Kenya's legal profession is governed by the Advocates Act (Cap 16, Laws of Kenya) and regulated by the Law Society of Kenya (LSK). Understanding the path to becoming an advocate, the role of the LSK, admission requirements, and the disciplinary framework is essential for any law student preparing for the Kenya Law Aptitude examination.

---

## 🏛️ The Law Society of Kenya (LSK)

### History and Establishment

The Law Society of Kenya was established in 1949 under the Order of the Chief Justice. It is the **statutory body** that regulates the legal profession in Kenya, mandated under the **Advocates Act (Cap 16)**.

**Legal Basis:**
- The Advocates Act (Cap 16) empowers the Chief Justice to make rules for the admission, registration, and discipline of advocates
- The LSK is a membership organization — every advocate must be a member before they can practice law in Kenya

### Functions of the LSK

1. **Regulation of the Profession** — Setting standards for legal practice, admission requirements, and continuing professional development (CPD)
2. **Disciplinary Proceedings** — Receiving and investigating complaints against advocates; referring matters to the Disciplinary Tribunal
3. **Advocacy for the Rule of Law** — Making representations to government on legal reforms, filing *amicus curiae* briefs in significant constitutional cases
4. **Member Services** — Providing legal aid, training, and professional support to members
5. **P practicing Certificates** — Issuing annual practicing certificates; no advocate may practice without one (contravention is an offence under the Advocates Act)

### Structure

- **Council** — Elected body that runs the LSK (President, Vice President, Treasurer, Council Members)
- **Branches** — Nairobi, Coast, Western, Nyanza, Rift Valley, and others
- **Committees** — Practice, Ethics, CPD, Legal Aid

### The Advocates Act (Cap 16)

The Advocates Act is the principal legislation governing:
- Admission to the bar (Section 10)
- Registration as an advocate (Section 11)
- Requirements for annual practicing certificates
- Discipline and removal from the roll

## ⚖️ Admission to the Bar — Becoming an Advocate

### Path to Admission

**Step 1: Qualify with a Law Degree**
A person must hold a **Bachelor of Laws (LLB)** degree from a recognized university. Kenya's law schools include:
- University of Nairobi (UoN)
- Kenyatta University (KU)
- Moi University (MU)
- University of Dar es Salaam (for Tanzanian graduates — subject to recognition)
- Strathmore University
- Catholic University of Eastern Africa (CUEA)
- Other recognized institutions

**Step 2: Complete the Post-Law Bar Course**
After obtaining the LLB, a graduate must complete a **Post-Law Bar Course** (also called the Bar Intake Course) at the **Kenya Law School** (KLS), formerly the Council of Legal Education. This is a 12-month intensive programme covering:
- Legal ethics and professional responsibility
- Civil practice and procedure
- Criminal practice and procedure
- Conveyancing and land law
- Commercial law (company law, banking law)
- Constitutional and human rights law
- Trial advocacy

**Step 3: Pass the Bar Examinations**
Upon completing the Post-Law Bar Course, graduates must pass the Bar Examinations set by the Kenya Law School. The examinations are typically held twice a year.

**Step 4: Serve a period of Apprenticeship**
After passing the Bar Examinations, a graduate must serve a **pupilage** (apprenticeship) of at least **6 months** under a qualified advocate (a principal). During pupilage, the graduate (pupil) observes court proceedings, drafts legal documents, and assists in case preparation.

**Step 5: Admission to the Roll**
A person who has completed Steps 1–4 may apply to the **High Court** for admission as an advocate. The application is made by Motion, supported by an affidavit. Once admitted, the person's name is entered on the **Roll of Advocates** maintained by the High Court.

### Section 10 of the Advocates Act — Requirements for Admission

An applicant for admission must:
1. Be a citizen of Kenya (or such other nationality as permitted by law)
2. Be of good conduct
3. Hold a recognized LLB degree
4. Have completed the Post-Law Bar Course at KLS
5. Have passed the Bar Examinations
6. Have served 6 months pupilage under a qualified advocate
7. Be sponsored by two advocates

## 🎓 The Kenya Law School (KLS)

The Kenya Law School, established under the Kenya Law School Act 2011 (as amended), is the institution responsible for the **Bar Programme** and the **Continuing Professional Development (CPD)** requirements for advocates.

### Bar Programme Structure

| Phase | Duration | Content |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Academic Phase | ~9 months | Law substantive courses (legal ethics, civil procedure, criminal procedure, conveyancing, commercial law) |
| Examinations | — | Bar examinations set by the KLS Examinations Board |
| Pupilage | 6 months | Practical training under a qualified advocate |

### Continuing Professional Development (CPD)

 Advocates are required to undertake **CPD hours** annually to maintain their practicing certificates. The LSK mandates a minimum of CPD credit hours per year (typically 10-15 hours), covering areas such as legal updates, ethics, and specialized areas of law.

## 📜 Discipline and Professional Ethics

### The Disciplinary Tribunal

Under the Advocates Act, complaints against advocates are heard by the **Disciplinary Tribunal** (also known as the Advocates Disciplinary Tribunal). The Tribunal has the power to:
- Strike an advocate off the Roll (most serious sanction)
- Suspend an advocate for a specified period
- Censure an advocate (public reprimand)
- Fine an advocate
- Order payment of costs to the client

### Striking Off the Roll

A striking-off order means the person ceases to be an advocate and must re-apply for admission from scratch (including repeating pupilage). Grounds for striking off include:
- **Professional misconduct** — e.g., misappropriation of client funds, dishonest conduct, failure to act with diligence in a client's matter
- **Criminal conviction involving dishonesty** — e.g., fraud, theft
- **Breach of advocate-client privilege**
- **Inadequate professional services**

### The Statutory Demand and Client Account Rules

**Client Account:** Every advocate who receives money on behalf of a client must deposit it in a **client account** at a bank (separate from the advocate's own account). Misappropriation of client funds is a serious offence that commonly leads to striking off.

**The Advocate's Remuneration:** Governed by the Advocates Remuneration Order (legal scale fees) — advocates must comply with these or face sanctions for overcharging.

### Key Cases on Discipline

- *Republic v Disciplinary Tribunal ex parte Kuria* [2006] — The scope of the Tribunal's jurisdiction
- *Okwach v Kenya Law Society* — Adequacy of professional services
- *In re Advocates (Disciplinary Proceedings)* — Various cases on misappropriation

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. **Law Society of Kenya (LSK)** — established 1949; every advocate must be a member and hold an annual practicing certificate
2. **Advocates Act (Cap 16)** is the primary legislation — it governs admission, practice, and discipline
3. **Path to admission:** LLB → Post-Law Bar Course (KLS) → Bar Exams → 6 months Pupilage → Admission to High Court Roll
4. **Kenya Law School (KLS)** runs the Bar Programme and examinations (established 2011)
5. **Disciplinary Tribunal** — has power to strike off, suspend, or censure advocates
6. **Misappropriation of client funds** is the most common ground for striking off
7. **Continuing Professional Development (CPD)** — mandatory for annual practicing certificate renewal
8. The Chief Justice makes rules for admission under the Advocates Act, upon recommendation from the LSK and KLS
9. **Appeals** from the Disciplinary Tribunal go to the Court of Appeal
10. *Amesthetic* — any person who practices law without being on the Roll commits an offence (unauthorized practice of law)
