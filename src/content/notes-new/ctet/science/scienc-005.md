# Plant Life and Reproduction

## Plant Life: Structure and Function

### Flower Structure

A typical flower consists of four whorls (concentric rings) attached to the **receptacle** (swollen end of the flower stalk):

1. **Calyx (sepals):** Outermost whorl; usually green; protects the bud before opening
2. **Corolla (petals):** Inner to sepals; usually colorful and scented; attracts pollinators
3. **Androecium (stamens):** Male reproductive part
   - **Anther:** Terminal part producing pollen grains (pollen mother cells → pollen grains via meiosis)
   - **Filament:** Stalk supporting the anther
4. **Gynoecium (pistil/Carpel):** Female reproductive part
   - **Stigma:** Top sticky surface receiving pollen
   - **Style:** Elongated tube connecting stigma to ovary
   - **Ovary:** Swollen basal part containing **ovules** (each ovule contains one egg cell/oosphere)
   - **Placentation:** Arrangement of ovules inside ovary (marginal, axile, parietal, free central, basal)

**Types of flowers:**
- **Complete flower:** Has all four parts (e.g., hibiscus)
- **Incomplete flower:** Missing one or more parts (e.g., cucumber — missing corolla)
- **unisexual flower:** Has only stamens or only pistil (e.g., papaya, maize)
- **Bisexual/hermaphrodite flower:** Has both stamens and pistil (e.g., rose, mustard)

### Reproduction in Plants

#### Sexual Reproduction

**Pollination** is the transfer of pollen grains from the **anther** to the **stigma** of the same flower or another flower of the same species.

**Types of pollination:**

| Type | Agent | Example |
|------|-------|---------|
| **Self-pollination (Autogamy)** | Gravity, contact | Wheat, rice, oats |
| **Cross-pollination (Xenogamy)** | Wind, insects, birds, animals, water | Sunflower, mango, apple |

**Agents of pollination:**
- **Insects (entomophily):** Bees, butterflies; flowers are usually bright, scented, with nectar (e.g., sunflower, jasmine)
- **Wind (anemophily):** Light, powdery pollen; feathery stigmas (e.g., maize, wheat, grass)
- **Water (hydrophily):** Occurs in aquatic plants (e.g., Vallisneria, Hydrilla)
- **Birds (ornithophily):** Bright red or orange flowers (e.g., silk cotton, Bombax)
- **Bat (chiropterophily):** Night-blooming, strong-scented flowers

**Post-pollination events:**

1. **Pollen germination on stigma:** Pollen grain absorbs moisture, germinates to produce a **pollen tube**
2. **Pollen tube growth:** Tube grows down through the style, guided by chemical substances
3. **Double fertilization (unique to flowering plants):**
   - First fertilization: One male gamete + egg cell (oosphere) → **Zygote** (2n) → develops into **embryo**
   - Second fertilization: Other male gamete + two polar nuclei → **Primary endosperm nucleus (PEN)** (3n) → develops into **endosperm** (food for developing embryo)

4. **Post-fertilization changes:**
   - Ovary → **Fruit**
   - Ovule → **Seed**
   - Zygote → Embryo
   - Outer integuments → Seed coat

### Seed Structure and Germination

**Dicot seed (e.g., gram/pea):**
- **Seed coat:** Outer protective layer (derived from integuments)
- **Hilum:** Scar on seed coat marking point of attachment to funicle
- **Micropyle:** Small pore allowing water entry
- **Embryo:** Consists of **radicle** (embryonic root), **plumule** (embryonic shoot), and two **cotyledons** (food storage)

**Monocot seed (e.g., maize/wheat):**
- Single cotyledon (scutellum)
- Radical and plumule enclosed in a **coleoptile** and **coleorhiza** respectively
- **Endosperm:** Food stored outside the embryo (starchy)

**Seed Germination:**
- Requires **water, warmth, and oxygen** (not light — except in some photoblastic seeds)
- **Epigeal germination:** Cotyledons come out above soil (e.g., castor, onion)
- **Hypogeal germination:** Cotyledons remain below soil (e.g., maize, gram)
- **Vivipary:** Seed germinates inside the fruit while still attached to parent plant (e.g., mangrove)

### Asexual Reproduction in Plants

Asexual reproduction involves a single parent and produces offspring **genetically identical** to the parent (clones).

| Method | Description | Examples |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **Vegetative propagation** | New plants arise from vegetative parts (stem, root, leaf) | Potato (tuber), Rose (stem cutting), Bryophyllum (leaf), Banana (sucker) |
| **Tubers** | Modified underground stems for food storage | Potato (eyes = axillary buds) |
| **Runners/Stolons** | Horizontal stems that grow above ground and produce new plants | Strawberry, grass |
| **Bulbs** | Underground storage organs with fleshy leaves | Onion, garlic |
| **Rhizomes** | Underground modified stems | Ginger, turmeric |
| **Spore formation** | Spores are produced in sporangia; germinate to form new plant | Fern, moss, fungus |
| **Budding** | Small buds develop on parent body and detach | Yeast |

**Artificial propagation methods:**
- **Cuttings:** Stem cutting planted in soil (rose, sugarcane)
- **Layering:** Stem bent and covered with soil; roots form at the node (jasmine)
- **Grafting:** Upper part (scion) joined to lower part (stock) of different plant (fruit trees)
- **Tissue culture (Micropropagation):** Growing plants from single cells/explants on nutrient medium; produces many identical plants from a single parent (banana, orchid)

**CTET Important Points:**
- Grafting results in genetically identical plants but with different root systems
- Spore formation in non-flowering plants (moss, fern) is important to remember
- Double fertilization is a unique characteristic of **angiosperms** (flowering plants) and produces both embryo and endosperm

**Key difference between sexual and asexual reproduction:**

| Character | Sexual | Asexual |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Parents involved | Two | One |
| Gamete formation | Meiosis | Mitosis |
| Genetic variation | High (recombination) | None (clones) |
| Offspring | Not identical to parents | Identical to parent |
| Spores/Seeds | Seeds formed after fertilization | Specialized structures (tubers, buds) or spores |
| Examples | Most flowering plants | Potato, banana, yeast |
