# Topic 6: Contract Law in Uganda — Contracts Act, Sale of Goods Act, and Related Areas

## 🟢 Quick Review

Contract law in Uganda is primarily governed by the **Contracts Act (Cap 73)** and the **Sale of Goods Act (Cap 82)** — both received from British legislation and adapted for Uganda. Uganda does not have its own comprehensive modern contract statute — it relies on these colonial-era acts. Understanding offer and acceptance, consideration, capacity, discharge, and sale of goods is essential for examination.

---

## 📜 The Contracts Act (Cap 73)

### Overview and Historical Background

The **Contracts Act (Cap 73)** was originally the Indian Contracts Act 1872, received into Uganda through British colonial legislation. It applies to all contracts in Uganda (including Zanzibar-incorporated contracts) and is the **primary source of contract law**.

The Contracts Act covers:
- **Part I** — General principles of contract (offer, acceptance, consideration, capacity, consent)
- **Part II** — Specific types of contracts (sale of goods, indemnity, guarantee, bailment, agency)

### Key Definitions (Section 2)

- **Proposal (Offer):** When a person signifies his willingness to do or to abstain from doing something, with a view to obtaining the assent of another person, he is said to make a proposal
- **Promise:** When the person to whom the proposal is made signifies his assent, the proposal becomes a promise
- **Agreement:** An agreement is every promise and every set of promises forming consideration for each other
- **Contract:** An agreement enforceable by law is a contract

### Formation of a Contract

#### 1. Offer and Acceptance (Sections 3–9)

**Offer (Proposal):**
- Must be **communicated** to the offeree
- Must be **clear and definite** — vague or indefinite offers are invalid
- **Distinguished from invitation to treat** — advertisements, display of goods, price lists are invitations to treat, not offers (the leading case is *Fisher v Bell* [1961] — English case applied in Uganda)

**Acceptance:**
- Must be **communicated** — Section 4: acceptance must be communicated; silence is NOT acceptance
- Must be **absolute and unconditional** — a qualified acceptance is a counter-offer (Section 7)
- Must be made by the person to whom the offer was made
- Must be within the **validity period** of the offer

**Revocation of Offer (Section 6):**
An offer may be revoked before acceptance is communicated. Revocation is effective upon communication to the offeree. If no time is specified, the offer lapses after a **reasonable time**.

**Cross-Offers (Section 9):** If two parties make offers to each other at the same time, neither constitutes acceptance of the other — there is no contract.

#### 2. Consideration (Sections 2(d) and 10–23)

**Definition:** Consideration is something of value given by each party to the other as the price of the other's promise.

**Rules on Valid Consideration:**
- **Must move at the desire of the promisor** (Section 10) — consideration given voluntarily is not valid consideration
- **Must be real** — not illusory (a promise not to sue is valid; a promise not to commit a crime is not)
- **Must have some value** — nominal consideration suffices (but must not be unreal)
- **Past consideration is not good consideration** (Section 18) — past services already rendered before a promise to pay are not valid consideration
- **Consideration must be competent** — illegal or unlawful consideration makes the contract void

**Contracts Without Consideration:**
Under **Section 25**, a written promise made on natural love and affection between close family members is enforceable without consideration — but it must be in writing and signed.

#### 3. Capacity to Contract (Sections 10–12, 24–33)

**Minors (Section 10):** A person under 18 years of age (minor) is not competent to contract. Any contract entered into by a minor is **voidable at the minor's option**. The other party cannot enforce the contract against the minor.

**Key rule:** A minor's contract is **voidable, not void** — the minor may affirm or disaffirm upon attaining majority.

**Exception — Contracts for Necessaries:** Contracts for **necessaries of life** supplied to a minor or a person of unsound mind are enforceable for their value (Section 68 — this is a quasi-contractual remedy). Necessaries include food, clothing, shelter, and basic education.

**Persons of Unsound Mind (Section 12):** A person of unsound mind is not competent to contract. Contracts with persons of unsound mind are voidable at their option.

**Disqualified Persons:** Persons adjudicated bankrupt (under the Insolvency Act) may be disqualified from entering contracts.

#### 4. Free Consent (Sections 13–22)

**Free consent** is consent that is not caused by:

- **Coercion (Section 15):** Physical force, threat of force, economic duress — includes oppression of a party in a weak position
- **Undue Influence (Section 16):** Abuse of a dominant position where a party is in a position of trust and confidence — e.g., solicitor-client, doctor-patient relationships
- **Fraud (Section 17):** False representation of a material fact, knowingly or recklessly, with intent to deceive
- **Misrepresentation (Section 18):** Innocent or negligent misstatement of a material fact
- **Mistake (Section 20–22):** 
  - **Bilateral mistake** of fact makes the contract **void** (e.g., both parties mistake the subject matter)
  - **Unilateral mistake** (one party mistakes a material fact) generally does NOT make the contract void
  - Mistake of **law** (not fact) does not make the contract void (but note: where a party is mistaken about a statute, some courts have held this may be relevant)

**Consequence:** Contracts caused by coercion, undue influence, fraud, or misrepresentation are **voidable** at the option of the deceived party. Contracts caused by bilateral mistake are **void**.

## 📦 The Sale of Goods Act (Cap 82)

### Overview

The **Sale of Goods Act (Cap 82)** (originally the UK Sale of Goods Act 1893, received as law) governs contracts for the sale of **movable goods**.

**Definition (Section 1):**
A contract of sale of goods is a contract whereby the **seller transfers or agrees to transfer the property (ownership) in goods** to the **buyer** for a **money consideration called the price**.

### Sale vs. Agreement to Sell

| | **Sale** | **Agreement to Sell** |
|--|---------|----------------------|
| Property | Passes **immediately** on contract | Passes at a **future date** or upon condition |
| Risk | Buyer bears risk immediately | Risk remains with seller until property passes |
| Breach | Seller must deliver; buyer must pay immediately | Party in breach liable for damages |

### Conditions and Warranties (Sections 11–14)

**Conditions (Section 11):**
Terms that go to the **root of the contract**. Breach of a condition gives the innocent party the right to:
- **Reject the goods** (treat the contract as void)
- Claim **damages**

**Warranties (Section 12):**
Collateral terms. Breach of a warranty gives the right to **damages only** — the contract remains valid.

**Examples of implied conditions:**
- **Condition of title (Section 13):** Seller has the right to sell; goods are free from encumbrances
- **Condition of merchantable quality (Section 14):** Goods are of satisfactory quality for their ordinary purpose
- **Condition of fitness for purpose (Section 14):** Where buyer makes known the purpose, goods are fit for that purpose
- **Condition of sale by description (Section 15):** Goods correspond to the description given

### Transfer of Property (Sections 17–19)

The key rule is that **property passes when it is intended to pass** (Section 17). Courts look to:
- The parties' **intention** (ascertained from the terms of the contract and surrounding circumstances)
- For **specific goods** (identified at the time of contract) — property may pass immediately
- For **unascertained goods** (not identified) — property cannot pass until goods are identified and appropriated

**Rule in *Dunlop v Selfmix*:** Where goods are sold by description from an unascertained bulk, property passes when the goods are unconditionally appropriated.

## ⚖️ Agency (Brief)

Under the Contracts Act (Sections 182–238), an **agent** who enters a contract on behalf of a **principal** binds the principal to the contract if the agent had **authority** (actual, implied, or ostensible).

- **Actual authority** — expressly given by the principal
- **Implied authority** — authority that can be inferred from the relationship and the nature of the agency
- **Ostensible (apparent) authority** — where the principal has held out the agent as having authority, third parties may rely on this apparent authority

**Principal's Liability:** The principal is bound by contracts made by the agent within the scope of the agent's authority.

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. **Contracts Act (Cap 73)** is the primary source of contract law — received Indian Contract Act 1872
2. **Offer must be communicated** to be valid; **silence is not acceptance** (Section 4)
3. **Consideration** must move at the desire of the promisor, be real, have value, and not be past consideration
4. **Minors (under 18)** — contracts with minors are **voidable at the minor's option**; the minor can affirm or disaffirm upon majority
5. **Contracts for necessaries** (Section 68) are enforceable against a minor — quasi-contractual remedy
6. Free consent must not be caused by **coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, or mistake**
7. **Bilateral mistake** of fact makes a contract **void**; unilateral mistake does NOT void the contract
8. **Sale of Goods Act (Cap 82)** — distinguishes **sale** (property passes immediately) from **agreement to sell** (property passes later)
9. **Conditions** go to the root of the contract (breach = right to reject); **warranties** are collateral (breach = damages only)
10. Property in goods passes when **intended to pass** (Section 17) — for unascertained goods, property passes when goods are unconditionally appropriated
11. **Implied conditions** under SGA: title (Section 13), merchantable quality, fitness for purpose, sale by description
12. **Agency** — actual, implied, or ostensible authority binds the principal to the contract
