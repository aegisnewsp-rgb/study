# Medieval Indian History

## Introduction

Medieval Indian history spans roughly from the 8th century to the 18th century, a period marked by the rise and fall of powerful dynasties, the establishment of the Delhi Sultanate, the splendor of the Mughal Empire, the flourishing of regional kingdoms, and profound socio-religious movements that shaped Indian culture. For CTET examination, questions from this period test candidates on dynasties, rulers, administrative systems, architectural achievements, and the Bhakti-Sufi traditions.

## The Delhi Sultanate (1206–1526)

### Origins and Establishment

Muhammad Ghori (Muhammad Ghuri) defeated Prithviraj Chauhan in the **Second Battle of Tarain (1192)**, establishing Muslim political control in North India. His slave-general **Qutb-ud-din Aibak** captured Delhi and became the first Sultan in 1206, establishing the **Mamluk (Slave) Dynasty** — the beginning of the Delhi Sultanate.

### Important Dynasties

| Dynasty | Period | Key Rulers |
|---|---|---|
| **Mamluk (Slave)** | 1206–1290 | Qutb-ud-din Aibak, Iltutmish, Balban |
| **Khalji** | 1290–1320 | Alauddin Khalji (conquered Deccan, Ranthambore) |
| **Tughlaq** | 1320–1414 | Muhammad bin Tughlaq (shifted capital to Daulatabad) |
| **Sayyid** | 1414–1451 | Weak rulers, loss of control over provinces |
| **Lodi** | 1451–1526 | Ibrahim Lodi; last dynasty before Babur |

### Key Rulers and Achievements

**Iltutmish (1211–1236):**
- Considered the greatest of the early Sultans
- Organized the **iqta system** (land grants to soldiers/ officials in lieu of salary)
- Expanded Turkish rule across North India
- Added the **qutb minar** (Qutb Minar) in Delhi

**Muhammad bin Tughlaq (1325–1351):**
- Known for eccentric reforms — capital shift to Daulatabad (and back)
- Introduced **token currency** (bronze coins) — failed due to forgery
- Created the **Dak Chowki** (postal system)
- Large-scale taxation in the Doab region — led to rebellions
- Patrons: Ibn Battuta (Moroccan traveler) visited his court

**Firoz Shah Tughlaq (1351–1388):**
- Built **Firoz Shah Kotla** stadium in Delhi
- Constructed canals for irrigation
- Taxed only Muslims; imposed jaziya tax revival
- Built **Hauz Khas** — model for later urban planning

### Administration under the Sultanate

- **Iqta System**: Land granted to military commanders (iqtadars) in exchange for military service — not hereditary ownership. This prevented feudalism but created military landholders.
- **Market Control (Maqatta)**: Fixed prices for goods; market inspectors (muhtasib) ensured quality
- **Character**: The Sultanate was a **military oligarchy** — power rested with the Turkish nobles (the **Chatriyah aristocracy**)
- **Revenue System**: Land revenue (kharaj) was the main source — collected in cash or kind

### Impact of Mongol Invasions

- Repeated Mongol (Mongol/ Turkic) invasions during 13th–14th centuries
- Alauddin Khalji defeated them at the **Battle of Kili (1305)**
- Defensive measures: increased army size, market reforms to finance defense

### Decline of the Sultanate

- Factionalism among Turkish nobles
- Timur's (Tamerlane) invasion (1398) — sacked Delhi
- Provincial governors (governors of Gujarat, Malwa, Jaunpur, Bengal) declared independence
- The **Sayyid and Lodi dynasties** were weak rulers
- Ibrahim Lodi defeated by **Babur** at the **First Battle of Panipat (1526)**

## The Mughal Empire (1526–1857)

### Babur (1526–1530)

- **Zahir-ud-din Muhammad Babur**, descended from Timur (father's side) and Chengiz Khan (mother's side)
- First Battle of **Panipat (1526)**: Defeated Ibrahim Lodi using artillery (cannons) — **End of the Delhi Sultanate**
- Established the Mughal Empire in India
- Built the **Humayun's Tomb** (later) and gardens in Kabul
- Authored **Baburnama** (autobiography in Chagatai Turkic)

### Humayun (1530–1540, restored 1555–56)

- Defeated by **Sher Shah Suri** at the **Battle of Chausa (1539)** and **Kannauj (1540)**
- Fled to Persia (Safavid court) for 15 years
- Restored empire after Sher Shah's death (1540–1545: Sur dynasty)
- Built the **Dinpanah** fort in Delhi

### Sher Shah Suri (1540–1545)

- **Farid Khan**, called Sher Shah ("Tiger King")
- Established the **Sur Dynasty** — greatest of the Afghan rulers
- Administrative reforms:
  - **Currency reform**: Introduced the silver **rupiya** (standardized silver rupee) — continued by the Mughals and British alike
  - **Land revenue**: Used ** Zabt ** system (measurement-based revenue) with the **Kashgari** (revenue officials)
  - **Grand Trunk Road**: Rebuilt the ancient Uttarapatha as a highway from Bengal to Afghanistan (still exists as NH-1)
  - **Postal system (Dak)**: Rest houses (sarais) at regular intervals
  - **Canal construction**: Ganga and Indus canals for irrigation

### Akbar (1556–1605) — The Greatest Mughal

**Reign and Conquests:**
- ascended at age 13; his guardian **Bairam Khan** won the **Second Battle of Panipat (1556)** against Hemu (Vijay Singh)
- Extended Mughal rule across most of India through warfare and diplomacy
- Married **Raja Man Singh** and **Maharana Pratap's** daughter (political marriages)

**Religious Policy:**
- **Din-i-Ilahi** (Tauhid-i-Ilahi): Syncretic religion founded by Akbar (1573) — promoted universal tolerance
- Abolished **jaziya** (tax on non-Muslims) and **pilgrimage tax**
- Built **Ibadat Khana** (House of Worship) at Fatehpur Sikri — invited saints of all religions for debates

**Administration:**
- **Mansabdari System**: Military-civilian grading (mansab) of all officials — ranks (mansabdars) from 10 to 5,000; each had a **zat** (personal) and **sawar** (cavalry) rank. This was the backbone of Mughal administration.
- **Nine-joy theory (Nuqtavis)** suppressed
- Division of empire into **Subas** (provinces) governed by Subedars
- Revenue system: **Ain-i-Akbari** (Part III of the Akbarnama) details land revenue (measured by **zabt**), crops, and administrative matters

**Cultural Achievements:**
- Built **Agra Fort**, **Fatehpur Sikri** (capital 1571–1585), **Buland Darwaza**
- Patronized art — Mughal miniatures flourished
- Translated Sanskrit works into Persian (Ramayana, Mahabharata by Abul Fazl)
- Navy expanded — fort building along coasts

### Jahangir (1605–1627)

- Son of Akbar; ruled from Agra
- Married **Nur Jahan** (who became powerful in court affairs)
- **Nur Jahan** designed the **Tomb of Itimad-ud-Daulah** (Agra) — first Mughal structure with white marble and pietra dura (inlay work)
- Issued farmans (decrees); supported painters like Ustad Mansoor
- Tolerant religious policy continued

### Shah Jahan (1628–1658)

- Known as the "Engineer King" for monumental architecture
- Built the **Taj Mahal** (1632–1653) in memory of wife Mumtaz Mahal — UNESCO World Heritage Site, one of the Seven Wonders
- Built **Red Fort** (Delhi) and ** Jama Masjid** (Delhi)
- Built the **Moti Masjid** in Agra Fort
- Peak of Mughal architecture — **Shah Jahani style**: white marble, symmetry, bulbous dome (gol bun)
- Faced rebellion by son **Aurangzeb** — imprisoned in **Agra Fort** until death (1666)

### Aurangzeb (1658–1707)

- Defeated brothers Dara Shikoh and Shuja in a war of succession
- Imprisoned father Shah Jahan
- **Last effective Mughal emperor** — empire began to fragment

**Key Policies:**
- Reversed religious tolerance: reimposed **jaziya** (1679)
- Promoted orthodox Sunni Islam
- Deccan campaigns against Marathas and Bijapur — increased military expenditure
- Destroyed many Hindu temples (Kashi Vishwanath, Keshav Rai at Varanasi) — caused resentment
- **Maximi:** Mughal revenue (timar) system of mansabdari under strain

**Decline under Aurangzeb:**
- Deccan wars drained treasury
- Increased Maratha insurgency under **Shivaji** (crowned as Chhatrapati, 1674)
- Provincial uprisings: Jats, Satnamis, Sikhs
- No architectural achievements comparable to predecessors
- After his death in 1707, the empire rapidly fragmented

### Later Mughals (1707–1857)

- **Bahadur Shah I (Shah Alam)**: Restored some tolerance; first emperor to reside at Delhi as a puppet
- **Muhammad Shah (1719–1748)**: Invasion by **Nader Shah** (1739) — sacked Delhi, took Peacock Throne, Koh-i-Noor diamond; killed ~30,000 civilians
- **Ahmed Shah Abdali**: Invaded India repeatedly (1747–1769); established Afghan rule in Punjab
- Mughal emperors became **mere pensioners** of the Marathas and later the British East India Company
- **Bahadur Shah Zafar** (1837–1857): Last Mughal emperor; tried to lead the 1857 rebellion; exiled to Rangoon (Yangon) by the British after defeat

## Regional Kingdoms

### Vijayanagara Empire (c. 1336–1646)

- Founded by **Harihara I and Bukka Raya I** in the Deccan (Hampi, Karnataka)
- Flourished under **Krishna Deva Raya** (1509–1529) — "king of the week" — zenith of Vijayanagara culture
- **Temple architecture**: Hampi (UNESCO site) — Dravidian style, monolithic statues (statue of Narasimha), Lotus Mahal, Hazara Rama temple
- Famous for **pushing** agricultural frontiers, building tanks and anicuts (dams)
- Literature: Sanskrit and Telugu classics — **Amukta Malyada** by Krishna Deva Raya
- Defeated by the **Deccan Sultanates** at the **Battle of Talikota (1565)** — five sultanates combined against Vijayanagara

### The Bahmani Sultanate (1347–1526)

- Founded by **Ala-ud-din Hasan Bahman Shah** in the Deccan
- Broke away from the Delhi Sultanate
- Five successor states after collapse: **Bijapur, Golconda, Ahmadnagar, Berar, Bidar**
- These sultanates fought constant wars with Vijayanagara
- Patrons of **literature and architecture** — fusion of Persian and Indian styles

### Other Regional Powers

- **Mewar (Rana Pratap)** — resisted Mughal expansion
- **Chhatrapati Shivaji** (Maratha) — established Hindavi Swarajya; defeated Mughals; built navy; coronation 1674
- **Ahom Kingdom (Assam)** — long resistance against Mughals; defeated Mughals at **Saraighat (1671)**; merged with India in 1947

## Bhakti and Sufi Movements

### The Bhakti Movement

The Bhakti movement was a devotional reform movement that emphasized personal love and devotion to God (often as a personal deity) rather than ritual, caste, or scriptural authority. It cut across caste boundaries and made religious practice accessible to all.

**Key Characteristics:**
- God is accessible through love and devotion (bhakti), not through rituals or priests
- Rejected caste discrimination and priestly authority
- Used regional languages (not Sanskrit) to reach common people
- Monotheistic — one god worshipped in different forms

**Major Saints:**

| Saint | Region | Language | Key Teachings |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ramanuja** (11th–12th c.) | Tamil Nadu | Tamil, Sanskrit | Vishishtadvaita (qualified monism); accessible devotion |
| **Namdev** (13th–14th c.) | Maharashtra | Marathi | God in all — bhakti available to all castes |
| **Nimbarka** (12th c.) | South India | Sanskrit | Dvaita-advaita synthesis; Radha-Krishna devotion |
| **Madhva** (13th c.) | Karnataka | Sanskrit | Dvaita (dualism) philosophy |
| **Guru Nanak** (1469–1539) | Punjab | Punjabi | Founder of Sikhism; ek onkar; universal brotherhood; rejected caste |
| **Tulsidas** (1532–1624) | North India | Awadhi | Ramcharitmanas; devotion to Rama |
| **Mirabai** (1498–1546) | Rajasthan, North | Hindi, Gujarati | Devotion to Krishna; broke caste and gender barriers |
| **Surdas** (16th c.) | North India | Hindi, Braj | Krishna bhakti; Sursagar; blind saint |
| **Tukaram** (1608–1649) | Maharashtra | Marathi | Abhanga poetry; devotion to Vithoba |
| **Eknath** (1533–1599) | Maharashtra | Marathi | Marathi bhakti literature; simple devotion |

**Guru Nanak's Teachings (Foundation of Sikhism):**
- **Ek Onkar**: There is one God
- Rejected caste system and ritualistic practices
- Emphasized **seva** (service), **langar** (community meal), equality
- Founded the institution of **Guru** (teacher succession)
- Guru Granth Sahib compiled by **Guru Arjan** (1604)

### The Sufi Movement

Sufism is Islamic mysticism emphasizing **tawhid** (oneness of God), love, and spiritual devotion. Sufi saints came to India from Central Asia and Persia, establishing **khanqahs** (Sufi lodges) across the subcontinent.

**Key Concepts:**
- **Wahdat-ul-Wajud**: Unity of being — all creation is a manifestation of God
- **Tariqah**: The spiritual path — Sufis follow a guide (Murshid/Pir)
- **Sama**: Whirling or listening to music as spiritual practice
- **Fana**: Annihilation of ego in union with God
- **Sufi orders (Silsilas)**: Chishti, Qadiri, Naqshbandi, Suhrawardi

**Major Sufi Saints in India:**

| Saint | Silsila | Location | Key Contribution |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Nizam-ud-din Auliya** | Chishti | Delhi | Most popular; focused on love for humanity |
| **Khwaja Moin-ud-din Chishti** | Chishti | Ajmer | Founded Ajmer Sharif Dargah |
| **Baba Farid** | Chishti | Faridkot, Punjab | Earliest major Sufi saint in India |
| **Shah Abdul Latif Bhittai** | Qadiri | Sindh | Wrote Shah Jo Risalo in Sindhi |
| **Khwaja Bandanawaz** | Chishti | Gulbarga | Famed saint of Karnataka |

**Impact of Bhakti and Sufi Movements:**
- Challenged rigid religious orthodoxy and caste hierarchy
- Promoted communal harmony — many Sufi shrines visited by people of all faiths
- Enriched regional languages and literature
- Created a syncretic tradition of Indian culture — Kabir (Bhakti-Sufi overlap) is the finest example

**Kabir (15th century):**
- Weavers' caste; neither Hindu nor Muslim formally
- Combined Bhakti and Sufi ideas
- Wrote in **Braj Bhasha** — verses in the Guru Granth Sahib and Bijak
- **Nirgun** (formless God) — God is beyond all form and ritual
- Radical equality — rejected caste, untouchability, religious formalism
- Famous lines: "Kaboji suniye sangat nahee man, jo narahi doori" (Don't go to holy places for God is within you)
