# Natural Resources and Environment

## Ecosystem: Structure and Function

An **ecosystem** is a self-sustaining unit consisting of living (biotic) and non-living (abiotic) components that interact with each other. Examples include a pond, forest, desert, or even a small aquarium.

### Components of an Ecosystem

**Abiotic components (non-living):**
- Sunlight, temperature, water, soil, air, minerals
- Wind, humidity, pH, light intensity

**Biotic components (living):**
- **Producers (Autotrophs):** Green plants, algae (make food via photosynthesis)
- **Consumers (Heterotrophs):** Animals that eat other organisms
  - **Primary consumers (Herbivores):** Eat producers (e.g., deer, rabbit, grasshopper)
  - **Secondary consumers (Carnivores):** Eat herbivores (e.g., frog, snake)
  - **Tertiary consumers (Top carnivores):** Eat other carnivores (e.g., eagle, lion)
- **Decomposers (Detritivores):** Fungi, bacteria that break down dead organic matter and return nutrients to soil

### Food Chain and Food Web

**Food chain:** A linear sequence showing who eats whom at each trophic level.

**Example of a terrestrial food chain:**
> Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Hawk
> (Producer) → (Primary Consumer) → (Secondary Consumer) → (Tertiary Consumer) → (Apex predator)

**Example of an aquatic food chain:**
> Phytoplankton → Zooplankton → Small fish → Large fish → Eagle

**Food web:** A network of interconnected food chains showing multiple feeding relationships (more realistic and complex).

**Trophic levels:** Each level in a food chain/food web is called a trophic level.

**10% rule:** Only about **10% of energy** is transferred from one trophic level to the next. The remaining 90% is lost as heat during metabolic processes. This explains why:
- Food chains are short (usually 3-5 levels)
- Number of organisms decreases at higher trophic levels
- Top predators require large territories to find enough prey

**CTET fact:** Energy decreases at each trophic level; biomass (living matter) also decreases. No energy is recycled — it flows through the ecosystem and is eventually lost as heat.

### Ecological Pyramids

A graphical representation of trophic levels showing:
- **Pyramid of numbers:** Number of organisms at each level (can be inverted in some cases like a single tree supporting many insects)
- **Pyramid of energy:** Energy available at each level (always upright — energy decreases at higher levels)
- **Pyramid of biomass:** Dry weight of organisms at each level

## Biogeochemical Cycles

These are the cyclic pathways through which matter (elements/compounds) moves between living organisms and the physical environment.

### The Nitrogen Cycle

Nitrogen is essential for making **proteins** and **nucleic acids (DNA, RNA)**.

**Key steps:**
1. **Nitrogen fixation:** Atmospheric N₂ (78% of air) is converted to ammonia/ammonium (NH₃/NH₄⁺) by:
   - **Nitrogen-fixing bacteria:** Rhizobium (in root nodules of legumes), Azotobacter, Clostridium
   - **Lightning:** High energy breaks N₂ bonds
   - **Industrial Haber-Bosch process:** Synthesizes ammonia from N₂ and H₂ (fertilizer production)

2. **Nitrification:** Ammonia → Nitrites (NO₂⁻) → Nitrates (NO₃⁻) by Nitrosomonas and Nitrobacter bacteria

3. **Assimilation:** Plants absorb nitrates from soil through roots

4. **Ammonification:** Decomposers break down dead organic matter, releasing ammonia back to soil

5. **Denitrification:** Denitrifying bacteria (e.g., Pseudomonas) convert nitrates back to N₂ gas, releasing to atmosphere

**Important CTET note:** **Rhizobium bacteria** live in root nodules of leguminous plants (pulses, peas, beans) and fix atmospheric nitrogen — this enriches soil fertility. This is why rotating crops with legumes is a farming practice.

### The Carbon Cycle

Carbon is the backbone of all organic molecules.

**Key processes:**
1. **Photosynthesis:** Plants absorb CO₂ from atmosphere → convert to glucose → build organic matter (removes CO₂ from atmosphere)
2. **Respiration:** All organisms release CO₂ by breaking down food for energy (adds CO₂ to atmosphere)
3. **Combustion:** Burning of fossil fuels (coal, petroleum, natural gas) and wood releases stored CO₂
4. **Decomposition:** Dead organic matter decomposed by microbes releases CO₂
5. **Ocean:** Oceans absorb and release CO₂ (act as carbon sinks)

**Carbon cycle balance:** Photosynthesis removes CO₂; respiration, decomposition, and combustion add CO₂. Human activities (deforestation + burning fossil fuels) have disrupted this balance, increasing atmospheric CO₂ → **global warming**.

### The Water Cycle (Hydrological Cycle)

The continuous circulation of water between the atmosphere, land, and oceans.

**Key stages:**
1. **Evaporation:** Water from oceans, lakes, rivers evaporates (solar energy) → water vapour rises
2. **Transpiration:** Plants release water vapour through stomata in leaves
3. **Condensation:** Water vapour cools and condenses around dust particles → forms **cloud droplets** → clouds form
4. **Precipitation:** When water droplets become heavy enough, they fall as **rain, snow, hail** (precipitation)
5. **Runoff:** Water flows over land into rivers, lakes, oceans
6. **Infiltration:** Some water seeps into soil → becomes groundwater → stored in aquifers
7. **Collection:** Water collects in bodies of water (rivers, lakes, oceans)

**CTET important:** Forests play a crucial role in the water cycle — they increase transpiration and facilitate water absorption in soil (reducing runoff and soil erosion).

## Environmental Pollution

### Air Pollution

**Primary pollutants:** CO, SO₂, NO₂, SPM (Suspended Particulate Matter), lead, ammonia

**Major sources:** Vehicle emissions, industrial exhaust, burning of fossil fuels, crop burning

**Effects:**
- **Smog:** London-type smog (SO₂ + fog) and Photochemical smog (NO₂ + hydrocarbons + sunlight → brown haze in cities like Delhi)
- **Ozone depletion:** CFCs (chlorofluorocarbons) destroy stratospheric ozone layer → increased UV radiation → skin cancer, global warming
- **Acid rain:** SO₂ and NO₂ dissolve in rainwater → H₂SO₄, HNO₃ → damages crops, buildings, aquatic life

**Control measures:** CNG (Compressed Natural Gas) use, catalytic converters, tree planting, promoting public transport

### Water Pollution

**Sources:** Industrial waste, agricultural runoff (pesticides, fertilizers), sewage, oil spills, thermal pollution

**Effects:**
- **Eutrophication:** Excessive nutrients (from fertilizers) cause algal bloom → algae die → decomposers consume O₂ → fish die → water body becomes lifeless
- **Bioaccumulation:** Toxins (heavy metals like mercury, lead) accumulate in organisms over time
- **Biomagnification:** Toxins increase in concentration at higher trophic levels (e.g., DDT in food chain → high concentrations in eagles → eggshell thinning)

**Control:** Sewage treatment, banning single-use plastics, checking industrial effluent

### Soil Pollution

- **Pesticides and chemical fertilizers** degrade soil quality and kill beneficial microorganisms
- **Heavy metals** from industrial waste
- **Solid waste** and plastic accumulation
- **Control:** Organic farming, composting, vermiculture, reducing pesticide use

### Noise Pollution

Sound above **85 decibels (dB)** is harmful. Sources: Traffic, aircraft, construction, loud music.
- **Effects:** Hearing loss, stress, sleep disturbance, cardiovascular issues
- **Control:** Noise barriers, restricted hours, ear protection, green belts

## Global Warming and Climate Change

**Greenhouse Effect:**
- Certain gases (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, CFCs) trap infrared radiation (heat) in the atmosphere
- Without this natural greenhouse effect, Earth's average temperature would be -18°C instead of 15°C
- Excessive greenhouse gases → excessive heat retention → **global warming**

**Consequences:**
- Rising temperatures → melting glaciers → sea-level rise → coastal flooding
- Changing rainfall patterns → droughts and floods
- Loss of biodiversity (coral bleaching, species extinction)
- More frequent extreme weather events (cyclones, heatwaves)

**Control measures:**
- **Kyoto Protocol (1997):** International agreement to reduce greenhouse gas emissions
- **Paris Agreement (2015):** Limit global temperature rise to 1.5–2°C
- Reducing fossil fuel use, switching to renewable energy, afforestation

## Biodiversity Conservation

**Biodiversity** = Variety of life forms in a given area.

**Causes of biodiversity loss:**
- Habitat destruction (deforestation, urbanization)
- Overexploitation (overfishing, hunting)
- Pollution
- Invasive species
- Climate change

**Conservation strategies:**

1. **In-situ conservation:** Protecting species in their natural habitat
   - National parks, wildlife sanctuaries, biosphere reserves
   - Examples: Kaziranga (rhino), Jim Corbett (tiger), Sundarbans (tiger)
   - **Biodiversity hotspots:** Western Ghats, Eastern Himalayas, Indo-Burma region

2. **Ex-situ conservation:** Protecting species outside their natural habitat
   - Botanical gardens, zoos, seed banks, gene banks, sperm banks

3. **Sacred groves:** Traditional forest patches protected by local communities (e.g., in Northeast India)

4. **International agreements:**
   - **CITES (1973):** Lists endangered species in appendices; prohibits international trade
   - **IUCN Red List:** Classifies species by extinction risk (Extinct, Critically Endangered, Endangered, Vulnerable, Near Threatened, Least Concern)
