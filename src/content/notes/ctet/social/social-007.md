# Indian Economy

## Introduction

India is the **sixth largest economy** in the world by nominal GDP and the **third largest** by Purchasing Power Parity (PPP). As of recent data, India's GDP is approximately USD 3.7 trillion (nominal) and USD 11.87 trillion (PPP). The Indian economy has transformed from a largely agrarian, closed economy at independence (1947) to one of the fastest-growing major economies in the world, now with a dominant **services sector**, a growing **industrial sector**, and a large but declining **agricultural sector**.

For CTET examination, questions focus on the structure of the Indian economy, key economic concepts, government schemes, and policy frameworks. Understanding the economy is essential for teachers, as economic conditions directly affect access to education, childhood nutrition, and social equity.

## Economic Structure: Three Sectors

### Primary Sector — Agriculture and Allied Activities

Contributes approximately **17–18%** of GDP (declining share) but employs about **42%** of the workforce — highlighting the problem of **underemployment** in agriculture.

**Components:**
- Agriculture (foodgrains, pulses, oilseeds, cotton, sugarcane, jute, tea, coffee, rubber)
- Allied activities: Animal husbandry, dairy, fisheries, forestry, sericulture

**Key Features:**
- **Green Revolution** (1960s–70s): High-Yield Variety (HYV) seeds, chemical fertilizers, irrigation — transformed India from a food-deficit to a food-surplus country
- **Operation Flood** (1970s): Dr. Verghese Kurien — made India the largest milk producer in the world through the White Revolution
- **Yellow Revolution**: Oilseeds production
- **Blue Revolution**: Fisheries

**Land Reforms:**
- Zamindari abolition (post-independence) — removed intermediaries
- Ceiling on land holdings — aimed at equitable distribution
- Tenancy reforms — security for tenant farmers
- However, implementation varied widely across states

**Challenges:**
- Small and fragmented landholdings (average land size declining)
- Dependence on monsoon (irrigation coverage only ~50%)
- Low productivity, disguised unemployment (more people on land than needed)
- Farmer distress, suicides, agrarian crisis

### Secondary Sector — Industry and Manufacturing

Contributes approximately **25–28%** of GDP and employs about **24%** of the workforce.

**Types of Industries:**

1. **Agro-based**: Cotton textiles, sugar, vegetable oil, tea processing
2. **Mineral-based**: Iron and steel, aluminum, cement
3. **Chemical-based**: Fertilizers, pesticides, drugs and pharmaceuticals
4. **Engineering**: Machinery, transport equipment, electronics
5. **Petroleum and energy**: Crude oil, natural gas, refineries

**Key Industrial Policies:**
- **Industrial Policy Resolution 1956 (IPR 1956)**: Based on Nehru's "temple of modern India" — aimed at building a socialist pattern of society; established the **public sector** as dominant; **Licence Raj** (government control of industry); reserved certain industries for the state
- **Liberalization (1991)**: Manmohan Singh's budget (June 1991) — reduced License Raj, opened sectors to foreign investment, devalued the rupee, fiscal discipline

**Make in India:**
- Launched 2014 — aimed to make India a global manufacturing hub
- Sectors: Automobiles, chemicals, IT, pharmaceuticals, textiles, etc.
- Focus: Ease of doing business, attracting FDI

**Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs):**
- Backbone of Indian industry — employ ~63 million people
- **UDYAM portal**: Simplified registration for MSMEs
- **MUDRA (Micro Units Development and Refinance Agency)**: Provides loans to small entrepreneurs without collateral

### Tertiary Sector — Services

Contributes approximately **54–58%** of GDP — the dominant sector of the Indian economy.

**Components:**
- **Trade and commerce**: Retail and wholesale
- **Transport and communication**: Roads, railways, airlines, telecom
- **Financial services**: Banking, insurance, stock markets
- **Real estate**: Housing, construction
- **IT and ITES**: Software services, BPO, KPO — major foreign exchange earner
- **Education and health**: Growing rapidly

**IT Revolution:**
- **Y2K problem** created opportunities for Indian IT firms
- **Software exports**: Major source of foreign exchange; companies like TCS, Infosys, Wipro
- **Digital India**: Government initiative to digitize governance and economy

## National Income and Key Concepts

### Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

The total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within the geographical boundaries of a country in a given year.

- **Nominal GDP**: Measured at current market prices
- **Real GDP**: Adjusted for inflation (GDP at constant prices)
- **GDP Growth Rate**: India's growth has been one of the fastest globally — ~7% average in the 2010s; projected to be among top 3 economies by 2050

### Gross National Income (GNI)

GDP + Net Factor Income from Abroad (remittances from Indian nationals working abroad)

### Per Capita Income

National Income ÷ Population. India ranks low in per capita income (~$2,500), indicating widespread poverty despite high GDP growth.

## Poverty and Unemployment

### Poverty in India

**Official Poverty Line:**
- Planning Commission (now NITI Aayog) defines poverty based on **Monthly Per Capita Expenditure (MPCE)**
- **Tendulkar Committee (2011–12)**: Poverty line at ₹32 in rural areas and ₹47 in urban areas per person per day (at 2011-12 prices)
- **Suresh Tendulkar Committee (2009)**: Unified poverty line basket — replaced the earlier dual (rural/urban) baskets

**Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI):**
- Launched in 2021 based on **NITI Aayog** and **Oxford Poverty and Human Development Initiative (OPHI)**
- Measures poverty across three dimensions: **Health, Education, Standard of Living**
- 415 million Indians are multidimensionally poor (2022 data) — ~30% of population

**Mahatma Gandhi's Views:**
- Poverty is the worst form of violence — Gandhi
- He believed in **village swadeshi** and self-reliance

**Poverty Reduction Schemes:**
- ** Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (MGNREGA)**: 100 days guaranteed wage employment per year to every rural household — largest employment guarantee program in the world
- **Public Distribution System (PDS)**: Ration shops providing subsidized foodgrains (wheat, rice, sugar, kerosene)
- **Antyodaya Anna Yojana**: Priority to the poorest among BPL families — 35 kg foodgrains at highly subsidized rates
- **Prime Minister Jan Dhan Yojana**: Financial inclusion — bank accounts for all households, overdraft facility, RuPay debit card, ₹3 lakh accident insurance

### Unemployment in India

**Types:**
1. **Disguised unemployment**: More people employed than required — especially in agriculture
2. **Seasonal unemployment**: Agriculture-dependent workers face unemployment between cropping seasons
3. **Open unemployment**: Actively seeking work but not finding it — educated unemployed
4. **Structural unemployment**: Mismatch between skills and available jobs

**Data Sources:**
- **National Sample Survey Office (NSSO)** — Periodic Labour Force Survey (PLFS)
- **Centre for Monitoring Indian Economy (CMIE)** — alternative private data

**Recent Data:**
- Unemployment rate (2023): ~7–8% (urban); ~5–6% (rural) — figures vary by source
- Youth unemployment (15–24 years): Higher, around 20–23% (a significant concern)

**Government Programs:**
- **MGNREGA** (2005): Rural employment guarantee
- **PM SVANidhi**: Street vendors
- **PM Rozgar Yojana**: Skill development and employment
- **Start-Up India**: Encouraging entrepreneurship
- **Skill India Mission**: Vocational training, kaushal vikas

## NITI Aayog and Economic Planning

### From Planning Commission to NITI Aayog

**Planning Commission (1950–2014):**
- Established in 1950 under Jawaharlal Nehru
- Formulated Five-Year Plans — served as India's central planning body
- **Goswami Committee (1975)** and **Mahalanobis Model**: Heavy industry-focused growth strategy
- Heavily criticized for centralized control, slow decision-making, insufficient focus on outcomes

**NITI Aayog (National Institution for Transforming India):**
- Founded on **January 1, 2015**, replacing the Planning Commission
- **Composition**: Chairperson (PM), Vice Chairperson, CEO, Governing Council (all Chief Ministers, Lt. Governors), Regional Councils (for special areas)
- **Key Functions**:
  - Cooperative federalism — states as partners, not recipients
  - Design strategic and long-term policy framework
  - Foster competitive federalism among states
  - Allocate resources among competing priorities
  - Monitor and evaluate implementation of programs

**Objectives of NITI Aayog:**
1. Achieve the SDGs (Sustainable Development Goals)
2. Inclusive development — reduce regional disparities
3. Leverage technology
4. Promote entrepreneurship and innovation
5. Attain self-reliance (Atmanirbhar Bharat)

### Atmanirbhar Bharat Abhiyan (2020)

- ₹20 lakh crore stimulus package announced by PM Modi in response to COVID-19
- Five pillars: Economy, Infrastructure, System, Vibrant Demography, and Demand
- Key components: PLI (Production Linked Incentive), MSME relief, Agriculture reforms
- Aim: Make India self-reliant in critical sectors

## Important Economic Concepts for CTET

### Economic Reforms (1991)

The **New Economic Policy (Liberalization, Privatization, Globalization — LPG)** was introduced in 1991 under Finance Minister Dr. Manmohan Singh, facing a severe balance of payments crisis.

**Components:**
- **Liberalization**: Reduced government control on industry; delicensing; freedom to import/export
- **Privatization**: Disinvestment in public sector undertakings (PSUs); entry of private sector
- **Globalization**: Integration with the world economy; Foreign Direct Investment (FDI); WTO commitments

**Impact:**
- GDP growth accelerated
- Services sector (especially IT) boomed
- Foreign exchange reserves increased
- However, agricultural growth remained slow; inequality widened

### Goods and Services Tax (GST)

Implemented **July 1, 2017** — India became a unified common market by replacing multiple indirect taxes (VAT, excise, service tax). GST is levied at multiple rates (0%, 5%, 12%, 18%, 28%) based on the good/service category.

### Fiscal Deficit and Inflation

- **Fiscal deficit**: When government spending exceeds revenue — India has struggled to reduce fiscal deficit
- **Inflation**: Rise in general price level — food inflation and fuel inflation disproportionately affect the poor

### Key Institutions

- **Reserve Bank of India (RBI)**: India's central bank; controls monetary policy, issues currency, regulates banks
- **Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI)**: Regulates stock markets
- **Competition Commission of India (CCI)**: Ensures fair competition
- **International Monetary Fund (IMF)**: India is a member; provides loans in crisis
- **World Trade Organization (WTO)**: India is a founding member; disputes over agriculture subsidies
