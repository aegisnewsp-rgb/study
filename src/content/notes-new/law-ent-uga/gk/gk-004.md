# Topic 4: Human Rights & Constitutional Law in Uganda — UDHR, ACHPR, and Enforcement

## 🟢 Quick Review

Human rights law in Uganda is grounded in Chapter 4 of the Constitution of Uganda 1995 (the Bill of Rights) and international instruments that Uganda has ratified. Uganda was among the first African states to ratify the African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights (ACHPR) in 1986. Understanding the international framework, domestic enforcement mechanisms, and landmark cases is essential for examination.

---

## 🌍 International Human Rights Instruments Applicable to Uganda

### 1. Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR) — 1948

The **UDHR** is not a treaty, but it is recognized as:
- **Customary international law** — binding on states through consistent practice and opinio juris
- **General principle of law** (Article 38(1)(c) ICJ Statute) — courts may apply it interpretatively

Key rights declared by the UDHR:
- All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights (Article 1)
- Right to life, liberty, and security (Article 3)
- Freedom from slavery and torture (Articles 4–5)
- Equality before the law (Article 7)
- Fair trial rights (Articles 10–11)
- Privacy (Article 12)
- Freedom of movement (Article 13)
- Asylum rights (Article 14)
- Property rights (Article 17)
- Social and economic rights (Articles 22–25)

### 2. African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights (ACHPR) — 1981

Uganda **ratified the ACHPR in 1986** — one of the first African states to do so. This was significant, as President Yoweri Museveni's government was committed to human rights as part of its post-1986 political programme.

**ACHPR Articles and Their Relevance to Uganda:**

| Article | Content | Uganda Application |
|---------|---------|-------------------|
| Art. 1 | Duties of states to respect Charter rights | Uganda's obligation to domesticate |
| Art. 2 | Non-discrimination | Applies to all grounds |
| Art. 3 | Equality before law and equal protection | Gender equality, ethnic equality |
| Art. 4 | Right to life and integrity | Death penalty; conflict (LRA) |
| Art. 5 | Prohibition of torture and cruel treatment | Police torture; detention conditions |
| Art. 6 | Right to liberty and personal security | Arbitrary arrest |
| Art. 7 | Right to fair trial | Military tribunals; *Nakuru raid case* |
| Art. 8 | Freedom of religion | Religious freedom |
| Art. 9 | Freedom of expression | Media freedom; *Tenyekuli* case |
| Art. 10 | Right to free association | Political parties; trade unions |
| Art. 11 | Right to assembly | Public demonstrations |
| Art. 12 | Freedom of movement | IDP camps (northern Uganda conflict) |
| Art. 13 | Right to participate in government | Elections; *Besigye* cases |
| Art. 14 | Right to property | Land rights; forced evictions |
| Art. 16 | Right to health | Health policy; HIV/AIDS |
| Art. 17 | Right to education | Free primary education |
| Art. 18(3) | Rights of the family | Women's rights; marriage |
| Art. 20 | Right to self-determination | Applied in *Rwanda v DRC* context |
| Art. 21 | Right to development | Resource control debates |
| Art. 22 | Right to a healthy environment | Oil pipeline; environmental impact |

**Unique Feature: Duties (Article 27)**
Article 27 of the ACHPR imposes duties on individuals toward their family, community, state, and the international community. This is a distinctive feature not found in Western human rights instruments.

### 3. Other Ratified Instruments

Uganda has ratified:
- **ICCPR** (1966) — ratified 1995
- **ICESCR** (1966) — ratified 1987
- **Convention Against Torture (CAT)** — ratified 1987
- **Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC)** — ratified 1990
- **CEDAW** — ratified 1987
- **Rome Statute of the ICC** — ratified 2002 (controversial — Uganda cooperated with the ICC in investigating the Lord's Resistance Army (LRA) war crimes)

## ⚖️ Enforcement Mechanisms in Uganda

### 1. Domestic Enforcement — Chapter 4 of the Constitution

**Article 50** — Enforcement:
Any person who claims that a fundamental or other right has been violated may apply to a **competent court** for a remedy. The court may grant:
- **Declarations** — that a law or action is unconstitutional
- **Injunctions** — orders to stop ongoing violations
- **Compensation** — monetary remedy for loss or damage
- **Orders of mandamus** — compelling the State to act

**Article 137** — Constitutional Jurisdiction:
The High Court has original jurisdiction to hear questions of constitutional interpretation. Where a question arises in any other court, it must be referred to the **Court of Appeal sitting as a Constitutional Court** (5 judges). Constitutional questions of great public importance may go directly to the Supreme Court.

### 2. The Constitutional Court

Under Article 137, the Court of Appeal sitting as a Constitutional Court (5 judges) determines questions of constitutional interpretation referred from the High Court or raised directly.

**Constitutional Court Functions:**
- Determine whether a law is constitutional
- Determine whether a right under the Bill of Rights has been violated
- Issue binding declarations of constitutional validity
- Recommend remedies for constitutional violations

### 3. The African Commission and Court on Human and Peoples' Rights

**African Commission (Banjul, The Gambia):**
Uganda reports periodically to the African Commission under Article 62 of the ACHPR. Uganda's state reports have addressed:
- Civil and political rights
- Women's rights
- Land rights and displacement (northern Uganda)
- Media freedom

**Individual Communications:**
Ugandan individuals and NGOs can bring complaints against Uganda to the African Commission (Article 55 of the ACHPR) for violations of the ACHPR. The Commission may refer cases to the African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights.

**African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights (Arusha, Tanzania):**
Once a case is referred by the Commission, the African Court can issue binding judgments against Uganda. Uganda has accepted the **individual communications jurisdiction** of the African Court — significant for direct petitions by Ugandan individuals.

### 4. UN Human Rights Treaty Bodies

Uganda is state party to all major UN human rights treaties. Treaty bodies that monitor Uganda include:
- **Human Rights Committee (ICCPR)** — receives state reports and individual complaints (where Optional Protocol is ratified)
- **Committee Against Torture (CAT)** — monitoring CAT compliance
- **Committee on the Rights of the Child (CRC)** — monitoring children's rights
- **Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW)** — monitoring women's rights

## ⚖️ Landmark Human Rights Cases

### The Nakuru Raid Case

**Facts:** In 2002, the Ugandan police (Operation clean-up or "Nakuruit") conducted raids in the Nakuru neighbourhood of Kampala, resulting in alleged human rights violations including unlawful killings, displacement, and destruction of property. Human rights organizations petitioned the Constitutional Court.

**Issue:** Whether the government had violated constitutional rights (right to life, dignity, property, and fair trial) during the raid.

**Significance:** This case illustrated the role of constitutional petitions in Uganda and the willingness of the Constitutional Court to entertain challenges against state security operations. It engaged Articles 2, 3, 20, 21, 22, 24, 26, and 28 of the Constitution.

### Besigye Cases

**Kizza Besigye** (Colonel, former MP, and presidential candidate) has been a repeated petitioner on human rights issues:
- **Arrests and detentions** during post-election periods
- Right to political participation (Article 29 — freedom of expression, assembly)
- Right to personal liberty (Article 23) — arrests without warrant

The Besigye cases have repeatedly engaged Articles 23 (liberty), 28 (fair trial), and 29 (expression) of the Constitution.

### The LRA Conflict and Human Rights

The conflict with the **Lord's Resistance Army (LRA)** in northern Uganda (1987–2006) generated significant human rights jurisprudence:
- Cases on **internally displaced persons (IDPs)** — Article 12 (freedom of movement)
- Cases on **child soldiers** — CRC and ACHPR protections for children
- The ICC indicted LRA leaders (Joseph Kony, Vincent Otti) for war crimes and crimes against humanity

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. **ACHPR (ratified 1986)** — Uganda was one of the first states to ratify; Article 27 imposes duties on individuals alongside rights
2. **UDHR** — not a treaty but recognized as customary international law and general principle; widely cited by courts
3. **ICCPR** (ratified 1995) and **ICESCR** (ratified 1987) — the two foundational UN covenants
4. **Article 50 of Uganda's Constitution** — any person can petition a court to enforce fundamental rights
5. **Article 137** — Constitutional Court (5 judges of the Court of Appeal) has jurisdiction over constitutional questions
6. **Article 44** — Human Rights must be enforced even in times of emergency (non-derogable rights)
7. Uganda has accepted the **individual communications jurisdiction** of the African Court — direct petitions possible
8. **The Nakuru Raid Case** — landmark case on police operations, constitutional rights, and state accountability
9. The **Besigye cases** — repeatedly engaged Articles 23 (liberty), 28 (fair trial), and 29 (expression)
10. **Rome Statute** — Uganda ratified 2002; cooperated with the ICC on LRA war crimes investigations
11. Non-derogable rights (cannot be suspended even in emergency): right to life (Art. 22), dignity (Art. 24), freedom from slavery (Art. 25), fair trial (Art. 28)
