# Lower Limb — Hip, Thigh & Knee

### 🟢 Lite — Quick Review (1h–1d)

**Hip Joint**
- Ball-and-socket joint: femoral head + acetabulum
- Acetabular labrum deepens the socket
- Ligaments: iliofemoral (Y-shaped, strongest in body), pubofemoral, ischiofemoral
- Round ligament (ligamentum teres): transmits acetabular artery to femoral head — important in children, less so in adults
- **Blood supply of femoral head:** medial circumflex femoral artery (main supply in adults via artery of ligamentum teres), lateral circumflex femoral artery, artery of ligamentum teres
- **Clinical:** Hip fractures classified by location (femoral neck, intertrochanteric, subtrochanteric); intracapsular fractures risk avascular necrosis of femoral head due to disrupted blood supply

**Femoral Triangle**
- Boundaries: inguinal ligament (superior), sartorius (lateral), adductor longus (medial)
- Contents (lateral to medial): NA VA N — Nerve, Artery, Vein, Nails (lymphatics)
  - Femoral nerve (lateral compartment of thigh)
  - Femoral artery (external iliac → common femoral → superficial femoral)
  - Femoral vein (great saphenous opens into femoral vein)
  - Lymphatics (deep inguinal lymph nodes)
- **Clinical:** Femoral artery puncture for cardiac catheterization; femoral nerve block for thigh surgery

**Knee Joint**
- Largest and most complex joint
- Hinge-type synovial joint with some rotary movements
- Articular surfaces: femoral condyles, tibial condyles, patella
- **Menisci:** fibrocartilaginous C-shaped structures (medial is more mobile and more commonly torn; lateral is more mobile)
- **Cruciate ligaments:** ACL (prevents anterior tibial translation, tight in flexion) and PCL (prevents posterior tibial translation, tight in extension)
- Collateral ligaments: MCL (attached to medial meniscus — associated injuries) and LCL
- **Clinical:** ACL injuries common in sports; McMurray test for meniscal tears; valgus stress test for MCL; varus stress test for LCL

### 🟡 Standard — Regular Study (2d–2mo)

**Bony Anatomy of Lower Limb**

**Femur:**
- Head, neck (angle ~125° with shaft), greater and lesser trochanters, linea aspera, medial and lateral condyles, adductor tubercle
- Neck fracture leads to disruption of blood supply → avascular necrosis
- **Clinical:** Intertrochanteric fractures — fixed with dynamic hip screw; subcapital fractures — hemiarthroplasty in elderly

**Tibia & Fibula:**
- Tibia: medial condyle, lateral condyle, tibial tuberosity (patellar ligament insertion), anterior border, medial malleolus
- Fibula: head, lateral malleolus (forms lateral ankle joint)
- Interosseous membrane connects tibia and fibula
- **Clinical:** Tibial plateau fractures from axial loading (car accidents); tibial shaft fractures; fibular fractures associated with ankle injuries

**Patella:**
- Largest sesamoid bone (embedded in quadriceps tendon)
- Articular surface: 7 facets (odd medial, even lateral, odd number for each eye)
- **Clinical:** Patellar fractures (direct blow or forceful contraction); dislocation (lateral displacement)

**Compartments of Thigh**

**Anterior compartment (quadriceps):** rectus femoris, vastus lateralis, vastus medialis, vastus intermedius
- All supplied by femoral nerve (L2–L4)
- Extend knee; rectus femoris also flexes hip
- Quadriceps femoris inserts via patellar ligament on tibial tuberosity

**Medial compartment (adductors):** adductor longus, adductor brevis, adductor magnus, pectineus, gracilis
- Supplied by obturator nerve (L2–L4)
- Adduction of hip
- **Clinical:** Adductor compartment strain in athletes ("groin strain"); obturator nerve block for adductor muscle spasms

**Posterior compartment (hamstrings):** biceps femoris, semitendinosus, semimembranosus
- Supplied by sciatic nerve (tibial division)
- Hamstrings flex knee and extend hip
- **Clinical:** Hamstring injuries common in sprinters; common origin at ischial tuberosity

**Knee Joint — Detailed Structure**

**Synovial Membrane:**
- Largest synovial cavity in body
- Extends superiorly as suprapatellar bursa
- Infrapatellar fat pad (Hoffa's pad) lies anteriorly
- **Clinical:** Knee effusion (fluid in synovial cavity); Baker's cyst (semimembranosus bursa herniation in popliteal fossa)

**Bursae Around Knee:**
- Prepatellar bursa (housemaid's knee), infrapatellar bursa, suprapatellar bursa, anserine bursa (between sartorius, gracilis, semitendinosus — "pes anserinus")
- **Clinical:** Prepatellar bursitis from kneeling; anserine bursitis in obese patients

**Extensor Mechanism:**
- Quadriceps femoris → patella → patellar ligament → tibial tuberosity
- Medial and lateral patellar retinacula (tendinous expansions from vasti)
- **Clinical:** Patellar tendinitis (jumper's knee); Osgood-Schlatter disease (apophysitis of tibial tuberosity in adolescents)

### 🔴 Extended — Deep Study (3mo+)

**Hip — Detailed Clinical and Surgical Anatomy**

**Acetabulum:**
- Formed by ilium, ischium, and pubis (meeting at triradiate cartilage)
- Acetabular labrum: fibrocartilaginous rim deepening the socket; crucial for stability
- Acetabular notch: inferior defect transmits acetabular artery into ligamentum teres
- **Clinical:** Hip dysplasia → shallow acetabulum → early osteoarthritis; FAI (femoroacetabular impingement) — CAM or pincer type

**Hip Joint Stability Mechanisms:**
- Capsule: strong, dense, reinforced by iliofemoral ligament (prevents hyperextension)
- Labrum: deepens socket and creates negative intra-articular pressure
- Ligamentum teres: minimal role in adults
- Muscle tone: especially gluteus medius/minimus
- **Clinical:** Hip dislocations require immediate reduction (<6 hours) to prevent avascular necrosis; posterior dislocations most common (dashboard injury in car accidents)

**Gluteal Region**

**Gluteus maximus:** largest muscle in body; extends and laterally rotates hip; supplied by inferior gluteal nerve (L5–S2)
- Forms buttock contour; used for sitting up from lying, climbing stairs

**Gluteus medius & minimus:** abduct and medially rotate hip; supplied by superior gluteal nerve
- **Clinical:** Trendelenburg sign/test — when standing on affected limb, pelvis drops contralaterally if gluteus medius is weak (positive Trendelenburg = superior gluteal nerve palsy)

**Deep Gluteal Space (Piriformis Syndrome):**
- Contents: piriformis muscle, sciatic nerve (emerges below it), superior gluteal vessels and nerve, internal pudendal vessels
- **Clinical:** Piriformis syndrome — sciatic nerve compression causing buttock pain radiating down leg; mimics disc herniation

**Popliteal Fossa**
- Boundaries: superolateral (biceps femoris), superomedial (semimembranosus/semitendinosus), inferolateral (lateral head of gastrocnemius), inferomedial (medial head of gastrocnemius)
- Floor: popliteal surface of femur, capsule of knee, popliteus muscle
- Contents (superficial to deep): popliteal vessels, tibial nerve, common fibular nerve, lymphatics
- **Clinical:** Popliteal aneurysm (painful pulsatile mass); popliteal artery occlusion causes severe leg ischemia; Baker's cyst

**Knee Ligament Injuries — Clinical Testing**

| Ligament | Test | Movement Tested |
|---|---|---|
| ACL | Lachman test | Anterior translation of tibia |
| PCL | Posterior drawer test | Posterior translation of tibia |
| MCL | Valgus stress test (knee flexed 30°) | Medial opening |
| LCL | Varus stress test (knee flexed 30°) | Lateral opening |
| Meniscus | McMurray test | Click/lock with rotation |

**Vascular Supply of Lower Limb**
- External iliac → common femoral → superficial femoral (deep artery of thigh) → popliteal → anterior tibial, posterior tibial, fibular arteries
- **Clinical:** Superficial femoral artery (now called femoral artery in SFA segment) — atherosclerosis causes intermittent claudication; popliteal artery aneurysm
