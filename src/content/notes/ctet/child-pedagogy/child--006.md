# Behaviourist Learning Theories

## Introduction

Behaviourism views learning as a change in observable behavior resulting from the association between stimuli and responses. Internal mental processes (thinking, reasoning) are considered outside the scope of scientific study. For CTET, three key figures dominate this section: Pavlov, Skinner, and Bandura.

## Classical Conditioning — Ivan Pavlov (1849–1936)

Pavlov discovered classical conditioning through his research on digestion in dogs. This is the most fundamental form of associative learning.

### Key Concepts

**Unconditioned Stimulus (US)**: A stimulus that naturally and automatically triggers a response (e.g., food → salivation)
**Unconditioned Response (UR)**: The unlearned, natural response to the US (salivation when food is presented)
**Conditioned Stimulus (CS)**: A previously neutral stimulus that, after repeated pairing with the US, triggers a response (e.g., bell)
**Conditioned Response (CR)**: The learned response to the CS (salivation when bell rings)

### Pavlov's Experiment

1. Before conditioning: Food (US) → Salivation (UR); Bell alone → No response
2. Conditioning: Bell (CS) + Food (US) → Salivation (UR) — repeated pairings
3. After conditioning: Bell alone → Salivation (CR)

### Laws of Learning Involved

- **Law of Contiguity**: Two stimuli occurring together repeatedly become associated
- **Law of Readiness**: Learning occurs only when the organism is ready (not exhausted or fatigued)

### Real-World Applications

- Phobias: A neutral object (spider) paired with fear (loud noise) creates phobic response
- Advertising: Product (neutral) paired with positive emotions (beautiful scenery, happy music)
- Classroom: A teacher's warm voice (CS) paired with praise (positive experience) makes students feel good about learning

## Operant Conditioning — B.F. Skinner (1904–1990)

Skinner extended behaviourism to voluntary behavior — actions that are emitted rather than reflexive. His work on operant conditioning is the most directly applicable to classroom management.

### Reinforcement

Reinforcement **increases** the likelihood of a behavior being repeated.

| Type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Positive reinforcement | Add a pleasant stimulus after behavior | Teacher praises a student for raising hand — praise is added |
| Negative reinforcement | Remove an unpleasant stimulus after behavior | Child cleans room to stop mother's nagging — nagging is removed |

**Key distinction for CTET**: Negative reinforcement is NOT punishment. In negative reinforcement, the behavior increases because something bad stops. In punishment, the behavior decreases.

### Punishment

Punishment **decreases** the likelihood of a behavior being repeated.

| Type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Positive punishment | Add an unpleasant stimulus | Student gets scolded for talking in class |
| Negative punishment | Remove a pleasant stimulus | Child loses phone for misbehavior |

> **Memory trick**: Positive = add, Negative = remove. Reinforcement = increase behavior, Punishment = decrease behavior.

### Schedules of Reinforcement

Skinner discovered that how often you reinforce matters significantly for learning:

**Continuous Reinforcement (CRF)**: Every correct response is reinforced. Fast learning but rapid extinction once reinforcement stops.
**Partial Reinforcement**: Not every response is reinforced:

| Schedule | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed Ratio (FR) | Reinforce after set number of responses | Sales commission (every 5 sales) |
| Variable Ratio (VR) | Reinforce after unpredictable number | Slot machines — most powerful for maintaining behavior |
| Fixed Interval (FI) | Reinforce after set time period | Weekly test — effort increases near test time |
| Variable Interval (VI) | Reinforce after unpredictable time | Pop quizzes — consistent study motivation |

**CTET exam note**: Variable ratio schedules produce the most resistance to extinction — this is why gambling and slot machines are so compelling.

## Observational Learning — Albert Bandura (1925–)

Bandura showed that learning can occur by observing others — no direct reinforcement needed. His Social Learning Theory bridges behaviorism and cognitivism.

### Bobo Doll Experiment (1961)

Bandura had children watch adults behave aggressively toward a Bobo doll (inflatable punching bag). Children who observed the aggressive model later reproduced the same behaviors — even without reinforcement.

**Key findings:**
- Learning occurred without immediate performance
- Aggression was higher in children who saw aggressive adult models rewarded (vicarious reinforcement)
- Both boys and girls imitated same-sex models

### Requirements for Observational Learning

1. **Attention**: Must notice the behavior
2. **Retention**: Must remember the behavior
3. **Reproduction**: Must be physically capable of reproducing it
4. **Motivation**: Must have a reason/desire to imitate

### Social Cognitive Theory

Bandura later expanded his theory to Social Cognitive Theory, emphasizing:
- **Reciprocal determinism**: Person, environment, and behavior continuously interact
- **Self-efficacy**: Belief in one's own ability to succeed — the strongest predictor of behavior change
- **Self-regulation**: Ability to control one's own behavior through self-monitoring and self-reward

## CTET Exam Focus

- Pavlov: US, UR, CS, CR — classical conditioning
- Skinner: Positive/negative reinforcement and punishment — know examples
- Schedules: VR most resistant to extinction
- Bandura: Bobo doll experiment, observational learning (attention, retention, reproduction, motivation)
- Vicarious reinforcement: Learning by watching others get rewarded
- Limitations of behaviorism: Ignores cognition, doesn't explain all learning
