#### Major International Sporting Events

**Olympics:**

*Summer Olympics:* Ancient Olympics began 776 BCE in Olympia, Greece; revived in 1896 in Athens.

India's Olympic History:
- First participation: 1900 (Paris); Norman Pritchard (2 silver medals in athletics — 200m, 200m hurdles)
- Independent India's first medal: KD Jadhav (Bronze, Wrestling, Helsinki 1952)
- Gold drought: India went 60 years without individual gold; Milkha Singh famously missed bronze by 0.013 seconds (1960 Rome 400m)
- Individual Gold: Abhinav Bindra (Air Rifle, Beijing 2008) — first individual gold; Neeraj Chopra (Javelin, Tokyo 2020/2021) — second individual gold; Vinesh Phogat (Wrestling, Paris 2024) — 50kg silver, became first Indian woman wrestler to win Olympic medal
- Team Gold: Hockey (1928 Amsterdam to 1980 Moscow — 8 golds; declined period, then bronze 1972; silver 2020 Tokyo)
- Paris 2024: India won 6 medals (1 silver, 5 bronze) — best ever tally; Neeraj Chopra (Silver in Javelin); Manu Bhaker (Bronze in Shooting); Swapnil Kusale (Bronze in Shooting); Sarabjot Singh (Bronze in Shooting); Vignaswaran Sakthivel (Bronze in Wrestling); Aman Sehrawat (Bronze in Wrestling); Indian men's hockey won bronze (2-1 vs Spain)

*Winter Olympics:* India has very few medals; Shiva Keshavan (Luge, 1998–2018) was pioneer; no medals yet

*Commonwealth Games (CWG):*
- Held every 4 years; India is 2nd or 3rd best nation historically
- India topped CWG medal tally: 2010 Delhi (101 gold), 2022 Birmingham (22 gold, 2nd after Australia)
- 2026 CWG: Glasgow, Scotland
- Notable: Shooting removed from 2022 CWG (affects India's medal count)

**Cricket World Cups:**

*ODI World Cups:*
- 1983 (England): India won (Kapil Dev's team); defeated West Indies in final (243/7 vs 183)
- 1992 (Australia/New Zealand): Pakistan won; India did not qualify for semi-finals
- 1996 (India/Pakistan/Sri Lanka): Sri Lanka won; India lost to Sri Lanka in semifinals (1996 Chasing India)
- 1999 (England): Australia won; India lost in Super 5 stage
- 2003 (South Africa/Zimbabwe/Kenya): Australia won; India runners-up (Sachin Tendulkar's 100 centuries)
- 2007 (West Indies): Australia won; India eliminated in group stage (unexpected; led to Dhoni's selection)
- 2011 (India/Sri Lanka/Bangladesh): India won (MS Dhoni captain); Yuvraj Singh — Player of Tournament; defeating Sri Lanka in Mumbai final; first World Cup win since 1983
- 2015 (Australia/New Zealand): Australia won; India lost in semi-finals to Australia
- 2019 (England): England won; India lost in semi-finals to New Zealand (Raina, Jadeja partnership; Dhoni run-out)
- 2023 (India): Australia won; India runners-up; defeated New Zealand in semifinals; lost to Australia in final (Ahmedabad)

*T20 World Cups:*
- 2007 (South Africa): India won; Gautam Gambhir (54-ball 75), Irfan Pathan (3/16); MS Dhoni (32-ball 47*); defeated Pakistan in Johannesburg final
- 2022 (Australia): England won; India lost to England in semi-finals
- 2024 (USA/West Indies): India won; defeated South Africa in final; first T20 WC under Rohit Sharma captaincy; Bumrah Player of the Tournament

*ICC Champions Trophy:*
- 2000 (Nairobi): India won; Sourav Ganguly captain; defeated South Africa in final
- 2013 (England): India won; MS Dhoni captain; defeated England in final
- 2017 (England): Pakistan won; India lost to Pakistan in group stage; Pakistan defeated India in final

**Asian Games:**
- Held every 4 years; 2022 (Hangzhou, China) and 2026 (Aichi-Nagoya, Japan)
- India performs well in shooting, wrestling, boxing, athletics, archery
- Cricket debuted in 2010 Asian Games (Guangzhou); India won gold; absent in 2014; returned in 2022 (Silver — lost to Sri Lanka)

#### Indian Sportspersons — Major Achievements

**Athletics:**
- Neeraj Chopra: Olympic Gold (Tokyo 2020), Silver (Paris 2024); World Champion (2023, Budapest); first Indian male athlete to win Olympic gold in track and field
- PT Usna (Sprinting): 1984 Olympics — 3000m steeplechase bronze; long jump gold at Asian Games; still holds Asian records
- Hima Das: "Dhara Express"; first Indian woman to win gold in track (400m, World U20 Championships 2018); also in mixed relay

**Badminton:**
- Pullela Gopichand: All England Champion (2001); founded Gopichand Badminton Academy; coached PV Sindhu, Kidambi Srikanth
- Saina Nehwal: Olympic Bronze (2012 London); World No. 1; CWG Gold (2010, 2018)
- PV Sindhu: Olympic Silver (2016 Rio), Bronze (2020 Tokyo); World Champion (2019); multiple Super Series titles
- Kidambi Srikanth: World No. 1 (2018); won 4 Super Series titles in a calendar year (2017 — record)
- Prakash Padukone: All England Champion (1980); first Indian to achieve this

**Boxing:**
- Vijender Singh: Olympic Bronze (2008 Beijing); turned professional; remained unbeaten in professional career
- Vikas Krishan: Asian Games Gold; turned professional; competed at Tokyo Olympics

**Wrestling:**
- Sushil Kumar: Olympic Silver (2012 London), Bronze (2008 Beijing); only Indian athlete with 2 individual Olympic medals in wrestling; 2-time World Championship medalist
- Vinesh Phogat: Olympic Silver (Paris 2024, 50kg); first Indian woman wrestler to win Olympic medal; previously injured in 2016 and 2020 Olympics; bronze at CWG
- Sakshi Malik: Olympic Bronze (2016 Rio); first Indian woman to win Olympic medal in wrestling
- Aman Sehrawat: Olympic Bronze (Paris 2024, 57kg freestyle); youngest Indian male wrestler to win Olympic medal (21 years old)

**Shooting:**
- Abhinav Bindra: Olympic Gold (2008 Beijing); first Indian to win individual gold
- Joydeep Karmakar: Silver (2012 London, 50m rifle prone); closest India came to medal before Bindra's gold
- Saurabh Chaudhary: Asian Games Gold (2018); World Cup Gold (2019)
- Manu Bhaker: Bronze (Paris 2024, 10m air pistol); became youngest Indian to win Olympic medal in shooting (17 years old)

**Chess:**
- Viswanathan Anand: World Champion (2000–2002, 2007–2013); 5-time World Champion; helped popularise chess in India; introduced rapid and blitz formats
- Koneru Humpy: Women's World Rapid Chess Champion (2019); second Indian woman after Anand to become GM

**Tennis:**
- Leander Paes: 1996 Olympic Bronze (Atlanta); 18 Grand Slam doubles titles
- Mahesh Bhupathi: 1999 French Open, 1999 Wimbledon (Doubles); started Indian tennis
- Sania Mirza: 18 WTA titles; Mixed Doubles Grand Slam winner (Australian Open 2016, Wimbledon 2015); married to Pakistani cricketer Shoaib Malik

**Football:**
- India never qualified for FIFA World Cup; best AFC Asian Cup performance: 1964 (Runners-up)
- Sunil Chhetri: Most capped Indian player (150+ caps); all-time top scorer (90+ goals)
- AIFF: All India Football Federation

**Other Sports:**
- Mirabai Chanu (Weightlifting): Olympic Silver (Tokyo 2020, 49kg category); World Champion; CWG Gold
- Saikhom Mirabai Chanu: World record in clean & jerk (119kg at 2021)

#### National Sports Awards

**Major Dhyan Chand Khel Ratna (formerly Rajiv Gandhi Khel Ratna):**
- India's highest sporting honour
- Awarded for outstanding performance in sports
- Recent recipients: 2024 — Manika Batra, Vinesh Phogat, etc.; 2023 — Nikhat Zareen, Lakshya Sen, etc.

**Arjuna Award:**
- Second highest; instituted 1961
- For consistent excellent performance
- Named after Arjuna (Mahabharata archer)

**Dronacharya Award:**
- For coaches who have coached sportspersons who have excelled
- Named after Dronacharya (Guru of Pandavas and Kauravas in Mahabharata)

**Maana (Major Dhyan Chand) Award:**
- For lifetime contribution to sports development

#### Sports Governance and Administration

**International Bodies:**
- IOC (International Olympic Committee): Founded 1894 by Pierre de Coubertin; HQ: Lausanne, Switzerland; organises Summer and Winter Olympics; Thomas Bach is President (2013–present)
- ICC (International Cricket Council): HQ: Dubai; administers cricket globally; Test, ODI, T20 formats; Greg Dyke (Chair), Geoff Allardyce (CEO)
- FIFA: Founded 1904; HQ: Zurich; governs football globally; Gianni Infantino (President, 2016–present)
- BCCI: Board of Control for Cricket in India; richest cricket board; not an ICC member (ICC is the global body)
- IOA (Indian Olympic Association): HQ: New Delhi; President: PT Usha (2023 onwards); selects Indian contingent for Olympics

**Sports Leagues in India:**
- IPL (Indian Premier League): 2008; T20 format; 10 teams; world's richest cricket league; BCCI organises; $7+ billion brand value
- ISL (Indian Super League): 2014; football; 12 teams; promoted as India's top football league; Mohun Bagan and East Bengal are historic clubs outside ISL (now joined)
- PKL (Pro Kabaddi League): 2014; 12 teams; massive viewership; considered second most popular league after IPL
- PHL (Pro Hockey League): Field hockey; launched 2023

#### Famous Stadiums and Venues

**Cricket:**
- Lord's (London, England): "Home of Cricket"; 1789 established; 30,000 capacity
- Melbourne Cricket Ground (MCG): Australia; 100,024 capacity; 1981 "Botham's match"
- Eden Gardens (Kolkata): India's largest cricket stadium; 68,000+; historic matches; 1996 World Cup semi-final (India vs England; India lost)
- Wankhede Stadium (Mumbai): 33,000; 2011 World Cup final venue
- Narendra Modi Stadium (Ahmedabad): World's largest cricket stadium (132,000 seats); 2023 World Cup final venue; replaced Sardar Patel Stadium

**Olympics and Athletics:**
- Maracanã (Rio de Janeiro): Football stadium; 1950 FIFA World Cup and 2016 Olympics
- Bird's Nest (Beijing National Stadium): 2008 Olympics; architect: Herzog & de Meuron

**India-Specific:**
- Kalinga Stadium (Bhubaneswar): 2018 Asian Athletics Championships; hockey
- Indira Gandhi Athletic Stadium (Guwahati): 2016 South Asian Games
- Jawaharlal Nehru Stadium (Delhi): 2010 Commonwealth Games; renovated 2010

#### Awards — Beyond Sports

**Major Indian Civilian Awards:**

*Padma Vibhushan:* Second highest civilian award; for exceptional service; ₹5 lakh

*Padma Bhushan:* Third highest; for distinguished service; ₹3 lakh

*Padma Shri:* Fourth highest; for distinguished service in any field; ₹2 lakh

*Note:* All three awards are announced on Republic Day (26 January) every year

**Nobel Prizes — Indian Winners:**
- Rabindranath Tagore (Literature, 1913): Gitanjali; first Asian to win Literature Nobel
- C.V. Raman (Physics, 1930): Raman Effect (scattering of light); discovered Raman scattering
- Har Gobind Khorana (Physiology/Medicine, 1968): Genetic code interpretation; shared with Nirenberg and Holley
- Mother Teresa (Peace, 1979): Founded Missionaries of Charity; worked in Kolkata
- Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar (Physics, 1983): Chandrasekhar Limit; stellar evolution; shared with Fowler
- Amartya Sen (Economics, 1998): Welfare economics; social choice theory; development economics; most famous work: "Development as Freedom"; Nobel in Economics
- Venkatraman Ramakrishnan (Chemistry, 2009): Structure of ribosome; shared with Yonath and Steitz

**Fields Medal (Mathematics — equivalent of Nobel):**
- Manjul Bhargava (2014): Number theory; first Indian-origin mathematician to win;Princeton; raised in Mumbai, studied in USA
- Maryam Mirzakhani (2014, Iran): First woman to win; passed away 2017

#### Cricket Records — For GK

**Test Cricket — Milestones:**
- First Test: Australia vs England (1877, Melbourne)
- First Indian Test: India vs England (1932, Lord's)
- First Indian Test win: India vs New Zealand (1952, Auckland); captain: Lala Amarnath

**ODI Cricket — Milestones:**
- First ODI: England vs Australia (1971, Melbourne)
- First World Cup: 1975 (England); West Indies won

**World Records:**
- Highest individual ODI score: 264 (Rohit Sharma, 2014 vs Sri Lanka)

- Fastest 50/100/150 in ODI: Various records (Shahid Afridi holds 37-ball 100)
- First bowler to take all 10 wickets in Test: Anil Kumble (1999 vs Pakistan, Feroz Shah Kotla, Delhi)

**Great Indian Batters:**
- Sachin Tendulkar: 100 international centuries; "Little Master"; debuted 1989; 200th Test (retired 2013)
- Rahul Dravid: "The Wall"; 13,288 Test runs; played 164 Tests; famously steady
- Virender Sehwag: "Nawab of Najafgarh"; fastest 200 in ODI (219 balls); 254 vs South Africa (2010)
- MS Dhoni: 2007 T20 WC, 2011 WC captain; "Helicopter Shot"; 10,000+ ODI runs

- Sunil Gavaskar: First to score 10,000 Test runs; 1983 WC winning captain
