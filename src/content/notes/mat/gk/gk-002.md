# Indian Polity & Constitution

### 🟢 Lite — Quick Review (1h–1d)
> Rapid summary for last-minute revision before your exam.

Indian Polity is a core component of MAT's General Knowledge section. The Constitution of India (adopted 26 November 1949, enforced 26 January 1950) is the foundation. Key areas to revise: Preamble, Fundamental Rights, Directive Principles, key Amendments, Parliament structure, President vs Prime Minister powers, and Supreme Court jurisdiction.

**High-yield points:**
- Parts of Indian Constitution: Originally 22 parts, 395 articles; now 25 parts, 448 articles
- Schedules: 12 schedules (originally 8)
- Fundamental Rights (Part III): 6 rights; originally 7 (Right to Property removed by 44th Amendment)
- DPSP (Part IV): Non-justiciable; include Gandhiji's concept of Gram Panchayat
- 42nd Amendment (1976): Added "Socialist", "Secular", "Integrity" to Preamble
- 73rd Amendment (1992): Panchayati Raj institutions; 3-tier system
- 74th Amendment (1992): Municipalities; urban local bodies

**Exam tip:** Questions on the constitutional evolution and landmark amendments are frequent. Remember that "Basic Structure Doctrine" was established in Kesavananda Bharati case (1973).

### 🟡 Standard — Regular Study (2d–2mo)
> Standard content for students with a few days to months.

#### The Constitution — Making and Philosophy

**Background:**
- Objective Resolution moved by Jawaharlal Nehru (December 1946) — laid philosophical foundation
- Drafting Committee chaired by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar
- Constituent Assembly: 299 members; first sitting 9 December 1946; final session 26 November 1949
- Adopted on 26 November (now celebrated as Constitution Day); enforced 26 January 1950 (Purna Swaraj Day)

**Sources of Indian Constitution:**
- Government of India Act 1935 (bulk of administrative provisions)
- US Constitution (fundamental rights, president, supreme court, vice-president)
- UK Constitution (parliamentary system, cabinet, convention of minister responsibility)
- Irish Constitution (DPSP — inspired by Irish Constitution)
- Canadian Constitution (quasi-federal structure, residual powers)
- Australian Constitution (concurrent list)

**Preamble:**
"We, THE PEOPLE OF INDIA, having solemnly resolved to constitute India into a SOVEREIGN SOCIALIST SECULAR DEMOCRATIC REPUBLIC..."

Key words: Sovereign (free from external control), Socialist (means of production can be state or cooperative), Secular (no state religion), Democratic (people's rule), Republic (head of state is elected, not hereditary)

#### Parts and Schedules

**Key Parts:**
- Part I: Union and its Territory
- Part II: Citizenship
- Part III: Fundamental Rights (Articles 12–35)
- Part IV: Directive Principles of State Policy (Articles 36–51)
- Part IVA: Fundamental Duties (Article 51A)
- Part V: Union Government (President, Vice-President, Council of Ministers, Parliament)
- Part VI: State Governments (Governor, Chief Minister, Council of Ministers, State Legislature)
- Part XI: Relations between Union and States
- Part XII: Finance, Property, Contracts and Suits
- Part XIX: Miscellaneous

**12 Schedules:**
1. States and Union Territories
2. Emoluments of President, Governors, Judges
3. Oaths/Assertions of constitutional authorities
4. Territories of Indian states (Parliament can alter)
5. Tribal areas (NE states, Jharkhand, Odisha)
6. Scheduled and Tribal Areas (administrative districts)
7. Union List (100 subjects), State List (61 subjects), Concurrent List (52 subjects)
8. Fronturia and excluded areas
9. Repealed (was about property acquisition)
10. Provisions related to Panchayats (added by 73rd Amendment)
11. Provisions related to Municipalities (added by 74th Amendment)
12. Partition of states

#### Fundamental Rights (Articles 12–35)

**Six Fundamental Rights:**

1. **Right to Equality (Articles 14–18):**
   - Equality before law (Article 14)
   - Prohibition of discrimination (Article 15)
   - Equality of opportunity in public employment (Article 16)
   - Abolition of untouchability (Article 17)
   - Abolition of titles (Article 18)

2. **Right to Freedom (Articles 19–22):**
   - Freedom of speech and expression, assembly, association, movement, residence, profession (Article 19) — applicable only to citizens
   - Protection of life and personal liberty (Article 21) — includes right to privacy (Putney vs Union of India, 2017)
   - Protection against arrest and detention (Article 22)

3. **Right against Exploitation (Articles 23–24):**
   - Prohibition of traffic in human beings and begar (Article 23)
   - Prohibition of employment of children below 14 in factories (Article 24)

4. **Right to Freedom of Religion (Articles 25–28):**
   - Freedom of conscience and free profession (Article 25)
   - Freedom to manage religious affairs (Article 26)
   - Freedom from payment of taxes for promotion of religion (Article 27)
   - Freedom from religious instruction in certain educational institutions (Article 28)

5. **Cultural and Educational Rights (Articles 29–30):**
   - Protection of interests of minorities (Article 29)
   - Right of minorities to establish educational institutions (Article 30)

6. **Right to Constitutional Remedies (Article 32):**
   - Dr. Ambedkar called it the "heart and soul" of the Constitution
   - Supreme Court can issue writs: Habeas Corpus, Mandamus, Prohibition, Certiorari, Quo Warranto

**Right to Property (Article 31) — Removed:**
Originally a fundamental right; made a legal right under 44th Amendment (1978). Now covered under Article 300A.

#### Directive Principles of State Policy (Articles 36–51)

**Classification:**

*Socialist Principles:* Equal justice, distribution of material resources; prohibition of monopoly.

*Gandhian Principles:* Village Panchayats (Article 40); protection of cow and calves; establishment of cooperative societies.

*Liberal-Intellectual Principles:* Uniform civil code (Article 44);分离 of judiciary from executive; free and compulsory education (Article 45, later Article 21A by 86th Amendment).

**Key point:** DPSP are non-justiciable (cannot be enforced in court) but are fundamental in governance.

#### Union Government

**President of India:**
- Head of state; elected by indirect election (elected MPs and MLAs)
- 5-year term; eligible for re-election
- Emergency powers under Article 352 (National Emergency): During war, armed rebellion
- Article 356: President's Rule in states (originally from Dr. B.R. Ambedkar's caution)
- Money Bill: President's recommendation needed to introduce

**Prime Minister of India:**
- Head of government; leader of majority in Lok Sabha
- President appoints; no explicit qualifications in Constitution
- Powers: Cabinet meetings, allocation of portfolios, summoning Parliament

**Parliament:**
- Lok Sabha (lower): Maximum 552 members; directly elected; 5-year term
- Rajya Sabha (upper): Maximum 250 members; indirectly elected by state MLAs; 6-year term with 1/3 retiring every 2 years
- President can summon, prorogue, dissolve Lok Sabha
- Ordinary Bill: Both houses; Money Bill: Lok Sabha only; joint sitting to resolve disagreements

#### Key Amendments

| Amendment | Year | Key Change |
|-----------|------|------------|
| 1st | 1951 | Added 9th Schedule; curbed freedom of speech |
| 7th | 1956 | Reorganization of states along linguistic lines |
| 42nd | 1976 | Added Socialist, Secular, Integrity to Preamble |
| 44th | 1978 | Removed Right to Property from FR; made it legal right |
| 61st | 1989 | Reduced voting age from 21 to 18 |
| 73rd | 1992 | 3-tier Panchayati Raj; reservations for SC/ST, women |
| 74th | 1992 | Urban municipalities; reservations for SC/ST, women |
| 86th | 2002 | Right to Education as fundamental right (Article 21A) |
| 101st | 2019 | GST (101st amendment); abolished India's multiple tax system |

#### Supreme Court and High Courts

**Supreme Court:**
- Chief Justice of India appointed by President (after consultation)
- Original jurisdiction: Disputes between states; centre vs state
- Appellate jurisdiction: Constitutional matters; criminal appeals (High Court certificate)
- Advisory jurisdiction: President can refer questions for opinion
- Basic Structure Doctrine: Kesavananda Bharati vs Union of India (1973) — Parliament cannot amend the basic structure of the Constitution

**High Courts:**
- Each state has one High Court (some states share)
- Judges appointed by President (CJs are consulted)
- Powers to issue writs (Article 226) — broader than Article 32

#### Federal Structure and Center-State Relations

**India's Quasi-Federal Nature:**
India is described as a "federal structure with unitary bias." Features of federation: separate constitutions, division of powers, independent judiciary. Features of unity: single citizenship, unified judiciary, emergency provisions.

**Articles on Centre-State Relations:**
- Article 245: Parliament can make laws for whole or part of India
- Article 246: 7th Schedule divides powers
- Article 250: Parliament has overriding power during Emergency
- Article 256: State's duty to comply with Union laws
- Article 355: Duty of Union to protect states from external aggression and internal disturbance
- Article 365: President's Rule when state fails to comply with Union directions

** GST (Goods and Services Tax):**
The 101st Amendment (2019) introduced India's biggest tax reform:
- Subsumed multiple indirect taxes (VAT, CST, Service Tax, Excise, Entry Tax)
- One nation, one market, one tax
- GST Council (Article 279A): Recommends GST rates; requires 3/4th majority (75% of weighted votes)
- Tax slabs: 5%, 12%, 18%, 28%, and exempt categories

#### Electoral Politics

**Election Commission:**
- Chief Election Commissioner (CEC) appointed by President
- Other Election Commissioners: Also appointed by President
- CEC cannot be removed except by impeachment (unlike other ECs)
- Model Code of Conduct: Comes into force from date of election announcement
- NOTA: None of the Above; introduced by Supreme Court in 2013; NOT winner takes office

**Anti-Defection Law (52nd Amendment, 1985):**
- 10th Schedule: Defection rules
- If MP/MLA voluntarily gives up party membership → Disqualification
- If party merges with another (2/3rd members) → No disqualification
- Merger exception: If less than 2/3rd merge, individual defectors face disqualification

#### Emergency Provisions

**Three types of Emergency (Article 352):**
1. National Emergency (war, external aggression, armed rebellion)
2. State Emergency (Article 356) — President's Rule
3. Financial Emergency (Article 360) — rare; never invoked

**National Emergency effect:**
- Article 19 rights suspended
- Parliament can make laws on state subjects
- President's Rule can be imposed without state legislature approval

#### Judicial Review and Basic Structure

**Basic Structure Doctrine evolution:**
- Shankari Prasad vs Union of India (1951): Parliament can amend Fundamental Rights
- Golak Nath vs State of Punjab (1967): Supreme Court reversed — Parliament cannot amend FR
- Kesavananda Bharati vs Union of India (1973): Supreme Court again reversed — Parliament can amend but cannot alter "basic structure" — 7-judge bench, 6-1 majority
- Minerva Mills vs Union of India (1980): Extended and reaffirmed basic structure; held that judicial review is part of basic structure

**What constitutes Basic Structure:**
- Supremacy of Constitution
- Rule of law
- Separation of powers
- Federalism
- Secularism
- Democratic republic
- Judicial review
- Individual freedoms

#### Panchayati Raj (73rd Amendment)

**3-tier system:**
1. Gram Panchayat (village level) — directly elected Sarpanch
2. Panchayat Samiti (block level)
3. Zilla Parishad (district level)

**Elections:** State Election Commissions conduct Panchayat elections (not Election Commission of India, which handles parliamentary and assembly elections)

**Reservation:** Seats reserved for SC/ST (proportionate to population) and women (33%)

#### MAT Exam Strategy for Polity

**Most repeated question types:**
- Matching constitutional articles with their content
- Questions on schedules and parts
- Landmark case years and facts
- Amendment numbers and their content
- President's powers vs Governor's powers
- Differences between Parliament and State Legislature

**Common mistakes:**
- Confusing Article 356 (President's Rule in states) with Governor's rule
- Mixing up original jurisdiction and appellate jurisdiction of Supreme Court
- Confusing GST Council powers with Parliament's powers

**Preparation tip:** Make a single-page chart of the 44th, 73rd, 74th, and 86th Amendments — these are the most frequently tested.
