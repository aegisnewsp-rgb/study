# Topic 7: Governance & Current Affairs in Uganda — NRM, Elections, LCV, and District Administration

## 🟢 Quick Review

Uganda's governance framework has been dominated by President **Yoweri Museveni** and the **National Resistance Movement (NRM)** since 1986. The Constitution of Uganda 1995 introduced a unique "Movement political system" — a form of non-party democracy that was later modified by the 2005 constitutional amendment introducing multiparty politics. Understanding the NRM, the electoral system, local council governance, and district administration is essential for the examination.

---

## 🏛️ The National Resistance Movement (NRM)

### Origins

The **National Resistance Movement (NRM)** was founded in 1981 by **Yoweri Museveni** as a rebel movement to overthrow the Milton Obote government (Obote II regime). The NRM's bush war began from bases in Luweero District and progressively expanded.

In **January 1986**, the NRM captured Kampala and Museveni became President. Rather than establishing a traditional political party system, the NRM introduced the **Movement political system** (also called "no-party democracy").

### The Movement Political System

The Movement was designed as a **non-party system** — political party activity was restricted (not banned outright but effectively limited) for the first two decades of Museveni's rule. Key features:
- **Political parties** were allowed to exist but could not campaign or hold primaries for elections
- Candidates for elected office ran as **individuals** without party labels
- The **NRM was not a formal political party** but rather a broad "movement" open to all Ugandans regardless of political affiliation
- Power was decentralized through the **Resistance Councils** system (later formalized as Local Councils)

**Rationale (according to NRM):** The Movement system was justified as preventing sectarianism, ethnicity, and political violence — problems that had fueled Uganda's previous political crises (the coups and counter-coups of the 1970s and 1980s).

### 2005 Constitutional Amendment — Introduction of Multiparty Politics

In **2005**, a constitutional amendment changed Uganda's political system from the Movement to a **multiparty system**. This followed:
- The **Uganda v Charles K. K.** referendum on the political system (which was controversially conducted without adequate public education)
- Political pressure for change from opposition parties (Forum for Democratic Change — FDC, and others)
- The 2005 amendment formally introduced multiparty politics while maintaining a dominant-party system under the NRM

**Current System:** Uganda now operates as a **multiparty democracy**, but the **NRM remains dominant** — holding the presidency, a majority in Parliament, and controlling most local governments.

## 🗳️ Elections in Uganda

### Presidential Election

**Electoral System:** First-past-the-post (simple majority) in a **single national constituency**.

**Qualifications (Article 102):**
- Ugandan citizen by birth
- At least 35 years old and not more than 75 years old
- Resided in Uganda for at least 10 of the previous 15 years
- Must be nominated by a political party or be an independent candidate

**Term:** 5 years. **Maximum of two terms** (Article 102(b) — originally 2 terms; the 2017 amendment removed the upper age limit but the term limit remained at 2 terms for future presidents, though Museveni had already served beyond this limit through previous terms).

**Notable Elections:**
- **1996** — First multiparty presidential election; Museveni won
- **2001** — Museveni won; strong opposition from Kizza Besigye (FDC)
- **2006** — Museveni won; Besigye contested the results
- **2011** — Museveni won; Besigye again challenged
- **2016** — Museveni won; Besigye challenged in Constitutional Court
- **2021** — Museveni won; Bobi Wine (Robert Kyagulanyi Ssentamu, NUP) challenged the results; significant post-election violence

### Parliamentary Elections

The Parliament of Uganda has **529 members** (as of current composition):
- **286 constituency seats** — directly elected (one MP per constituency)
- **1 woman MP per district** — the 2015 amendment increased the women's quota and introduced youth and disability categories
- **Special Representation** — youth (5 seats for people aged 18–35), workers (5 seats), Persons with Disabilities (5 seats)
- **Ex-officio members** — the Vice President and Attorney General

**Note:** The **2017 amendment** introduced the youth and PWD special representation categories, significantly expanding the number of MPs and increasing representation for marginalized groups.

### Electoral Commission

The **Electoral Commission (EC)** is established under Article 60 of the Constitution. It is responsible for:
- Conducting and supervising presidential, parliamentary, and local government elections
- Delimiting electoral constituencies (redrawing boundaries)
- Registering voters
- Certifying election results

**Chairperson:** The Electoral Commission is headed by a **Chairperson** who is appointed by the President with the approval of Parliament.

**Criticism:** Uganda's Electoral Commission has faced criticism for alleged bias in favour of the ruling NRM, particularly regarding:
- Inadequate access to state media for opposition candidates
- Delayed delivery of election materials
- Concerns about the transparency of the vote tallying process

## 🏡 Local Government — LCV and District Administration

### Constitutional Basis

Article 176 of the Constitution establishes local government as a **core component of Uganda's democratic system**. Local government is a key mechanism for decentralization and community participation.

### Local Council V (LC5) — District Level

**LC5 Chairpersons** (Local Council V Chairpersons) are elected every 5 years in each of Uganda's **over 130 districts**. The LC5 is the highest political authority at the district level.

**Functions of LC5:**
- Supervising the district administration
- Approving district budgets and plans
- Oversight of local development projects
- Representing the district at national level

**Note on Districts:** Uganda has undergone dramatic **proliferation of districts** — from 34 in 1995 to over 130 today. This process of creating new districts has been criticized as politically motivated (creating new administrative units to reward political allies).

### The District Administration

**District Chairman (LC5 Chairperson):** The political head of the district, elected by popular vote.

**Chief Administrative Officer (CAO):** The professional civil servant head of the district administration, appointed by the President. The CAO is responsible for:
- Implementing government programmes
- Managing district civil servants
- Coordinating with central government ministries

**District Planning Committee:** Coordinates development planning at the district level.

### Lower Local Councils

- **LC4** — County level
- **LC3** — Sub-County/Municipality level
- **LC2** — Parish level
- **LC1** — Village level

These lower councils handle community-level governance and dispute resolution.

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. **NRM (National Resistance Movement)** — founded 1981; captured power in 1986; introduced the Movement political system ("no-party democracy")
2. **2005 Constitutional Amendment** — switched Uganda from the Movement system to multiparty democracy
3. **Yoweri Museveni** — President since 1986; longest-serving president in Uganda's history
4. Presidential election is **first-past-the-post** in a **single national constituency** — simple majority wins
5. **Maximum of two 5-year terms** for presidents (Article 102(b)) — applies to future presidents; Museveni has served many terms through the transition from the Movement system
6. **Electoral Commission (EC)** — constitutional body under Article 60; responsible for conducting elections and delimiting constituencies
7. **Uganda has over 130 districts** — district proliferation has been significant and politically controversial
8. **LC5 (Local Council V) chairperson** — elected political head of the district; heads the local council
9. **Chief Administrative Officer (CAO)** — civil servant head of district administration; appointed by the President
10. **Local government structure:** LC5 (district) → LC4 (county) → LC3 (sub-county) → LC2 (parish) → LC1 (village)
11. **2017 amendment** — introduced youth (18–35) and PWD special representation seats in Parliament; removed upper age limit for presidential candidates
12. Opposition parties (FDC, NUP) are active but the **NRM dominates** all levels of government
