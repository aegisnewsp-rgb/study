# IUPAC Nomenclature of Organic Compounds — Naming systematically

### 🟢 Lite — Quick Review (1h–1d)
> Rapid summary for last-minute revision before your exam.

The IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) system provides a universal naming convention for organic compounds. For SLMC, master the systematic naming algorithm: longest chain → numbering → substituent identification → alphabetical ordering of prefixes. The suffix tells you the functional group; prefixes tell you substituents.

**High-Yield Facts for SLMC:**
- Longest chain determines the parent name; number from the end giving substituents lowest numbers
- Alphabetize substituent prefixes (chloro-, methyl-, ethyl-) — ignore prefixes like di-, tri-, tetra-
- Suffix changes with functional group: –ane (alkane), –ene (alkene), –yne (alkyne), –ol (alcohol), –al (aldehyde), –one (ketone), –oic acid (carboxylic acid)
- ⚡ Exam tip: "2-methylbutane" is correct; "3-methylbutane" is wrong because you should number to give the substituent the lower number

## The IUPAC Naming Algorithm — Step by Step

**Step 1: Identify all functional groups** → determine the principal functional group (highest priority)
**Step 2: Choose the longest carbon chain** containing the principal functional group
**Step 3: Number the chain** from the end that gives the principal functional group the lowest number
**Step 4: Name substituents** (alkyl groups, halogens) in alphabetical order with position numbers
**Step 5: Assemble the name** — position numbers + substituent names + parent chain name + suffix

## Functional Group Priority (Highest to Lowest)

When a molecule has multiple functional groups, the highest-priority group gets the suffix and determines the numbering direction:

| Priority | Functional Group | Suffix | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 (highest) | Carboxylic acid | –oic acid | Butanoic acid |
| 2 | Ester | –oate | Methyl ethanoate |
| 3 | Amide | –amide | Propanamide |
| 4 | Aldehyde | –al | Propanal |
| 5 | Ketone | –one | Butanone |
| 6 | Alcohol | –ol | Butanol |
| 7 | Amine | –amine | Butanamine |
| 8 | Alkene | –ene | Butene |
| 9 | Alkyne | –yne | Butyne |
| 10 (lowest) | Halide/R substituents | halo- / alkyl | Chloromethane |

## Straight-Chain Alkanes

| C atoms | Name | Formula |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Methane | CH₄ |
| 2 | Ethane | C₂H₆ |
| 3 | Propane | C₃H₈ |
| 4 | Butane | C₄H₁₀ |
| 5 | Pentane | C₅H₁₂ |
| 6 | Hexane | C₆H₁₄ |
| 7 | Heptane | C₇H₁₆ |
| 8 | Octane | C₈H₁₈ |
| 9 | Nonane | C₉H₂₀ |
| 10 | Decane | C₁₀H₂₂ |

## Alkyl Substituents (Prefixes)

Removing one hydrogen from an alkane gives an alkyl group:

| Parent Alkane | Alkyl Group | Structure |
|---|---|---|
| Methane | Methyl | –CH₃ |
| Ethane | Ethyl | –CH₂CH₃ |
| Propane | Propyl | –CH₂CH₂CH₃ |
| Propane | Isopropyl | –CH(CH₃)₂ |
| Butane | Butyl | –CH₂CH₂CH₂CH₃ |
| Butane | sec-Butyl | –CH(CH₃)CH₂CH₃ |
| Butane | Isobutyl | –CH₂CH(CH₃)₂ |
| Butane | tert-Butyl | –C(CH₃)₃ |

**Alphabetizing substituents:** methyl, ethyl, propyl, butyl — alphabetical order. "di-", "tri-", "sec-", "tert-" are ignored for alphabetizing — but "iso" and "neo" are considered.

**Correct:** 3-ethyl-2-methylpentane (e before m = ethyl before methyl)
**Wrong:** 2-methyl-3-ethylpentane

## Naming Alkenes and Alkynes

**Alkenes (–ene):**
1. Find the longest chain containing the double bond
2. Number to give the double bond the lowest possible number
3. The position of the double bond is given by the lower-numbered carbon of the double bond
4. If cis-trans isomerism is possible, indicate with cis- or trans- prefix

**Examples:**
- CH₂=CH–CH₂–CH₃ → but-1-ene (NOT butene — must give position)
- CH₃–CH=CH–CH₃ → but-2-ene (can be cis-but-2-ene or trans-but-2-ene)
- CH₃–CH₂–CH=CH₂ → but-1-ene

**Alkynes (–yne):** Same rules as alkenes but suffix is –yne
- HC≡C–CH₂–CH₃ → but-1-yne
- CH₃–C≡C–CH₃ → but-2-yne

## Naming Alcohols (–ol)

**Examples:**
- CH₃–CH₂–CH₂–OH → propan-1-ol (1-propanol)
- CH₃–CH(OH)–CH₃ → propan-2-ol (2-propanol / isopropanol)
- When CH₂OH and CH₃ are on a chain: HO–CH₂–CH₂–CH₃ → propan-1-ol

**Diols:**
- HO–CH₂–CH₂–OH → ethane-1,2-diol (ethylene glycol)
- HO–CH₂–CH(OH)–CH₃ → propane-1,2-diol

**Priority:** Alcohol (–OH) outranks halide and alkyl groups → OH gets the lowest number in the chain.

## Naming Aldehydes (–al)

The aldehyde carbon is always C-1 (given priority in numbering).
- HCHO → methanal (formaldehyde)
- CH₃CHO → ethanal (acetaldehyde)
- CH₃–CH₂–CHO → propanal
- OHC–CH₂–CHO → propanedial

## Naming Ketones (–one)

- CH₃–CO–CH₃ → propanone (acetone)
- CH₃–CO–CH₂–CH₃ → butan-2-one (methyl ethyl ketone / MEK)
- CH₃–CH₂–CO–CH₂–CH₃ → pentan-3-one

## Naming Carboxylic Acids (–oic acid)

- HCOOH → methanoic acid (formic acid)
- CH₃COOH → ethanoic acid (acetic acid)
- CH₃–CH₂–COOH → propanoic acid

## Naming Halides (Halo–alkanes)

**Alphabetically:** fluoro-, chloro-, bromo-, iodo-

**Examples:**
- CH₃Cl → chloromethane
- CH₃–CH(Cl)–CH₃ → 2-chloropropane
- CH₃–CCl₃ → 1,1,1-trichloro-2-methylpropane or 2-methyl-1,1,1-trichloropropane
- BrCH₂CH₂Br → 1,2-dibromoethane

**Alphabetizing halides:** "bromo" comes before "chloro" before "fluoro" before "iodo"

## Naming Cyclic Compounds

**Cycloalkanes:**
- Cyclohexane (C₆H₁₂)
- Methylcyclohexane (a methyl group on a cyclohexane ring)
- 1,2-dimethylcyclohexane (two methyl groups on adjacent carbons of the ring)

**Aromatic compounds (Arenes):**
- Benzene is the parent
- Methylbenzene = toluene
- C₆H₅–CH₃ → methylbenzene (toluene)
- C₆H₄Cl₂ → 1,2-dichlorobenzene (ortho), 1,3-dichlorobenzene (meta), 1,4-dichlorobenzene (para)

## Common Substituent Names (Trivial/Common Names)

Some groups are better known by their common names:

| Structure | Common Name | IUPAC Name |
|---|---|---|
| –CH₃ | Methyl | Methyl |
| –CH₂CH₃ | Ethyl | Ethyl |
| –CH(CH₃)₂ | Isopropyl | 1-methylethyl |
| –C(CH₃)₃ | tert-Butyl | 1,1-dimethylethyl |
| –CH₂CH₂CH₃ | Propyl | Propyl |
| –C₆H₅ | Phenyl | Phenyl / Benzene |
| –CHO | Formyl | Oxo / –carbaldehyde |
| –OH | Hydroxyl | Hydroxy |
| –NH₂ | Amino | Amino |

## Numbering Rules for Multiple Functional Groups

When two or more substituents/functional groups exist:

1. **Principal functional group** gets the suffix; the chain is numbered to give it the lowest number
2. **Other substituents** get prefixes with their position numbers
3. **Alphabetize** all substituent prefixes

**Example:** CH₃–CH(Cl)–CH(OH)–CH₃
- Chain: 4 carbons = butane
- Functional group: OH (alcohol) → butanol
- Number from OH side (gives C-1 to the OH carbon): 2-chlorobutan-1-ol? NO — C1 is the CH₂OH end
- Actually: CH₃–CH(Cl)–CH(OH)–CH₃ → number from OH carbon: C1=CH(OH), C2=CH(Cl), C3=CH₃, C4=CH₃ → 3-chlorobutan-2-ol

# Isomerism in Organic Chemistry — Structure, Space, and Shape

### 🟢 Lite — Quick Review (1h–1d)
> Rapid summary for last-minute revision before your exam.

Isomerism is the phenomenon where two or more compounds share the same molecular formula but differ in the arrangement of atoms. For SLMC, distinguish between **structural (constitutional) isomerism** (different connectivity) and **stereoisomerism** (same connectivity, different spatial arrangement). Within stereoisomerism, know the difference between geometric (cis-trans/E-Z) isomerism and optical isomerism (enantiomers).

**High-Yield Facts for SLMC:**
- Structural isomers: same formula, different bond connections — butane vs isobutane (C₄H₁₀)
- Geometric isomers: same formula, different spatial arrangement around a C=C or ring — cis vs trans
- Optical isomers: non-superimposable mirror images — enantiomers rotate plane-polarized light
- Enantiomers: one chiral center → two enantiomers (R and S); two chiral centers → up to 4 stereoisomers
- ⚡ Exam tip: Meso compounds have chiral centers but are achiral overall (internal plane of symmetry) — e.g., tartaric acid meso form

## Overview of Isomerism

```
Isomers (same molecular formula)
├── Structural (Constitutional) Isomers
│   ├── Chain isomerism (straight vs branched chain)
│   ├── Position isomerism (substituent/functional group at different position)
│   ├── Functional group isomerism (different functional groups)
│   └── Metamerism (different alkyl group attachments to same functional group)
└── Stereoisomers (same connectivity, different spatial arrangement)
    ├── Geometric isomerism
    │   ├── Alkenes: cis-trans / E-Z
    │   └── Cycloalkanes: cis-trans
    └── Optical isomerism
        ├── Enantiomers (non-superimposable mirror images)
        └── Diastereomers (non-mirror-image stereoisomers)
```

## Structural Isomerism

### 1. Chain Isomerism
Same molecular formula but different carbon skeleton:

**C₄H₁₀:**
- Butane: CH₃–CH₂–CH₂–CH₃ (straight chain)
- Isobutane (2-methylpropane): CH₃–CH(CH₃)–CH₃ (branched)

**C₅H₁₂:** Pentane, isopentane (2-methylbutane), neopentane (2,2-dimethylpropane)

### 2. Position Isomerism
Same molecular formula and same carbon skeleton but functional group/substituent at different position:

**C₃H₈O (position isomers of propanol):**
- Propan-1-ol: CH₃–CH₂–CH₂OH (OH on terminal carbon)
- Propan-2-ol: CH₃–CH(OH)–CH₃ (OH on middle carbon)

**C₄H₈ (position isomers of butene):**
- But-1-ene: CH₂=CH–CH₂–CH₃
- But-2-ene: CH₃–CH=CH–CH₃

### 3. Functional Group Isomerism
Same molecular formula but different functional groups:

| Formula | Isomeric Pair | Functional Groups |
|---|---|---|
| C₂H₆O | Ethanol / Dimethyl ether | Alcohol / Ether |
| C₃H₆O | Propanal / Propanone | Aldehyde / Ketone |
| C₃H₆O₂ | Methyl methanoate / Propanoic acid | Ester / Carboxylic acid |
| C₃H₈O | Propan-1-ol / Propan-2-ol | Alcohol / Alcohol (structural only) |
| C₄H₁₀O | Butan-1-ol / ethoxyethane | Alcohol / Ether |

### 4. Metamerism
Same molecular formula, same functional group, but different alkyl groups attached to the functional group:

- Diethyl ether (CH₃CH₂–O–CH₂CH₃) vs methyl propyl ether (CH₃–O–CH₂CH₂CH₃) — both C₄H₁₀O

## Stereoisomerism in Alkenes — Geometric Isomerism

### cis-trans (or E/Z) Isomerism

**Condition:** Geometric isomerism requires:
1. Restricted rotation around the bond (C=C double bond or cycloalkane ring)
2. Each carbon of the double bond must have **two different substituents**

**For cycloalkanes:** Cis-trans is possible when two substituents are on the same side vs opposite sides of the ring plane. A disubstituted cycloalkane (e.g., 1,2-dimethylcyclohexane) can be cis (both substituents on same face) or trans (opposite faces).

### E-Z Nomenclature System

When cis/trans is ambiguous (e.g., both carbons have similar groups), use E-Z:

**E (Entgegen = opposite):** Higher priority groups on opposite sides
**Z (Zusammen = together):** Higher priority groups on same side

**Assigning priority:** Cahn-Ingold-Prelog (CIP) rules:
1. Compare atomic numbers of atoms directly attached to the double bond carbon
2. If tied, compare the next set of atoms (treat double/triple bonds as duplicate atoms)
3. Keep going until a difference is found

**Example: But-2-ene (CH₃–CH=CH–CH₃)**
- Both C2 and C3 have: one H, one C (the methyl group)
- Both methyl groups are identical → no E/Z distinction (cis/trans is clear)

**Example: 2-bromo-2-butene (CH₃–C(Br)=CH–CH₃)**
- C2: Br (atomic number 35) and CH₃ (C, atomic number 6) → Br is higher priority
- C3: CH₃ (C, atomic number 6) and H (atomic number 1) → CH₃ is higher priority
- If Br and CH₃ are on SAME side → Z; OPPOSITE sides → E

## Stereoisomerism — Optical Isomerism

### Chirality and Chiral Centers

A molecule is **chiral** (Greek: hand) if it is non-superimposable on its mirror image — like left and right hands.

A **chiral center** (stereocenter) is typically a carbon atom bonded to **four different atoms or groups**:
- Example: 2-bromobutane — CH₃–CH(Br)–CH₂–CH₃
  - The CH(Br) carbon is attached to: CH₃, Br, CH₂CH₃, and H → four different groups → chiral center

**A molecule with NO chiral center may still be chiral** (e.g., allenes, biphenyls with hindered rotation — examples beyond SLMC scope).

### Enantiomers

A pair of enantiomers are:
- Mirror images of each other
- Non-superimposable (even after rotation)
- Identical in physical properties (melting point, boiling point, density)
- EXCEPT: They rotate plane-polarized light in OPPOSITE directions

**R/S Nomenclature:**

**Step 1:** Identify all four substituents on the chiral center
**Step 2:** Assign priorities 1→4 using Cahn-Ingold-Prelog (highest atomic number = priority 1, lowest = priority 4)
**Step 3:** Orient the molecule so the lowest priority (4) group points AWAY from you (behind the plane)
**Step 4:** Trace from 1→2→3:
  - Clockwise → **R** (rectus, Latin for right)
  - Counterclockwise → **S** (sinister, Latin for left)

**Step 5:** If the lowest priority is NOT away, you can:
  - Swap it with whatever IS away (each swap inverts the assignment — if it was R it becomes S)
  - Or mentally invert your result

**Example — 2-bromobutane (CH₃–CH(Br)–CH₂–CH₃):**
- Priorities: Br (1, Z=35) > CH₂CH₃ (2, C attached to C,H,H) > CH₃ (3, C attached to H,H,H) > H (4, Z=1)
- If H is pointing away and the sequence 1→2→3 is clockwise → **R**
- The enantiomer (mirror image) would be **S**

### Meso Compounds

A meso compound has chiral centers but is achiral (optically inactive) due to an **internal plane of symmetry**:

**Classic example: Tartaric acid (2,3-dihydroxybutanedioic acid)**
- Two chiral centers (C2 and C3), both with: H, OH, COOH, and CH(OH)COOH
- meso-tartaric acid: internal plane of symmetry; the molecule is superimposable on its mirror image
- (+)-tartaric acid and (–)-tartaric acid are enantiomers (both chiral, optically active)
- meso-tartaric acid is achiral (optically inactive) despite having two chiral centers

### Diastereomers

Diastereomers are stereoisomers that are NOT mirror images of each other. They arise when a molecule has two or more chiral centers.

**Example: 2,3-dibromobutane (CH₃–CH(Br)–CH(Br)–CH₃)**
- Two identical chiral centers (each has: H, Br, CH₃, CH(Br)CH₃)
- Possible stereoisomers:
  1. **(R,R)** — enantiomer of (S,S)
  2. **(S,S)** — enantiomer of (R,R)
  3. **(R,S) = meso** — has internal plane of symmetry, achiral
- Total: 3 stereoisomers (not 4) because two chiral centers are identical

**Comparison with 2,3-dichlorobutane** (similar structure): Also gives 3 stereoisomers including one meso form.

### Physical Properties of Stereoisomers

| Property | Enantiomers | Diastereomers |
|---|---|---|
| Melting point | Identical | Different |
| Boiling point | Identical | Different |
| Optical rotation | Equal and opposite (±) | Different |
| Chemical reactivity (with achiral reagents) | Identical | Different |

## Degrees of Unsaturation and Isomerism Counting

**DOU formula:** DOU = (2C + 2 – H – X + N) / 2

Each degree of unsaturation can be:
- One double bond (C=C or C=O)
- One ring
- One triple bond (counts as 2 DOU)

**Example:** C₄H₈ → DOU = 1
Possible isomers: but-1-ene, but-2-ene (cis + trans), cyclobutane, methylcyclopropane (5 isomers total)

**Example:** C₃H₈O → DOU = 0 (fully saturated)
Functional group isomers: propan-1-ol, propan-2-ol, methoxyethane (ethyl methyl ether)

## Common Exam Traps

1. **"Chiral center" vs "chiral molecule"** — a molecule with one chiral center is always chiral; a molecule with two identical chiral centers may be meso (achiral)
2. **Cis-trans requires BOTH carbons to have two different substituents** — ethene (CH₂=CH₂) has no geometric isomers even though it has a double bond
3. **Enantiomers have IDENTICAL physical properties** (except optical rotation) — if asked which has higher BP, the answer is: they have the same BP
4. **A racemic mixture** (50:50 R + S) is optically inactive because the rotations cancel
5. **Two chiral centers do NOT always give 4 stereoisomers** — if the centers are identical and there's a plane of symmetry, you get a meso form and only 3 total

## How to Approach Isomerism Questions in SLMC

1. **Calculate DOU first** — tells you how many rings/double bonds/triple bonds are possible
2. **List functional group isomers** (different functional groups with same formula)
3. **List chain isomers** (straight chain vs branched)
4. **List position isomers** (functional group in different position)
5. **For alkenes:** Check cis-trans or E/Z possibility
6. **For chiral compounds:** Count chiral centers; apply n chiral centers → up to 2ⁿ stereoisomers (reduce if symmetry exists)

⚡ **Exam tip:** To find if a molecule is meso — look for two or more identical chiral centers AND an internal plane of symmetry. Draw the Fischer projection: if you can draw a horizontal line through two identical chiral centers and both halves are mirror images, it's meso.
