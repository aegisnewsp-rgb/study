# Word Usage & Analogies

### 🟢 Lite — Quick Review (1h–1d)
> Rapid summary for last-minute revision before your exam.

Word Usage and Analogies test your understanding of word relationships and how words function in context. Analogies present a word pair with a relationship — you must find another pair with the same relationship from the options.

**Types of analogies you'll see:**
- Synonym: Words with similar meanings (happy : joyful)
- Antonym: Words with opposite meanings (hot : cold)
- Part to whole: (page : book)
- Function/purpose: (scalpel : surgeon)
- Worker to tool: (writer : pen)
- Cause and effect: (fire : burn)

**Key strategy:** Establish the relationship between the first pair clearly before looking at options. The most specific relationship is usually correct.

**Exam tip:** Be cautious of superficial similarity — the relationship must be precise. "Dog : bark" is not the same as "Cat : meow" as animal-sound pairs — that's the relationship.

#### Understanding Analogies

An analogy states that two things are similar in certain respects. In MAT, analogies appear as "A is to B as C is to D." Your task is to identify the relationship and apply it.

**Standard format:** A : B :: C : ?

You need to find which option has the same relationship with C as A has with B.

**Step-by-step approach:**
1. Understand the relationship between A and B
2. Identify the specific type of relationship
3. Find an option with the identical relationship between C and its answer

#### Types of Analogies

**1. Synonyms (Similar Meaning):**
Example: CACOPHONY : HARSH :: MELODY : ?
(A) Sweet (B) Loud (C) Musical (D) Strange

Answer: (A) — Cacophony means harsh/discordant sound; melody means sweet/pleasant sound.

**2. Antonyms (Opposite Meaning):**
Example: ELOQUENT : INARTICULATE :: DILIGENT : ?
(A) Lazy (B) Busy (C) Active (D) Hardworking

Answer: (A) — Eloquent (articulate) is opposite of inarticulate; diligent (hardworking) is opposite of lazy.

**3. Part to Whole / Whole to Part:**
Example: SENTENCE : WORD :: CHAPTER : ?
(A) Page (B) Book (C) Paragraph (D) Letter

Answer: (C) — A sentence is made of words; a chapter is made of paragraphs. Note: page is part of chapter too, but paragraph is the direct building block.

**4. Worker to Tool or Action:**
Example: SURGEON : SCALPEL :: CARPENTER : ?
(A) Wood (B) Saw (C) Furniture (D) Nails

Answer: (B) — A surgeon uses a scalpel; a carpenter uses a saw. This is worker-to-instrument.

**5. Purpose or Function:**
Example: CANDLE : LIGHT :: FIREPLACE : ?
(A) Warmth (B) Wood (C) Chimney (D) Room

Answer: (A) — The purpose of a candle is to provide light; the purpose of a fireplace is to provide warmth.

**6. Cause and Effect:**
Example: DROUGHT : FAMINE :: FLOOD : ?
(A) Water (B) Destruction (C) Drought (D) Overflow

Answer: (B) — Drought can lead to famine; flood can lead to destruction.

**7. Degree/Intensity:**
Example: WARM : HOT :: COLD : ?
(A) Cool (B) Freezing (C) Chilly (D) Ice

Answer: (B) — Warm to hot is increasing intensity; cold to freezing continues that progression.

**8. Classification:**
Example: OAK : TREE :: ROSE : ?
(A) Flower (B) Garden (C) Perfume (D) Thorn

Answer: (A) — Oak is a type of tree; rose is a type of flower.

**9. Sequence/Order:**
Example: WEEK : MONTH :: DAY : ?
(A) Hour (B) Year (C) Week (D) Minute

Answer: (B) — A week is part of a month; a day is part of a year.

**10. Symbol to Represented:**
Example: SCALES : JUSTICE :: CROSS : ?
(A) Religion (B) Medicine (C) Christianity (D) Emergency

Answer: (A) — Scales represent justice; cross represents religion (Christianity specifically).

#### Word Usage Questions

MAT also tests your ability to use words correctly in sentences. These are direct vocabulary-in-context questions.

**Strategies for Word Usage:**

1. **Know denotation vs connotation:** Denotation is literal meaning; connotation is the emotional/shultural association.
   - "Childish" and "childlike" both relate to children, but childish implies immaturity (negative), childlike implies innocence (positive/neutral).

2. **Identify the register:** Formal writing requires precise, elevated vocabulary; informal allows colloquialisms.
   - "Enormity" means great evil (not just size); "immense" or "huge" means large size — they are NOT interchangeable.

3. **Watch for words that sound alike but differ:**
   - Continuous (without break) vs Continual (frequent but with breaks)
   - Historic (significant in history) vs Historical (related to history in general)
   - Discrete (separate) vs Discreet (careful, prudent)

4. **Check transitive/intransitive verbs:** Some verbs require objects; some don't.
   - "Lay" requires an object (lay the book); "lie" doesn't (lie down)
   - Past tense: Lay (laid) vs Lie (lay) — commonly confused

#### Advanced Analogy Strategies

**Multiple valid relationships — choose the most specific:**

Sometimes more than one relationship could apply. Always choose the most precise and specific relationship.

Example: PAINTING : MUSEUM :: BOOK : ?
Options: (A) Library (B) Author (C) Page (D) Cover

Both (A) and (C) could work — a painting is displayed in a museum, a book is found in a library. But the relationship should be "artwork : display venue" — so library is better than page.

However, if the question is "PAINTING : CANVAS :: BOOK : ?" then page is correct (the material a book is made on).

**Verbs vs Nouns in Analogies:**

The grammatical role matters. "Paint : Artist" (what an artist does) is different from "Artist : Paint" (artist uses paint).

Example: BIRD : FEATHER :: FISH : ?
(A) Water (B) Scale (C) Swim (D) Gills

"Bird has feathers" (possession/characteristic). "Fish has scales" — so (B) is correct. "Water" is where fish lives (habitat); "gills" is an organ. But the direct characteristic relationship points to scale.

**Understanding Complex Relationships:**

Example: SYMPHONY : COMPOSER :: MONUMENT : ?
(A) Stone (B) Sculptor (C) City (D) Memory

A composer creates a symphony. A sculptor creates a monument. So (B). But note: an architect also designs monuments — however, sculptor is the more direct creator relationship.

**When options seem wrong:**

Sometimes none of the options seem correct. Choose the closest.

- The relationship should be parallel (A:B = C:D)
- Avoid weak or vague relationships
- If stuck, create a sentence: "A is used for B" vs "A is a type of B"

#### Practice Questions

Q1: PROSE : POETRY :: NOVEL : ?
(A) Rhyme (B) Verse (C) Drama (D) Fiction

Answer: (B) — Prose and poetry are two forms of literature; novel and verse (poetry) are forms of literature. Actually, novel is a form of prose fiction — so drama or fiction could work. The best answer is (D) fiction as the broader category novel belongs to.

Q2: CHAOS : ORDER :: IGNORANCE : ?
(A) Education (B) Wisdom (C) Knowledge (D) Intelligence

Answer: (C) — Chaos is opposite of order; ignorance is opposite of knowledge. Knowledge and education/education are related but knowledge is the direct opposite of ignorance.

Q3: RECTOR : UNIVERSITY :: PASTOR : ?
(A) Church (B) Sermon (C) Congregation (D) Prayer

Answer: (A) — A rector governs a university; a pastor leads a church. Church is the institution a pastor heads.

Q4: BELL : CHIME :: RAIN : ?
(A) Drizzle (B) Drop (C) Drum (D) Patter

Answer: (D) — Bell makes a chime sound; rain makes a patter sound. Both are onomatopoeic (sound words). Chime and patter are both sound-based descriptions.

#### MAT Analogy Quick Checklist

1. Identify the relationship between A and B precisely
2. Check: Is it synonym, antonym, part-whole, worker-tool, cause-effect, purpose, degree, or classification?
3. Eliminate options with different relationship types
4. For remaining options, verify the most specific parallel
5. Watch for traps: similar-sounding words, idiomatic pairs
6. When multiple relationships possible, choose the most direct
