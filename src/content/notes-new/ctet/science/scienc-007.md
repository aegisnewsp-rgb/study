# Light, Sound, and Heat

## Light

Light is a form of **electromagnetic radiation** that is visible to the human eye. It travels in straight lines (rectilinear propagation) and does not require a medium (can travel through vacuum).

**Speed of light:** 3 × 10⁸ m/s (approximately 300,000 km/s in vacuum)

### Reflection of Light

**Laws of Reflection:**
1. The incident ray, reflected ray, and normal to the surface all lie in the same plane
2. The angle of incidence (i) equals the angle of reflection (r)

**Key terms:** 
- **Incident ray:** Light ray striking the surface
- **Reflected ray:** Light ray bouncing off
- **Normal:** Perpendicular line drawn at point of incidence
- **Angle of incidence (i):** Angle between incident ray and normal
- **Angle of reflection (r):** Angle between reflected ray and normal

### Mirrors

**Plane Mirror:**
- Produces a laterally inverted (left-right reversed) image
- Image is virtual (cannot be projected on a screen), upright, and of the same size as the object
- Distance of image behind mirror = Distance of object in front of mirror

**Spherical Mirrors:**

| Feature | Concave Mirror | Convex Mirror |
|---------|---------------|---------------|
| **Shape** | Inner curved surface reflecting | Outer curved surface reflecting |
| **Image type** | Real (can be projected) for distant objects | Virtual (always) |
| **Uses** | Reflectors in torches, dentist's mirror, shaving mirror | Rear-view mirrors (vehicles) |
| **Focal length** | Negative (on same side as object) | Positive (behind the mirror) |

**Mirror formula:**
> 1/f = 1/u + 1/v
> Where f = focal length, u = object distance, v = image distance

**Sign convention (Cartesian sign convention):** 
- Object is always on the left (u is negative)
- Distances measured from pole (P); distances measured in direction of light travel are positive

### Refraction of Light

**Refraction** is the bending of light as it passes from one medium to another (e.g., from air to water).

**Laws of Refraction (Snell's Law):**
> n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂
> Where n = refractive index, θ = angle with normal

**Refractive index:** 
> n = Speed of light in vacuum / Speed of light in medium
> Or n = sin i / sin r (when light goes from air to medium)

**Important CTET concept:** When light enters a denser medium from a rarer medium, it bends **towards the normal** (decreases speed). When entering rarer from denser, it bends **away from the normal**.

### Lenses

| Type | Shape | Image | Uses |
|------|-------|-------|------|
| **Convex (Converging)** | Thicker in middle | Real, inverted (converges light) | Camera, eye, magnifying glass |
| **Concave (Diverging)** | Thinner in middle | Virtual, upright (diverges light) | Glasses for myopia |

**Lens formula:** 1/f = 1/v - 1/u

**Power of lens:** P = 1/f (in metres); Unit = Dioptre (D)
- Convex lens: Positive power
- Concave lens: Negative power

### Human Eye and Its Defects

**Structure of the eye:**
- **Cornea:** Transparent front layer; provides 2/3 of eye's focusing power
- **Iris:** Colored, muscular ring; controls size of pupil
- **Pupil:** Opening in iris; allows light to enter
- **Lens:** Flexible, convex lens; fine focusing by changing shape (accommodation)
- **Retina:** Light-sensitive layer at back; contains rods (dim light) and cones (color, bright light)
- **Optic nerve:** Carries signals from retina to brain

**Common defects:**

| Defect | Problem | Cause | Correction |
|--------|---------|-------|-----------|
| **Myopia (Near-sightedness)** | Cannot see distant objects clearly | Eyeball too long or lens too convex | Concave lens (diverging) |
| **Hypermetropia (Far-sightedness)** | Cannot see near objects clearly | Eyeball too short or lens too flat | Convex lens (converging) |
| **Presbyopia** | Difficulty focusing near objects after age 40 | Loss of lens elasticity | Bifocal glasses |
| **Astigmatism** | Blurred vision at all distances | Irregular cornea curvature | Cylindrical lens |
| **Cataract** | Clouding of lens | Age, UV exposure, diabetes | Surgery (artificial lens) |

**Accommodation:** The ability of the eye lens to change curvature to focus near and distant objects. The ciliary muscles contract to make lens more convex for near objects and relax to flatten for distant objects.

## Sound

Sound is a **longitudinal wave** (particles oscillate parallel to wave direction) requiring a medium for propagation. It cannot travel through vacuum.

**Speed of sound:**
- In air: ~343 m/s (at 20°C)
- In water: ~1500 m/s
- In steel: ~5000 m/s

**Sound is faster in solids than liquids than gases** (because particles are more closely packed for better transmission).

### Terms Related to Sound

- **Frequency (f):** Number of vibrations per second; Unit: **Hertz (Hz)**
- **Amplitude:** Maximum displacement from mean position; determines **loudness**
- **Time period (T):** Time for one complete vibration; T = 1/f
- **Wavelength (λ):** Distance between two consecutive compressions (or rarefactions)
- **Speed (v):** v = f × λ

**Loudness vs Pitch:**
- **Loudness:** Determined by amplitude (more amplitude = louder sound)
- **Pitch:** Determined by frequency (higher frequency = higher pitch)
- Example: A soprano sings at high pitch (high frequency); a bass at low pitch (low frequency)

### Echo

An **echo** is a reflected sound wave heard after the original sound. For echo to be heard separately from the original sound, the reflected sound must arrive at least **0.1 second** after the original sound.

Minimum distance for echo:
> d = v × t/2 = 343 × 0.1/2 ≈ 17 metres (in air at 20°C)

**Applications of echo:**
- **SONAR (Sound Navigation and Ranging):** Used to measure ocean depth and detect underwater objects
- **Ultrasound scanning:** Used in medical diagnosis (pregnancy scans, organ imaging)
- **Echoencephalography:** Brain ultrasound

### Ultrasound (above 20,000 Hz)

- Human hearing range: 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz
- **Ultrasound:** Frequency > 20,000 Hz (inaudible)
- **Infrasound:** Frequency < 20 Hz (inaudible)

**Uses of ultrasound:**
- Medical imaging (echocardiography, fetal imaging)
- Cleaning delicate machinery
- Detecting cracks in metals (industrial flaw detection)
- Animal deterrence (rodent repellers)

## Heat and Temperature

### Heat Transfer

**Conduction:** Transfer of heat through solids (without movement of matter). Metals are good conductors; wood and plastic are poor conductors (insulators).
> Good conductors: Copper, Aluminium, Silver
> Poor conductors (insulators): Wood, Plastic, Air, Wool

**Convection:** Transfer of heat in **fluids** (liquids and gases) through movement of the heated fluid.
- Hot fluid rises, cooler fluid sinks → creates convection currents
- Examples: Sea breeze, land breeze, hot air balloon, cooking (heating water)

**Radiation:** Transfer of heat in the form of **electromagnetic waves** (infrared radiation). Does not require a medium. All objects above 0 K radiate heat.
- Dark, matte surfaces: Good absorbers and good emitters of radiation
- Light, shiny surfaces: Poor absorbers and poor emitters (good reflectors)
- Example: Solar panels are painted black to absorb maximum heat; thermos flask has silvered walls to reflect heat back

### Thermometer Scales

| Scale | Freezing Point of Water | Boiling Point of Water | Formula to convert |
|-------|------------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Celsius (°C)** | 0°C | 100°C | — |
| **Fahrenheit (°F)** | 32°F | 212°F | F = (9/5) × C + 32 |
| **Kelvin (K)** | 273 K | 373 K | K = C + 273 |

**CTET Formula:**
- °C to °F: F = (9/5)C + 32
- °C to K: K = C + 273

**Note:** Kelvin is the SI unit of temperature. There are no negative values in Kelvin scale (0 K = absolute zero = -273°C).

### Specific Heat Capacity

The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of **1 kg** of a substance by **1°C** is its specific heat capacity.

> Q = mcΔT
> Where Q = heat, m = mass, c = specific heat capacity, ΔT = change in temperature

**Water's specific heat capacity** (c = 4200 J/kg°C) is very high — this is why water is used as a coolant in car engines and why coastal areas have moderate climates.

⚡ **CTET High-Yield Points:** Light reflects with angle of incidence = angle of reflection. Concave mirrors form real images (can be projected). Convex mirrors always form virtual, upright, diminished images — used as rear-view mirrors. Human eye can focus on near and far objects by accommodation. Sound cannot travel in vacuum. Heat flows from hot to cold objects. Water has high specific heat capacity (used as coolant).
