# Topic 3: Tanzania Court System — Court of Appeal, High Court, Subordinate Courts

## 🟢 Quick Review

Tanzania's court system is dual — the Union courts apply to all of Tanzania, while Zanzibar has its own court system (the Waqf and Zanzibar Court) for non-Union matters. The main hierarchy runs from the Court of Appeal (highest) down through the High Court to subordinate courts. Understanding the jurisdiction, composition, and distinctive features of each level is essential for the examination.

---

## 🏛️ The Court Hierarchy (Union Courts)

### 1. Court of Appeal (Article 109A)

The Court of Appeal is the **highest appellate court** in Tanzania under the Union Constitution. It replaced the former East African Court of Appeal.

**Composition:**
- A **President of the Court of Appeal** (who is also a judge of the Court of Appeal)
- Such number of **Judges of the Court of Appeal** as may be prescribed

**Jurisdiction:**
- Appeals from the High Court (both civil and criminal)
- Questions of law referred from the High Court
- Reviews of its own decisions (limited)
- Constitutional questions under the Constitution (though a separate Constitutional Court has been proposed)

**Sittings:** The Court of Appeal sits in panels of **3 judges** at minimum for most appeals. It may sit in larger benches for significant constitutional matters.

**Note:** Tanzania does not have a dedicated Supreme Court separate from the Court of Appeal. Proposals for establishing a Supreme Court of Tanzania (which would sit above the Court of Appeal) have been discussed in constitutional reform processes but have not yet been fully implemented.

---

### 2. High Court of Tanzania (Article 107)

The High Court is the **superior court of record** with unlimited original jurisdiction in civil and criminal matters.

**Composition:**
- The **Chief Justice of Tanzania** (head of the entire judiciary, including subordinate courts)
- **Judges of the High Court** (appointed by the President on the advice of the Judicial Service Commission)

**Jurisdiction:**

| Jurisdiction | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| Original civil | All civil matters without monetary limit |
| Original criminal | All criminal offences including capital offences |
| Appellate | Appeals from subordinate courts |
| Constitutional | Questions arising from the Constitution (though a Constitutional Court has been proposed) |
| Judicial Review | Supervisory jurisdiction over subordinate courts and tribunals |

**Specialized Divisions:**
The High Court has established specialized divisions:
- **Commercial Court** — Handles banking, insurance, corporate, and commercial disputes
- **Labour Court** — Handles employment and labour relations disputes
- **Land Division** — Handles land disputes (increasingly significant given Tanzania's land law complexity)
- **Family and Probate Division** — Handles succession, family law, adoption

**Key Feature:** The High Court also has jurisdiction over Zanzibar matters that involve Union law — particularly criminal law under the Penal Code (Cap 16).

---

### 3. District Courts

District Courts are **subordinate courts** presided over by **District Magistrates**.

**Jurisdiction:**
- Criminal matters: offences punishable with imprisonment up to **5 years**
- Civil matters: monetary jurisdiction up to a prescribed limit (typically TZS 100,000–500,000 depending on the scale)
- Cannot try capital offences (murder, treason)

**Appeal:** From District Courts, appeals lie to the **High Court**.

---

### 4. Resident Magistrate Courts

Resident Magistrate Courts are presided over by **Resident Magistrates** and have a wider jurisdiction than District Courts.

**Jurisdiction:**
- Criminal: offences punishable with imprisonment up to **7 years**
- Civil: monetary jurisdiction up to TZS 1,000,000 (or higher depending on the scale)
- More serious criminal matters may be tried

**Appeal:** Appeals lie to the **High Court**.

---

### 5. Primary Courts

Primary Courts are the **lowest level courts** in Tanzania, presided over by **Primary Court Magistrates**. They exist primarily for:
- Minor criminal offences (petty offences, traffic offences)
- Small civil claims
- Community-level dispute resolution

**Jurisdiction:**
- Criminal: minor offences (typically up to 3 months imprisonment or fines)
- Civil: very small monetary claims

**Appeals:** From Primary Courts, appeals go to the **District Court** or directly to the **High Court**.

## 🏛️ Zanzibar Courts

Zanzibar has a **parallel court system** for non-Union matters under the Zanzibar Constitution and Zanzibar-specific legislation.

### Zanzibar Court of Appeal (for Zanzibar)

Zanzibar has its own **Court of Appeal** that handles appeals from Zanzibar's courts on Zanzibar-specific matters.

### The Revolutionary Government's Court Structure (Zanzibar)

Zanzibar courts include:
- **Primary Courts** (Mahakama za Kata)
- **Middle Courts** (Mahakama za Wilaya)
- **Supreme Court of Zanzibar** (for certain matters)
- **Court of Appeal of Zanzibar** (final court for Zanzibar-specific matters)

**Note on Union vs. Zanzibar Jurisdiction:**

Where a case involves **Union law** (e.g., the Penal Code, Criminal Procedure Code, Evidence Act), it must be heard in the **Union courts** (Court of Appeal, High Court, subordinate courts), not the Zanzibar courts. The Zanzibar courts only have jurisdiction over **non-Union matters** (Zanzibar-specific legislation, Islamic personal law matters).

## ⚖️ Judicial Independence

### Constitutional Basis (Articles 107B–107F)

The Constitution of Tanzania (as amended) provides for the **Judicial Service Commission (JSC)** to oversee the appointment, discipline, and conditions of service of judicial officers.

**Judicial Independence Guarantees:**
- Judges are appointed by the **President**, but upon **advice** of the JSC (not political interference)
- Judges' remuneration is charged on the **Consolidated Fund** — not subject to annual budgetary approval that could be used to pressure
- Judges cannot be removed except for **misbehaviour or incapacity** following a tribunal recommendation
- No person or institution may interfere with judicial decisions

**Security of Tenure:** Judges hold office until the **mandatory retirement age** (65 years for most judges; extension possible to 70).

---

## 📜 Key Legislation Governing Courts

1. **The Court of Appeal Act (Cap 141)** — Governs the Court of Appeal, its sittings, appeals, and procedure
2. **The High Court Act (Cap 453)** — Establishes the High Court divisions, jurisdiction, and procedure
3. **The Subordinate Courts Act (Cap 54)** — Establishes District Courts, Resident Magistrate Courts, and Primary Courts
4. **Zanzibar Courts Act** — Establishes Zanzibar's parallel court structure

## 🔑 Exam High-Yield Points

1. **Court of Appeal** is the **highest court** in Tanzania (below a proposed Supreme Court that has not been fully established)
2. The **High Court** has unlimited original jurisdiction in civil and criminal matters
3. The **High Court** has specialized divisions: Commercial, Labour, Land, Family/Probate
4. **Subordinate courts hierarchy:** Primary Courts → Resident Magistrate Courts → District Courts → High Court → Court of Appeal
5. Zanzibar has its **own parallel court system** for non-Union matters under the Zanzibar Constitution
6. The **Court of Appeal of Zanzibar** handles appeals on Zanzibar-specific matters
7. Union courts apply **Union law** (Penal Code, Criminal Procedure Code, Evidence Act); Zanzibar courts apply **Zanzibar legislation** for non-Union matters
8. **Judicial Service Commission (JSC)** — established under Articles 107B–107F; advises on appointment and discipline of judges
9. Judges hold office until **mandatory retirement age (65)**, with possible extension to 70
10. The **Chief Justice of Tanzania** heads the entire judiciary (Union courts and the supervisory authority over subordinate courts)
