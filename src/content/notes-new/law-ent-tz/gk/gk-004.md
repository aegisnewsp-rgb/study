# Topic 4: Land Law in Tanzania — Village Land Act, Land Rights, and National Land Policy

## 🟢 Quick Review

Land law is among the most complex and contested areas of Tanzanian law. Tanzania has three categories of land (general land, village land, and reserved land) governed by different statutes. Land disputes are extremely common and land matters fall within the jurisdiction of the High Court's Land Division and specialized Land Courts. Understanding the Village Land Act, the national land policy framework, and the Haya land tenure system (as an example of customary land tenure) is essential.

---

## 🏞️ The Three Categories of Land (Land Act 1999)

The **Land Act 1999** (as amended) classifies all land in Tanzania into three categories, which is a foundational concept.

### 1. General Land (Section 4(1)(a))

**General Land** is all land that is not:
- Village land (under the Village Land Act)
- Reserved land (under forest, wildlife, or other conservation legislation)

General land includes:
- **Town land** (urban areas, municipalities)
- **Compulsorily acquired land** by the government for public purposes
- Land under private freehold or leasehold title

**Key Feature:** General land is administered under the Land Act 1999. It may be held under **right of occupancy** (a statutory right to use land) or under a **customary right of occupancy** (for communities with recognized customary tenure).

### 2. Village Land (Section 4(1)(b))

**Village Land** is land designated as such under the Village Land Act No. 22 of 1999. It comprises:
- Land within registered village boundaries
- Land occupied by villages under customary tenure
- Land designated for village use (grazing, agriculture, settlement)

The **Village Land Act 1999 (VLA)** is the key legislation governing village land.

### 3. Reserved Land (Section 4(1)(c))

**Reserved Land** is land reserved for:
- National parks (e.g., Serengeti, Ngorongoro)
- Forest reserves
- Game reserves
- Wildlife management areas
- Marine parks

This land is held by the government and protected from private development.

## 🏡 The Village Land Act No. 22 of 1999 (VLA)

### Overview

The Village Land Act 1999 was enacted to:
1. Recognize and secure **customary land rights** of village communities
2. Devolve land administration to village-level governance
3. Provide mechanisms for land registration at the village level

### Customary Rights of Occupancy

**Section 22 of the VLA** — All members of a village community who have been occupying land in accordance with customary law are deemed to hold a **Customary Right of Occupancy** over that land.

This means that villagers who have occupied land under customary arrangements (without formal title deeds) have legal recognition of their land rights under the Act.

### The Village Land Registry

**Section 37–44 of the VLA** — Village land must be surveyed, mapped, and registered in the **Village Land Registry**. The process includes:
1. **Land identification and adjudication** — village boundaries are determined
2. **Survey and mapping** — the land is surveyed and a map produced
3. **Registration** — the village land is registered under the Act

**Certificate of Customary Right of Occupancy** (CCRO) is issued to:
- The village community as a whole (village land)
- Individual households within the village (customary rights of occupancy for family land)

### Village Council Powers

Under the VLA, the **Village Council** (an elected body within the local government framework) is responsible for:
- Managing village land
- Allocating land to new residents
- Resolving land disputes within the village
- Protecting land from encroachment

**Section 64 of the VLA** — Disputes over village land may be referred to the **Village Land Dispute Resolution Committee** (VLDRC) before going to court. This is a local mechanism for resolving land disputes without formal litigation.

## 🌍 The National Land Policy (1995)

Tanzania's **National Land Policy** was adopted in 1995 and substantially reformed land law in Tanzania. Key principles:

1. **Land as a National Heritage** — Land is recognized as belonging to the nation (not privately). The State holds land in trust for the people.

2. **Security of Tenure** — The policy sought to provide security of tenure to all landholders — those under customary tenure (village land), those with statutory rights of occupancy, and those with granted rights (freehold/leasehold).

3. **Equitable Access** — All Tanzanians should have equal access to land regardless of gender, ethnicity, or social status.

4. **Gender Equality** — The policy recognized women's rights to land, including under customary tenure. This was an important progressive principle (though implementation has been uneven).

5. **Land Use Planning** — Systematic land use planning to prevent environmental degradation and conflicts.

6. **Market-Based Approaches** — The policy permitted land markets (sale, lease, mortgage of rights of occupancy), subject to regulatory oversight to prevent speculation and dispossession.

### The Right of Occupancy

The concept of **right of occupancy** (haki ya kumiliki) is central to Tanzanian land law:
- It is a **statutory right** to use and occupy land
- It may be **granted** by the government (for general land) or **customary** (for village land under the VLA)
- It can be **transferred** (sold, sub-let, mortgaged) subject to the Land Act
- It does NOT give ownership in the Western sense — the **state holds land in trust**

## 🌾 Haya Land Tenure — A Case Study in Customary Land Tenure

The **Haya people** of Kagera region (northwestern Tanzania) have a well-documented customary land tenure system that illustrates how customary law interacts with statutory land law.

### Key Features of Haya Land Tenure (Traditional)

1. **Land Belongs to the Clan** — Under Haya customary law, land was historically owned by the clan (ekimba) rather than individuals. The clan head (owugabane) administered land on behalf of the clan.

2. **Individual使用权 (Use Rights)** — Individual household heads were allocated land by the clan for cultivation (okusika). This allocation gave use rights but not full ownership. The land reverted to the clan upon abandonment or death without heirs.

3. **Banyankole (Herders) vs. Basomi (Cultivators)** — Haya society distinguished between pastoralist and cultivator communities with different land use arrangements.

4. **Succession** — Haya customary law governed succession: land passed to children (primarily sons) through the clan allocation system. Women had limited independent rights to land.

5. **Evolution Toward Individualization** — Colonial and post-colonial policies progressively encouraged individualization of Haya land rights, leading to formal registration of customary rights.

### Haya Land and the Village Land Act

The Haya land tenure system is now partially recognized under the **Village Land Act**:
- Haya village communities can have their village land recognized and registered under the VLA
- Individual household customary rights can be formalized as Certificates of Customary Right of Occupancy (CCRO)
- Haya clan land that falls within registered village boundaries is recognized as village land under the VLA

## ⚖️ Key Land Disputes and Cases

### The Land Disputes Resolution Framework

1. **Village Land Dispute Resolution Committee (VLDRC)** — local level; Section 64 VLA
2. **District Land and Housing Tribunal** — first formal court level for land disputes
3. **High Court (Land Division)** — appellate and original jurisdiction for significant land matters
4. **Court of Appeal** — final appellate court

### Landmark Cases on Land

- Cases involving **Mabinti** (land rights of women and children in customary succession)
- **Juma v The Attorney General** — government compulsory acquisition of land and the right to compensation
- Land dispute cases involving the **Haya land tenure** in Kagera region
- **Chato and others** — customary rights of occupancy and the right to transfer
