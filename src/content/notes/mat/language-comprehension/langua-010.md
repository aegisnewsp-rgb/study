# Summary & Passage Questions

### 🟢 Lite — Quick Review (1h–1d)
> Rapid summary for last-minute revision before your exam.

Summary and Passage questions are core to MAT's Language Comprehension section. Passages are 300-400 words on topics ranging from business and economics to social issues and science. Questions test your ability to identify the main idea, draw inferences, understand the author's intent, and separate facts from opinions.

**Key strategies:**
- Read the passage actively: identify the topic, the author's stance, and key supporting points
- Answer questions using only the passage — don't add external knowledge
- For inference questions: the answer must follow logically from the passage, not from what you know
- Watch for extreme language (always, never, must) — these are often wrong

**Exam tip:** 40 questions in ~30-35 minutes means under 1 minute per RC passage + questions. Develop a systematic approach.

### 🟡 Standard — Regular Study (2d–2mo)
> Standard content for students with a few days to months.

#### Types of Passage Questions

**1. Main Idea / Central Theme Questions:**
"What is the passage primarily about?"
"What is the main argument of the author?"

The main idea is the author's central point — not a detail, not a conclusion from the passage, but what the passage is fundamentally about.

How to find it:
- The first sentence often introduces the topic
- The last sentence often summarises the conclusion
- The title (if given) hints at the focus
- Ask: "If I had to describe this passage in one sentence, what would it be?"

Common wrong answers:
- Too broad (covers topics not in passage)
- Too narrow (covers only one example)
- A detail rather than the main point
- The opposite of what the author says

**2. Title Selection Questions:**
"Which of the following is the most appropriate title for the passage?"

The best title:
- Reflects the central theme
- Doesn't overreach (cover topics not discussed)
- Doesn't underreach (only covers a minor aspect)
- Creates appropriate expectations

**3. Inference Questions:**
"What can be inferred from the passage?"
"The author most likely believes that..."

Inference follows logically from the passage — it goes beyond what's directly stated but is supported by it.

Key distinction:
- Direct statement: "Sales decreased by 10% last quarter."
- Inference: "The company likely faced challenges in the last quarter." (Follows from sales decrease)

Inference NOT valid:
- Information that contradicts the passage
- Information that requires outside knowledge not provided
- Overgeneralisation from limited data

**4. Tone/Attitude Questions:**
"The author's tone can best be described as..."
"The author's attitude towards the topic is..."

Tone describes the emotional quality of the writing:
- Analytical: Objective examination of facts
- Critical: Finding fault or weakness
- Praising: Approving or endorsing
- Humorous/Satirical: Making fun of the topic
- Skeptical: Doubtful of claims
- Neutral: Balanced, not taking sides

**5. Factual Questions:**
"According to the passage..."
"Which of the following is mentioned as..."

Factual questions require retrieving specific information from the passage. These are generally easier — just locate the relevant part.

**6. Vocabulary-in-Context Questions:**
"The word 'X' as used in the passage means..."
"In the context of the passage, 'X' can best be described as..."

Never choose the dictionary meaning — always the meaning as used in this passage. Context determines word meaning.

#### Reading Strategies for RC Passages

**Step 1: Survey (10-15 seconds)**
- Read the first and last sentences
- Note the title if given
- Identify the broad topic

**Step 2: First Reading (1-2 minutes)**
- Read the entire passage at normal speed
- Try to understand — don't just scan
- Mark: main idea, author's stance, key supporting points

**Step 3: Question Approach (30 seconds each)**
- Read the question before re-reading the passage
- This directs your attention to the relevant part
- If the question asks about the main idea, you may not need to re-read

**Step 4: Answer (15-30 seconds each)**
- Eliminate clearly wrong options
- Choose the best remaining answer
- If unsure between two, pick the one that is more directly supported

#### How to Handle Different Passage Topics

**Business/Economics Passages:**
- Identify the key concept being discussed
- Note the trend or relationship described
- Watch for cause-effect statements
- Common topics: market trends, management strategies, globalisation, financial markets

**Science/Technology Passages:**
- Focus on the main discovery or development
- Note the significance or implications
- Watch for the degree of certainty expressed (may, might, likely vs will, must)
- Common topics: new research, environmental issues, digital transformation

**Social/Policy Passages:**
- Identify the social issue
- Note the author's stance (critical, supportive, balanced)
- Watch for the scope — does the author make generalisations?
- Common topics: education, healthcare, urbanisation, inequality

**Philosophy/Critical Thinking Passages:**
- Identify the central argument
- Note the logical structure (premise → conclusion)
- Watch for assumptions not stated
- These passages require careful reading for nuance

### 🔴 Extended — Deep Study (3mo+)

#### Deep Dive: Inference and Implication

**Levels of textual understanding:**

1. **Explicit content:** Directly stated in text
   - "The unemployment rate rose to 8%."

2. **Implicit content:** Logically implied but not stated
   - "The unemployment rate rose to 8%, while inflation reached a 10-year high."
   - Implied: The economy faced both high unemployment and inflation (stagflation scenario)

3. **Implicature:** Pragmatic implication based on context
   - "The minister said the policy would be 'reviewed shortly' when asked about its implementation timeline."
   - Implicature: The minister did not give a firm commitment — the policy may be delayed

**Types of valid inferences:**

**Deductive inference:** The conclusion necessarily follows from the premises
- If all A are B, and X is A, then X is B
- "All employees received the memo. Priya is an employee." → Priya received the memo.

**Probabilistic inference:** The conclusion likely follows but not necessarily
- "80% of graduates from Program X secure jobs within 6 months."
- It is likely but not certain that any particular graduate will find a job.

**Analogical inference:** Drawing parallels
- "City A's policy to reduce traffic congestion worked. City B faces similar conditions."
- It may work in City B, but conditions may not be identical.

**What cannot be inferred:**

- Information requiring knowledge outside the passage
- Generalisations beyond what the data supports
- Causal claims when only correlation is shown
- Predictions that go beyond what the passage provides

#### Distinguishing Facts, Opinions, and Arguments

**Fact:** Can be verified objectively. "India has 28 states."
**Opinion:** Subjective judgment. "India should have fewer states."
**Argument:** A claim supported by evidence. "India should have fewer states because administration becomes more efficient."

In passage analysis:
- Identify which statements are presented as facts vs opinions
- Note if the author attributes opinions to specific people ("According to economists...")
- Watch for opinion presented as fact

#### Author's Purpose and Intended Audience

**Purpose types:**
- Inform: Present information objectively
- Persuade: Convince the reader of a position
- Entertain: Engage through narrative or humour
- Critique: Analyse and find fault
- Compare/Contrast: Examine similarities and differences

**Audience clues:**
- Technical language suggests specialist audience
- Simple explanations suggest general audience
- Policy suggestions suggest government/policy audience
- Business context suggests corporate audience

The author's purpose and audience affect how evidence is presented and what conclusions are drawn.

#### Advanced Question-Writing Analysis

**Main idea — common structures:**

The main idea often appears in predictable locations:
- Introduction: "X has been Y. This essay argues that Z."
- Conclusion: "In conclusion, X means Y."
- Spread throughout: The main idea is reinforced repeatedly in multiple paragraphs

**Strengthening/weakening arguments:**

If a question asks which option would strengthen/weaken an argument, focus on the assumption — the unstated premise connecting evidence to conclusion.

Stronger argument options:
- Provide additional evidence for the conclusion
- Rule out alternative explanations
- Demonstrate the mechanism connecting cause and effect

Weaker argument options:
- Introduce evidence unrelated to the core logic
- Attack the argument's presentation rather than its substance
- Misstate the argument's position

#### Time Management for RC

**Per-passage time budget:**
- Reading: 2-3 minutes
- Questions (4-5): 4-5 minutes (under 1 minute each)
- Total: 6-8 minutes per passage

**Speed-reading tips:**
- Skip examples (unless the question asks about them)
- Focus on topic sentences and conclusion sentences
- Don't re-read for main idea if you understood it
- For factual questions, scan to the relevant section

**When stuck on a question:**
- Eliminate options you know are wrong
- The answer is often the one that doesn't contradict the passage
- If two options seem correct, choose the more directly stated one
- Don't spend more than 90 seconds on any single question

#### MAT RC Quick Checklist

1. Read actively — topic, stance, key points
2. For main idea: One-sentence summary
3. For inference: Must follow from passage, not from outside knowledge
4. For tone: Identify emotional quality
5. For vocabulary: Use context, not dictionary
6. Watch for extreme language (always, never, must) — often wrong
7. Eliminate before choosing
8. Stay within time: 6-8 minutes per passage maximum
