# Equilibrium

### ūüüĘ Lite ‚ÄĒ Quick Review (1h‚Äď1d)
> Rapid summary for last-minute revision before your exam.

Equilibrium in Chemistry has two parts: Chemical Equilibrium (this chapter) and Ionic Equilibrium (a closely related but distinct chapter). This file covers the general principles ‚ÄĒ the equilibrium constant, Le Chatelier's principle, and reaction quotient. Ionic equilibria (pH, buffers, solubility, hydrolysis) are covered separately.

**Key Formulae for Quick Recall:**

- **Law of Mass Action**: For aA + bB ‚áĆ cC + dD: K_c = [C]^c[D]^d/[A]^a[B]^b
- **K_p = K_c(RT)^{őĒn}** where őĒn = (c+d) ‚ąí (a+b) for gaseous reactions
- **K_p = K_c √ó (RT)^{őĒn_g}** ‚ÄĒ if őĒn_g = 0, K_p = K_c
- **Reaction Quotient Q**: Same formula as K_c but with initial concentrations. Compare Q vs K: Q < K ‚Üí forward reaction proceeds; Q > K ‚Üí reverse; Q = K ‚Üí at equilibrium
- **Le Chatelier's Principle**: If a stress is applied to a system at equilibrium, the system shifts to counteract the stress (favors endothermic direction for temperature increase, side with more moles for pressure decrease).
- **K‚āā = K‚āĀ √ó e^{(‚ąíőĒH¬į/R)(1/T‚āā ‚ąí 1/T‚āĀ)}** ‚ÄĒ van 't Hoff equation for temperature dependence of K

‚ö° **Exam tip**: In heterogeneous equilibrium (e.g., CaCO‚āÉ(s) ‚áĆ CaO(s) + CO‚āā(g)), pure solids and liquids are omitted from the equilibrium expression. Only gaseous and aqueous species appear. K_p = P_CO‚āā only.

**Equilibrium ‚ÄĒ JEE Chemistry Study Guide**

**1. Types of Equilibrium and Equilibrium Constant**

**Homogeneous Equilibrium**: All reactants and products in the same phase.
Example: N‚āā(g) + 3H‚āā(g) ‚áĆ 2NH‚āÉ(g)
K_c = [NH‚āÉ]¬≤ / ([N‚āā][H‚āā]¬≥) ‚ÄĒ concentration-based
K_p = P_NH‚āÉ¬≤ / (P_N‚āā ¬∑ P_H‚āā¬≥)

**Heterogeneous Equilibrium**: Reactants and products in different phases.
Example: Fe‚āÉO‚āĄ(s) + 4H‚āā(g) ‚áĆ 3Fe(s) + 4H‚āāO(g)
K_c = [H‚āāO]‚Āī / [H‚āā]‚Āī = ([H‚āāO]/[H‚āā])‚Āī (solid phases omitted)
K_p = (P_H‚āāO/P_H‚āā)‚Āī

**Relation between K_p and K_c**:
K_p = K_c(RT)^{őĒn} where őĒn = moles of gaseous products ‚ąí moles of gaseous reactants
For the Haber process: N‚āā + 3H‚āā ‚áĆ 2NH‚āÉ: őĒn = 2 ‚ąí 4 = ‚ąí2
K_p = K_c(RT)^{‚ąí2} = K_c/(RT)¬≤

**2. Equilibrium Constant ‚ÄĒ Physical Meaning and Units**

K has NO units when őĒn = 0 (dimensionless). For other cases, units depend on the reaction.
K‚āā = 1 (dimensionless by convention for all equilibria)
For CO + ¬ĹO‚āā ‚áĆ CO‚āā: K_c has units mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ/¬≤ L‚ĀĽ¬Ļ/¬≤ (or (mol/L)^{‚ąí1/2})

**Important properties of K**:
- K > 1: Products are favored at equilibrium
- K < 1: Reactants are favored at equilibrium
- K changes with temperature (depends on őĒH¬į)
- K does NOT change with concentration, pressure, or catalyst (catalyst only speeds up approach to equilibrium)
- K for reverse reaction = 1/K_forward
- K for reaction multiplied by n = (K)^n

**3. Le Chatelier's Principle ‚ÄĒ Quantitative Treatment**

**Effect of concentration change**:
Adding reactant ‚Üí Q < K ‚Üí system shifts right ‚Üí consumes added reactant
Removing product ‚Üí Q > K ‚Üí system shifts right ‚Üí produces more product to replace removed

**Effect of pressure change** (only for gaseous equilibria with őĒn ‚Č† 0):
Increasing P ‚Üí shifts toward fewer moles of gas
Decreasing P ‚Üí shifts toward more moles of gas

**Example**: In PCl‚āÖ(g) ‚áĆ PCl‚āÉ(g) + Cl‚āā(g): őĒn = +2. At constant T, if pressure is increased:
- Shift toward PCl‚āÖ (fewer gas moles) ‚ÄĒ equilibrium shifts left
- K remains constant; new equilibrium established with different concentrations

**Effect of temperature change**:
van 't Hoff equation: ln(K‚āā/K‚āĀ) = ‚ąí(őĒH¬į/R)(1/T‚āā ‚ąí 1/T‚āĀ)

For exothermic (őĒH¬į < 0): Increasing T ‚Üí K decreases (less product)
For endothermic (őĒH¬į > 0): Increasing T ‚Üí K increases (more product)

**Effect of inert gas at constant volume**: No effect on K or equilibrium composition (partial pressures of all components remain same since total molesšłćŚŹė, volumešłćŚŹė).

**Effect of inert gas at constant pressure**: Equilibrium shifts toward more moles of gas (because adding inert gas at constant P means V must increase, reducing all partial pressures equally, so Q = K and the system must shift to keep Q = K ‚ÄĒ actually check: for PCl‚āÖ ‚áĆ PCl‚āÉ + Cl‚āā, Q = P_PCl‚āÉ¬∑P_Cl‚āā/P_PCl‚āÖ = (n_PCl‚āÉ¬∑n_Cl‚āā/n_PCl‚āÖ)¬∑(P_total/V)... this gets complex. For most exam purposes: inert gas at constant V ‚Üí no effect; inert gas at constant P ‚Üí shifts toward more moles).

**4. Reaction Quotient Q ‚ÄĒ Identifying Direction of Shift**

For the general reaction, Q = K at equilibrium.
- Q < K: Net forward reaction (products too few) ‚Üí equilibrium shifts right
- Q > K: Net reverse reaction (products too many) ‚Üí equilibrium shifts left

**Example**: For 2SO‚āā(g) + O‚āā(g) ‚áĆ 2SO‚āÉ(g) with K_c = 4.0 at 500 K.
Initial: [SO‚āā] = 2 M, [O‚āā] = 1 M, [SO‚āÉ] = 0 M
Q = [SO‚āÉ]¬≤/([SO‚āā]¬≤[O‚āā]) = 0/(4√ó1) = 0 < K ‚Üí reaction proceeds forward.
At equilibrium: let x = [SO‚āÉ] formed = 2x (from stoichiometry). Then [SO‚āÉ] = 2x, [SO‚āā] = 2 ‚ąí 2x, [O‚āā] = 1 ‚ąí x.
4 = (2x)¬≤/((2‚ąí2x)¬≤(1‚ąíx)) ‚Üí solve for x.

**5. Degree of Dissociation (őĪ)**

For PCl‚āÖ(g) ‚áĆ PCl‚āÉ(g) + Cl‚āā(g):
Initially: n‚āÄ moles of PCl‚āÖ. At equilibrium: n = n‚āÄ(1‚ąíőĪ) PCl‚āÖ, n‚āÄőĪ PCl‚āÉ, n‚āÄőĪ Cl‚āā.
Total moles at equilibrium = n‚āÄ(1 + őĪ).
K_p = (őĪ¬≤P)/(1‚ąíőĪ)(1+őĪ) (where P = total pressure) ‚ÄĒ using partial pressures.

If őĪ is small (weak electrolytes, endothermic dissociation): őĪ = ‚ąö(K_p/P) for PCl‚āÖ decomposition where total pressure term simplifies.

‚ö° **Exam tip**: For a reaction A ‚áĆ 2B, if initially 1 mole of A is present and dissociation is őĪ, then at equilibrium: [A] = (1‚ąíőĪ)/V, [B] = 2őĪ/V. Total moles = 1 + őĪ. Partial pressure of B = (2őĪ/1+őĪ) √ó P_total.

**Equilibrium ‚ÄĒ Comprehensive JEE Advanced Notes**

**1. Thermodynamic Derivation of Equilibrium Constant**

From Gibbs free energy: őĒG = őĒG¬į + RT ln Q
At equilibrium: őĒG = 0, Q = K
Therefore: őĒG¬į = ‚ąíRT ln K

This is the fundamental link between thermodynamics and equilibrium.

**From chemical potentials**:
For ideal gases: őľŠĶĘ = őľ¬įŠĶĘ + RT ln(PŠĶĘ/P¬į)
For the reaction: őĒG¬į = ő£ őĹŠĶĘ őľ¬įŠĶĘ(products) ‚ąí ő£ őĹŠĶĘ őľ¬įŠĶĘ(reactants)
At equilibrium: ő£ őĹŠĶĘ őľŠĶĘ = 0 ‚Üí leads to K_p = ő† (PŠĶĘ)^{őĹŠĶĘ}

**van 't Hoff Equation ‚ÄĒ Temperature Dependence of K**:

d(ln K)/dT = őĒH¬į/RT¬≤

Integrating between T‚āĀ and T‚āā:
ln(K‚āā/K‚āĀ) = ‚ąí(őĒH¬į/R)(1/T‚āā ‚ąí 1/T‚āĀ)

This is the key to solving problems like: "Given K at 298 K and őĒH¬į, find K at 400 K."

**Example**: For N‚āāO‚āĄ ‚áĆ 2NO‚āā, őĒH¬į = +57 kJ/mol. K‚āĀ = 0.12 at 298 K. Find K at 400 K.
ln(K‚āā/0.12) = ‚ąí(57000/8.314)(1/400 ‚ąí 1/298)
= ‚ąí(6855)(‚ąí0.00115) = +7.88
K‚āā = 0.12 √ó e^{7.88} = 0.12 √ó 2650 ‚Čą 318
(The equilibrium shifts dramatically toward NO‚āā at higher temperature ‚ÄĒ consistent with endothermic dissociation.)

**2. Simultaneous and Consecutive Equilibria**

When multiple equilibria occur in the same system:

**Example**: In the "chamber process" for sulfuric acid:
SO‚āā + ¬ĹO‚āā ‚áĆ SO‚āÉ ... K‚āĀ
SO‚āÉ + H‚āāO ‚Üí H‚āāSO‚āĄ ... (essentially goes to completion, no K)
Combined: SO‚āā + H‚āāO + ¬ĹO‚āā ‚áĆ H‚āāSO‚āĄ ... K = K‚āĀ √ó K‚āā

For simultaneous equilibria sharing a common intermediate:
Equilibrium 1: A ‚áĆ B, K‚āĀ
Equilibrium 2: B ‚áĆ C, K‚āā
Net: A ‚áĆ C, K = K‚āĀ √ó K‚āā

**Complex reactions**: The overall equilibrium constant is the product of stepwise equilibrium constants.

**3. Equilibrium in Advanced Gas Phase Systems**

**Decomposition of CaCO‚āÉ in a closed vessel**:
CaCO‚āÉ(s) ‚áĆ CaO(s) + CO‚āā(g)
K_p = P_CO‚āā (only the gas appears)
This is called the "decomposition pressure." At a given T, P_CO‚āā is fixed regardless of amounts of CaCO‚āÉ or CaO present (as long as both solid phases are present).

**On heating**: P_CO‚āā increases. When P_CO‚āā exceeds atmospheric pressure, CaCO‚āÉ decomposes completely in an open container.

**Active mass**: In equilibrium expressions, we use concentrations (mol/L for solutions, partial pressures for gases). The term "active mass" of a solid is defined as 1 (or its molar concentration in the solid phase). So pure solids and liquids don't appear in K expressions.

**4. Shifting Paradigms ‚ÄĒ The Onsager Reciprocal Relations**

In equilibrium, the affinities (driving forces) are zero. Near equilibrium, the rates of processes are proportional to their affinities ‚ÄĒ linear non-equilibrium thermodynamics.

This extends beyond JEE scope but provides the theoretical basis for why Le Chatelier's principle works ‚ÄĒ systems respond to minimize applied stress.

**5. Equilibrium in Heterogeneous Systems ‚ÄĒ Detailed Examples**

**Example 1**: C(s) + H‚āāO(g) ‚áĆ CO(g) + H‚āā(g)
K_c = [CO][H‚āāO]/[H‚āāO] = [CO][H‚āā]/[H‚āā] ‚ÄĒ wait, carefully:
Actually: K_c = [CO][H‚āā]/[H‚āāO] (C is solid, omitted)
K_p = P_CO ¬∑ P_H‚āā / P_H‚āāO

**Example 2**: NH‚āĄHS(s) ‚áĆ NH‚āÉ(g) + H‚āāS(g)
K_p = P_NH‚āÉ ¬∑ P_H‚āāS (solids omitted)
If initially only NH‚āĄHS is present, let x = partial pressure of NH‚āÉ = partial pressure of H‚āāS (from stoichiometry).
K_p = x¬≤. Solve for x: x = ‚ąöK_p.

**If initially one gas is already present** (say, some NH‚āÉ is added), the equilibrium shifts:
Adding NH‚āÉ ‚Üí increases P_NH‚āÉ ‚Üí Q > K_p ‚Üí shift toward NH‚āĄHS (left) ‚Üí consumes some NH‚āÉ and H‚āāS. New equilibrium with different partial pressures.

**6. Degree of Dissociation ‚ÄĒ Advanced Treatment**

For PCl‚āÖ ‚áĆ PCl‚āÉ + Cl‚āā:
Total pressure P, initial moles n‚āÄ.
At equilibrium: n_PCl‚āÖ = n‚āÄ(1‚ąíőĪ), n_PCl‚āÉ = n‚āÄőĪ, n_Cl‚āā = n‚āÄőĪ
Total moles = n‚āÄ(1+őĪ)
PŠĶĘ = (nŠĶĘ/total) √ó P

P_PCl‚āÖ = [n‚āÄ(1‚ąíőĪ)/n‚āÄ(1+őĪ)] √ó P = [(1‚ąíőĪ)/(1+őĪ)] √ó P
P_PCl‚āÉ = [őĪ/(1+őĪ)] √ó P
P_Cl‚āā = [őĪ/(1+őĪ)] √ó P

K_p = (P_PCl‚āÉ ¬∑ P_Cl‚āā) / P_PCl‚āÖ = [őĪ¬≤/(1+őĪ)¬≤ √ó P¬≤] / [(1‚ąíőĪ)/(1+őĪ)] √ó P
= őĪ¬≤P/(1‚ąíőĪ¬≤)

This is the exact formula. For small őĪ: őĪ¬≤P/(1‚ąíőĪ¬≤) ‚Čą őĪ¬≤P ‚Üí őĪ ‚Čą ‚ąö(K_p/P)

**7. Effect of Temperature on Equilibrium ‚ÄĒ van 't Hoff Analysis**

**For endothermic reactions** (őĒH¬į > 0):
As T increases ‚Üí ln K increases ‚Üí K increases ‚Üí products favored.
As T decreases ‚Üí K decreases ‚Üí reactants favored.

**For exothermic reactions** (őĒH¬į < 0):
As T increases ‚Üí K decreases ‚Üí reactants favored.
As T decreases ‚Üí K increases ‚Üí products favored.

**Example**: Haber process N‚āā + 3H‚āā ‚áĆ 2NH‚āÉ, őĒH¬į = ‚ąí92 kJ/mol.
This is exothermic ‚Üí high T disfavors NH‚āÉ (K decreases). But high T is needed for sufficient reaction rate. Industrial compromise: ~400‚Äď500¬įC with catalyst.

**Le Chatelier at work**: The industrial Haber process uses:
- Moderate to high pressure (favors fewer gas moles ‚Üí NH‚āÉ)
- Moderate temperature (450¬įC compromise between yield and rate)
- Catalyst (Fe with K‚āāO, Al‚āāO‚āÉ promoters) ‚ÄĒ doesn't affect K, only rate
- Continuous removal of NH‚āÉ from reaction mixture ‚ÄĒ shifts equilibrium right

**8. Disturbing Equilibrium ‚ÄĒ Compressibility and Shift Direction**

For the general gas-phase reaction with őĒn:
When pressure is increased (volume decreased), the effect on Q depends on őĒn.

For aA + bB ‚áĆ cC + dD (all gases):
Q = (P_C/P¬į)^c (P_D/P¬į)^d / [(P_A/P¬į)^a (P_B/P¬į)^b]

If total pressure is increased n-fold (volume decreases n-fold), each partial pressure increases n-fold (assuming ideal gas behavior):
New Q = n^{(c+d‚ąía‚ąíb)} √ó original Q = n^{őĒn} √ó original Q

- If őĒn > 0: Q increases ‚Üí shift left (reverse reaction favored)
- If őĒn < 0: Q decreases ‚Üí shift right (forward reaction favored)
- If őĒn = 0: Q unchanged ‚Üí no shift

**9. Equilibrium in Dissociation of Weak Electrolytes**

For a weak electrolyte HA ‚áĆ H‚Āļ + A‚ĀĽ:
K_a = [H‚Āļ][A‚ĀĽ]/[HA]

If initial concentration is c and degree of dissociation is őĪ:
K_a = cőĪ¬≤/(1‚ąíőĪ) ‚Čą cőĪ¬≤ (for őĪ << 1)
őĪ = ‚ąö(K_a/c)

**Ostwald's Dilution Law**: őĪ ‚ąĚ 1/‚ąöc, K_a independent of c.

**For polyprotic acids** (H‚āāSO‚āĄ, H‚āÉPO‚āĄ):
Stepwise dissociation constants K‚āĀ, K‚āā, K‚āÉ.
For H‚āāSO‚āĄ: K‚āĀ (complete, strong acid behavior) ‚Čą ‚ąě; K‚āā ‚Čą 1.2 √ó 10‚ĀĽ¬≤ (weak).
For H‚āÉPO‚āĄ: K‚āĀ = 7.5 √ó 10‚ĀĽ¬≥, K‚āā = 6.2 √ó 10‚ĀĽ‚Āł, K‚āÉ = 4.8 √ó 10‚ĀĽ¬Ļ¬≥.

‚ö° **Exam tip**: When calculating pH of a mixture of weak acid and its salt (buffer), use Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pK_a + log([salt]/[acid]). For salts of weak acids with strong bases (e.g., Na‚āāCO‚āÉ), hydrolysis constant K_h = K_w/K_a, degree of hydrolysis h = ‚ąö(K_h/c).

**10. Solubility Product and Selective Precipitation**

For AgCl(s) ‚áĆ Ag‚Āļ(aq) + Cl‚ĀĽ(aq):
K_sp = [Ag‚Āļ][Cl‚ĀĽ] = 1.8 √ó 10‚ĀĽ¬Ļ‚Āį

**Molar solubility** s = [Ag‚Āļ] = [Cl‚ĀĽ] = ‚ąöK_sp

**Selective precipitation**: When two salts are present, adding a reagent precipitates the one with lower solubility product first.

**Example**: A solution has [Cl‚ĀĽ] = 0.1 M. Adding AgNO‚āÉ to precipitate AgCl:
[Ag‚Āļ] required = K_sp/[Cl‚ĀĽ] = 1.8 √ó 10‚ĀĽ¬Ļ‚Āį/0.1 = 1.8 √ó 10‚ĀĽ‚ĀĻ M
This very low [Ag‚Āļ] means AgCl precipitates readily.

**For salts with different stoichiometries** (e.g., Ag‚āāCrO‚āĄ vs AgCl):
Compare molar solubilities directly, not K_sp values.
Ag‚āāCrO‚āĄ: K_sp = 1.1 √ó 10‚ĀĽ¬Ļ¬≤ = s √ó (2s)¬≤ = 4s¬≥ ‚Üí s = (K_sp/4)^{1/3} = (2.75 √ó 10‚ĀĽ¬Ļ¬≥)^{1/3} ‚Čą 6.5 √ó 10‚ĀĽ‚ĀĶ M

Despite higher K_sp, AgCl is actually less soluble (lower s) than Ag‚āāCrO‚āĄ. Always compare molar solubilities, not K_sp directly for salts of different types.

‚ö° **Exam tip**: In ionic equilibrium problems with common ion effect, the solubility product remains constant (it's a function of temperature only). What changes is the molar solubility, which decreases in presence of a common ion. Write the dissociation equation carefully, include the common ion concentration from the added salt, then solve.
