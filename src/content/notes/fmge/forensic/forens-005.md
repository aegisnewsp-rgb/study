**Medical Jurisprudence and Legal Procedures — FMGE Study Guide**

## Medical Jurisprudence — Overview

Medical jurisprudence (legal medicine) encompasses the application of medical knowledge to legal matters. This includes the examination of living and deceased persons for legal purposes, the interpretation of medical findings for legal proceedings, and the rights and duties of medical practitioners under the law.

## Legal Procedures in Medicolegal Cases

### Inquest

An inquest is a legal inquiry into the cause and circumstances of death. In India, the Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC) 1973 governs inquest procedures.

**Section 174 CrPC — Police Inquest:**
- Conducted by Officer in Charge of Police Station (usually SHO)
- Applies to: Unnatural deaths, suicide, death during police custody, death where cause is unknown, deaths under suspicious circumstances
- The police officer must:
  - Proceed to the place of death
  - Investigate the facts and circumstances
  - If necessary, have the body examined by a medical officer
  - Submit a report to the Magistrate

**Section 176 CrPC — Magistrate's Inquest:**
- When the Magistrate considers it expedient
- Mandatory in cases of death in police custody
- Body must be sent to the nearest government doctor for examination
- The Magistrate may also order a second postmortem examination

**Types of Inquest Reports:**
- Natural death
- Accidental death
- Suicidal death
- Homicidal death
- Undetermined/ pending further investigation

### Autopsy (Postmortem Examination)

The postmortem examination is the cornerstone of forensic pathology. It is conducted to determine the cause of death, the manner of death, and to collect evidence.

**Types of Autopsy:**

**Medicolegal ( forensic) autopsy:**
- Ordered by Magistrate or police
- Aims to determine cause and manner of death
- Full external and internal examination
- All findings documented

**Clinical autopsy:**
- Conducted with written consent from next of kin
- For academic/quality improvement purposes
- May be limited to specific organs

**Exhumation:**
- When a buried body needs examination
- Requires written order from District Magistrate
- Should be performed as soon as possible
- Multiple bodies may be exhumed in mass disasters

**Who can conduct medicolegal autopsy?**
- Any registered medical practitioner can perform in an emergency
- Ideally conducted by a forensic medicine specialist (MD Forensic Medicine)
- At least two medical practitioners (one must be a specialist) in case of deaths during medical treatment

**Autopsy Procedure:**
1. **Documentation:** Photograph body, note body measurements, external appearance
2. **External examination:** Describe all external findings — injuries, marks, tattoos, deformities
3. **Internal examination:** 
   - Y-shaped incision from shoulders to pubis
   - Examine thoracic, abdominal, pelvic cavities
   - Remove and examine thoracic viscera en bloc
   - Remove and examine abdominal viscera
   - Examine neck for hidden injuries
   - Remove brain (after reflecting scalp)
   - Examine spinal cord if indicated
4. **Viscera preservation:** Preserve separate organs in separate containers for toxicology
5. **Documentation:** Write comprehensive report with findings and opinion

## Rights and Duties of Medical Practitioners

### Duties in Medicolegal Cases

**Examine without consent when:**
- Court orders examination
- Patient is unconscious and requires emergency treatment
- Public health emergency
- Patient is minor or mentally incompetent (consent from guardian)

**When to inform police:**
- Firearm injuries
- Suspected poisoning
- Suspected homicide
- Suspected suicide
- Deaths without attending physician
- Industrial accidents
- Road traffic accidents

### Consent

**Valid consent requires:**
1. Person must be legally competent to give consent (age, mental capacity)
2. Consent must be given voluntarily (not under duress)
3. Consent must be informed (person must understand what they are consenting to)
4. Consent must be specific (for the procedure being performed)

**Implied consent:** In emergencies where patient cannot give consent and no guardian is available, treatment may be given in patient's best interest.

**Consent for medicolegal examination:**
- Patient's consent required for examination when alive
- In emergency, examination can be done without consent if patient is unconscious
- Autopsy requires consent only from legal heirs (not from the deceased)

### Professional Secrecy

A medical practitioner is bound to maintain confidentiality about patients. Breach of confidentiality is permitted only in specific circumstances:
- When ordered by a court of law
- When required by public health laws (notifiable diseases)
- When patient gives consent
- When disclosure is in patient's interest but without consent
- When information is needed for insurance claims (with consent)
- When required under specific statutes

**Notifiable diseases** (under various state public health acts): cholera, plague, typhus, tuberculosis, hepatitis, HIV/AIDS (in some states), etc.

### Medical Negligence

**Elements of medical negligence:**
1. **Duty:** A doctor-patient relationship existed
2. **Breach of duty:** Doctor failed to meet the standard of care
3. **Causation:** Breach caused the injury/loss
4. **Damages:** Actual harm resulted

**Types of negligence:**
- **Criminal negligence:** Gross negligence with death or grievous hurt — Section 304A IPC (causing death by negligence), Section 325 IPC
- **Civil negligence:** Breach of duty leading to damages — consumer protection Act 1986
- **Professional negligence:** Breach of professional standards

**Standard of care:** Measured against what a reasonably competent practitioner in the same field would do in similar circumstances.

**Res ipsa loquitur:** "The thing speaks for itself" — when the very occurrence indicates negligence (e.g., leaving instrument inside patient after surgery).

**Defences against negligence:**
- Consent of patient
- Emergency situations
- Following accepted practice
- Contributory negligence by patient

## Indian Penal Code and Medical Practice

**Section 52 IPC:** "Nothing is said to be done or believed by a person who does it or believes it in good faith" — relevant to medical practice

**Section 80 IPC:** Accident in doing lawful act with proper care and skill — not an offence

**Section 88 IPC:** Act not intended to cause death, done in good faith for person's benefit — not an offence (requires consent)

**Section 89 IPC:** Act done in good faith for benefit of child/mentally incompetent person — not an offence

**Section 90 IPC:** Consent given under fear/misconception is not valid consent

**Sections 319–338 IPC:** Offences relating to hurt — medical practitioners may face charges under these sections for negligence causing injury

**Section 304A IPC:** Causing death by negligence — most commonly applied in medical negligence cases leading to death; punishment: imprisonment up to 2 years, fine, or both

**Section 201 IPC:** Causing disappearance of evidence of offence — may apply if medical records are falsified or destroyed

**Section 204 IPC:** Destruction of documents — if medical records are destroyed to prevent their use as evidence

## Consumer Protection Act and Medical Practice

The Consumer Protection Act 1986 (now CPA 2019) brought medical professionals under its jurisdiction:
- Medical services are considered "service" under the Act
- Patients are "consumers"
- Complaints can be filed at District, State, or National level
- Remedy includes compensation for deficiency in service
- This is a civil remedy — does not preclude criminal prosecution

**What constitutes "deficiency":**
- Negligence
- Incomplete treatment
- Lack of proper facilities
- Overcharging
- Refusal to treat emergency patients

## Medical Certification

### Death Certificate

A death certificate must be issued by the registered medical practitioner who attended the deceased during their last illness.

**Format of death certificate (as per Registration of Births and Deaths Act 1969):**
- Name of deceased
- Sex
- Age
- Occupation
- Address
- Date and time of death
- Cause of death (immediate cause, antecedent causes, underlying cause)
- Whether medical attention was received

**Causes that must be referred to police/Magistrate:**
- Death due to unnatural causes
- Death within 24 hours of admission
- Death where cause is unknown
- Death of a pregnant woman within 12 weeks of delivery
- Death due to suicide/homicide/accident

## The Physician and the Law

### Registration and Practice

**The Indian Medical Council Act 1956** (repealed and replaced by NMC Act 2019):
- National Medical Commission (NMC) now governs medical education and practice in India
- State Medical Councils handle registration and disciplinary matters
- Every medical practitioner must be registered with the State Medical Council
- Registration must be renewed periodically (every 5 years in most states)

**Who can practice medicine?**
- MBBS degree holders with valid registration
-BSMS, BHMS, BAMS — registered under respective councils (AYUSH)
- Postgraduate qualifications allow specialist practice
- Unregistered practitioners commit medical impersonation (Section 5 of the Act)

**ross practice:** Practicing a system other than the one registered for is prohibited.

### Professional Misconduct

**Grounds for disciplinary action:**
- Conviction for any offence involving moral turpitude
- Habitual drunkenness or addiction to drugs
- Professional negligence or infamous conduct
- Violation of regulations framed under the Act
- False certificates or documents

**Punishments:**
- Warning
- Fine
- Suspension of registration
- Removal of name from register (permanent)

### Medical Ethics

**Indian Medical Council (Professional Conduct, Etiquette and Ethics) Regulations 2002:**

**Duties of physicians:**
- Render care to all patients regardless of background
- Maintain confidentiality
- Maintain medical records
- Obtain informed consent
- Refer cases when beyond expertise
- Not advertise falsely
- Not dispense spurious drugs
- Not refuse emergency care

**Consent in special situations:**
- **Minors:** Consent from parent or legal guardian; emergency treatment without consent if guardian unavailable
- **Unconscious patients:** Implied consent in emergencies; consent from legal guardian when possible
- **Mentally incompetent:** Consent from legal guardian
- **Emergencies:** Treatment in patient's best interest without explicit consent

## Legal Medicine in Criminal Cases

### Examination of the Accused

When a person is accused of an offence and their medical examination is ordered by a court or police:
- Examination should be conducted at the request of police with court order
- The accused has the right to be examined by his own doctor (second opinion)
- A report must be prepared with findings
- The report is admissible in court

**Sections related to medical examination of accused:**
- Section 53 CrPC: Examination of accused by medical practitioner at request of police officer
- Section 54 CrPC: Accused can demand examination by his own doctor
- Section 55 CrPC: Sample collection for DNA analysis

### Examination of the Victim

**In sexual offences:**
- Detailed examination with consent
- Documentation of all injuries (fresh and healing)
- Collection of forensic evidence (swabs, hair, clothing)
- Pregnancy test if indicated
- HIV and STI screening
- Psychological support and referral

**Evidence collection in sexual assault:**
- Vaginal/anal/oral swabs for sperm
- Swabs for DNA profiling
- Fingernail scrapings
- Hair samples
- Clothing for foreign fibres/semen
- Blood for HIV, hepatitis, VDRL

### Forensic Science Laboratory

**Role of FSL:**
- Chemical analysis of poisons
- DNA profiling
- Ballistic examination
- Document examination (handwriting, forgery)
- Tool mark analysis
- Serology (blood group, species identification)

**Viscera forwarding for analysis:**
- Must be sent with forwarding letter
- Each organ in separate container
- Sealed and labelled
- Refrigerated during transport
- FSL report is a valuable piece of evidence in court

## Medical Documentation

**Importance of documentation:**
- Primary record of patient's condition and treatment
- Evidence in case of legal proceedings
- Enables continuity of care
- Required for insurance and legal claims

**Principles of good documentation (SOAP):**
- **S:** Subjective — patient's complaints
- **O:** Objective — clinical findings, observations
- **A:** Assessment — diagnosis, differential diagnosis
- **P:** Plan — treatment plan, follow-up

**Features of good medical records:**
- Clear, legible, dated
- Written in permanent ink
- No blank spaces (draw a line through them)
- Corrections should be signed and dated
- Entries should be contemporaneous
- Separate notes for each patient

**Retention of records:**
- Indoor case records: 3 years (minimum)
- Medicolegal cases: Permanent
- Birth/death records: Permanent
- Insurance records: As per policy requirements

## Medical Certificates

### Types of Certificates

**Medical certificate of cause of death (Form 4):**
- Issued by attending physician
- Must be issued within 3 days of death
- Sent to Registrar of Births and Deaths
- Cause of death must be in standard format (ICD-10)

**Fitness certificate (Form 3):**
- Issued after examination
- Should clearly state fitness or unfitness
- Should mention any limitations

**Age certificate:**
- Required for school admission, employment, court cases
- Based on physical examination ( Tanner staging, dental examination) or radiological examination (bone age assessment)
- Cannot give exact age — only estimated range

**Disability certificate:**
- Issued by a board of doctors
- Required for disabled persons certificate for government benefits
- Specifies type and percentage of disability

**Certificate for injury (Form 4A):**
- Describes all injuries in detail
- Indicates whether injury is simple or grievous
- Must be filed with police

## Artificial Insemination and Surrogacy

**The Assisted Reproductive Technology (Regulation) Act 2021:**
- Regulates ART banks and clinics in India
- Surrogacy (Regulation) Act 2021: Altruistic surrogacy permitted for Indian citizens
- Commercial surrogacy is prohibited
- Both gamete providers must be Indian citizens

## Transplantation of Human Organs

**Transplantation of Human Organs and Tissues Act (THOA) 1994:**
- Brainstem death certification allows organ donation
- Living donors: Near relatives only (with exceptions)
- Brain death: Four-doctor panel certification
- Eyes (cornea) can be donated by any deceased person
- Commercial dealing in organs is a punishable offence

## Civil and Criminal Responsibilities

**Civil liability:** Compensation for damages under CPA
**Criminal liability:** Sections 304A, 336, 337, 338 IPC
**Professional regulation:** NMC/State Medical Council disciplinary action
