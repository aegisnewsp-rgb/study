## Newton's Laws of Motion — Complete Study Notes

### Introduction

Mechanics is divided into two parts: **Kinematics** (description of motion without considering its cause) and **Dynamics** (study of forces that cause motion). Newton's laws form the foundation of classical dynamics and are essential for the DU admission test.

### Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia)

> "A body continues in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by an external force."

**Key concepts:**
- **Inertia**: The property of a body that resists changes in its state of motion
- **Mass** is the quantitative measure of inertia
- Greater mass → Greater inertia → Harder to accelerate or decelerate
- **Uniform motion**: Constant velocity (including zero velocity)
- The law defines an **inertial reference frame**

**Examples in everyday life:**
- Passenger jerks backward when a bus suddenly starts (tendency to remain at rest)
- Dust flying off a carpet when beaten (tendency to remain at rest)
- Seat belts prevent passengers from flying forward during sudden braking

### Newton's Second Law (Law of Acceleration)

> "The rate of change of momentum of a body is directly proportional to the applied force and takes place in the direction of the force."

**Mathematical form:**
**F = ma**

Where:
- F = Net external force (Newton, N)
- m = Mass of body (kg)
- a = Acceleration (m/s²)

**Important points:**
- 1 Newton: Force that gives 1 kg mass an acceleration of 1 m/s²
- **1 N = 1 kg·m/s²**
- The law applies to **net force**, not individual forces
- Direction of acceleration is same as direction of net force
- If multiple forces: **ΣF = ma**

**Momentum (p):** p = mv (kg·m/s)
- Momentum is a **vector quantity**
- Second law can also be written: **F = dp/dt**

### Newton's Third Law (Law of Action-Reaction)

> "For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."

**Key properties of action-reaction pairs:**
1. They act on **different bodies** (never on the same body)
2. They are **equal in magnitude**
3. They are **opposite in direction**
4. They occur **simultaneously**

**Examples:**
- Walking: Foot pushes backward on ground (action) → Ground pushes foot forward (reaction)
- Rocket propulsion: Hot gases pushed downward (action) → Rocket pushed upward (reaction)
- Swimming: Hands push water backward (action) → Water pushes swimmer forward (reaction)

**Common misconceptions:**
- Action and reaction do NOT cancel because they act on different bodies
- "Equal and opposite" does NOT mean they cancel each other

### Free Body Diagrams (FBD)

Essential tool for solving mechanics problems. Draw ALL forces acting on the body of interest.

**Steps:**
1. Identify the body/system
2. Draw a dot or box to represent the body
3. Draw arrows representing all external forces (with correct direction)
4. Label each force (gravity, normal, tension, friction, applied force)
5. Choose coordinate system
6. Apply Newton's laws

### Applications — Common Problem Types

#### 1. Horizontal Surface
- Weight: W = mg (downward)
- Normal force: N = mg (upward, equals weight on flat surface)
- Applied force F: Horizontal
- Friction: f = μN (opposes motion)

#### 2. Inclined Plane
- Resolve weight into components:
  - **Parallel to plane**: mg sinθ
  - **Perpendicular to plane**: mg cosθ
- Normal force: N = mg cosθ
- Acceleration down plane: a = g(sinθ − μcosθ)

#### 3. Pulley Systems

**Fixed pulley** (ideal): Changes direction only, T₁ = T₂

**Movable pulley**: Tension is halved, 2T = mg

**Atwood's machine** (two masses on frictionless pulley):
- a = (m₁ − m₂)g / (m₁ + m₂)
- T = 2m₁m₂g / (m₁ + m₂)

**Tension**: Same throughout a massless, frictionless rope

#### 4. Connected Bodies
For two blocks A and B on frictionless surface, pulled by force F:
- a = F / (m_A + m_B)
- T = m_B × a

### Linear Momentum

**p = mv**

**Conservation of Linear Momentum:**
In absence of external forces, total momentum of a system remains constant.

**Impulse (J):**
- J = FΔt = Δp = mv − mu
- Impulse changes momentum
- Area under F-t graph = Impulse

**Impulse-Momentum Theorem:**
J = Δp (always true, even with variable force)

### Friction

**Static friction (f_s):** Prevents motion initiation
- 0 ≤ f_s ≤ μ_s N
- f_s = applied force (at threshold of motion)
- Maximum static friction: f_s(max) = μ_s N

**Kinetic friction (f_k):** Opposes motion
- f_k = μ_k N (constant)
- μ_k < μ_s (kinetic < static)

**Rolling friction**: Much smaller than sliding friction

### Circular Motion and Newton's Laws

For a body moving in a circle with constant speed:
- **Centripetal force**: F_c = mv²/r = mrω²
- This force is NOT a new type — it's whatever force keeps the body in circular path (tension, gravity, friction, normal force)

### Common DU Admission Questions Pattern

1. Draw free body diagram and find acceleration
2. Pulley system — find tension and acceleration
3. Two blocks connected — find acceleration and force between them
4. Object on inclined plane — find acceleration, normal force
5. Apply conservation of momentum in collision problems

### Must-Remember Formulas

| Formula | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| F = ma | Newton's Second Law |
| W = mg | Weight |
| p = mv | Linear momentum |
| J = FΔt | Impulse |
| f_k = μ_k N | Kinetic friction |
| f_s(max) = μ_s N | Maximum static friction |
| a = g(sinθ − μcosθ) | Inclined plane with friction |
| a = (m₁−m₂)g/(m₁+m₂) | Atwood's machine |
| F_c = mv²/r | Centripetal force |

### Exam Tips

- Always check units: 1 N = 1 kg·m/s²
- "Normal reaction" is perpendicular to the surface
- Friction always opposes relative motion (or impending motion)
- Action and reaction act on DIFFERENT bodies — never draw them on the same free body diagram
- When in doubt, write ΣF = ma for each direction separately
- For pulley problems: String tension is same on both sides if rope is massless and frictionless
