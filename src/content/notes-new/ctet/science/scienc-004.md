# Human Body Systems

## Skeletal System

The human skeleton consists of **206 bones** in adults. It provides structural support, protects internal organs, allows movement, and produces blood cells (in red bone marrow).

### Divisions of the Skeleton

- **Axial skeleton (80 bones):** Skull, vertebral column (33 vertebrae), rib cage (12 pairs of ribs + sternum)
- **Appendicular skeleton (126 bones):** Bones of upper limbs (shoulder girdle, arm, forearm, hand), lower limbs (pelvic girdle, thigh, leg, foot)

### Types of Bones

| Shape | Examples | Features |
|-------|----------|----------|
| **Long bones** | Femur, humerus | Longer than wide; lever action for movement |
| **Short bones** | Carpals, tarsals | Cube-shaped; provide stability |
| **Flat bones** | Skull bones, scapula, sternum | Flat, thin; protection function |
| **Irregular bones** | Vertebrae, hip bones | Complex shapes |

### Joints and Types

A **joint** is the point where two or more bones meet. Joints allow movement and are classified as:

- **Ball and socket joint:** Permits movement in all directions (e.g., shoulder joint, hip joint)
- **Hinge joint:** Movement in one plane only (e.g., elbow, knee)
- **Pivot joint:** Rotational movement (e.g., atlas-axis joint allowing head rotation)
- **Gliding joint:** Sliding movement (e.g., between carpals)
- **Fixed joint (Fibrous/Sutures):** No movement (e.g., skull bones in adults)

**Important CTET facts:**
- The knee joint is the largest joint in the human body
- The elbow has a hinge joint; the shoulder has a ball-and-socket joint
- In the skull of infants, bones are separated by soft spots called **fontanelles**, which fuse as the child grows

## Muscular System

Humans have three types of muscle tissue:

| Type | Location | Control | Function |
|------|----------|---------|----------|
| **Skeletal (striated/voluntary)** | Attached to bones | Voluntary (conscious) | Body movement |
| **Smooth (involuntary/non-striated)** | Walls of internal organs (stomach, intestine, blood vessels) | Involuntary | Movement of substances (peristalsis) |
| **Cardiac muscle** | Heart | Involuntary | Heart beating |

**Key concept:** Cardiac muscle is **striated** (like skeletal) but **involuntary** (like smooth). It has the property of **automaticity** — it can contract and relax without external stimulation.

**Muscle contraction:** Muscles contract when stimulated by nerve impulses. The sliding filament theory explains contraction: actin and myosin filaments slide past each other, shortening the muscle fiber. Energy for contraction comes from **ATP**.

**Antagonistic muscles:** Muscles work in pairs — when one contracts, the other relaxes. Example: Biceps (flexor) and Triceps (extensor) in the upper arm. When you bend your arm, biceps contracts and triceps relaxes; when you straighten it, triceps contracts and biceps relaxes.

## Nervous System

The nervous system is the body's control and communication center.

### Central Nervous System (CNS)

- **Brain:** Encased in the cranium (skull), divided into three main parts:
  - **Cerebrum (Forebrain):** Largest part; controls voluntary actions, intelligence, memory, speech, vision, hearing
  - **Cerebellum:** Coordinates body movements, maintains posture and balance
  - **Medulla oblongata:** Controls involuntary functions (heartbeat, breathing, blood pressure)
- **Spinal Cord:** Long, cylindrical structure inside the vertebral column; transmits messages between brain and rest of body; controls reflex actions

### Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

- **Somatic nervous system:** Controls voluntary actions (moving limbs, speaking)
- **Autonomic nervous system:** Controls involuntary actions (heartbeat, digestion)

### Neurons (Nerve Cells)

The functional unit of the nervous system is the **neuron**, which consists of:
- **Cell body (soma):** Contains the nucleus
- **Dendrites:** Short, branched extensions that receive stimuli
- **Axon:** Long fiber that transmits impulses away from cell body
- **Myelin sheath:** Fatty layer covering some axons (in white matter); speeds up impulse conduction

**Types of neurons:**
- **Sensory neurons:** Carry impulses from sense organs to CNS
- **Motor neurons:** Carry impulses from CNS to effectors (muscles/glands)
- **Relay/Association neurons:** Connect sensory and motor neurons in CNS

### Reflex Arc

A **reflex** is an automatic, involuntary response to a stimulus. The pathway followed is the **reflex arc**:

**Stimulus → Sensory receptor → Sensory neuron → Spinal cord (relay neuron) → Motor neuron → Effector (muscle/gland) → Response**

**Example:** When you touch a hot object, sensory neurons carry the pain signal to the spinal cord, which immediately sends a motor response to withdraw your hand, without involving the brain (which would take longer). This is why touching a hot stove and withdrawing your hand before you "feel" pain is a reflex action.

**Important CTET fact:** Reflex actions are protective mechanisms that occur without conscious thought, mediated by the spinal cord rather than the brain.

## Endocrine System

The endocrine system uses **hormones** (chemical messengers secreted into the blood) to regulate body functions.

| Gland | Hormone(s) | Function |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| **Pituitary** (Master gland) | Growth hormone (GH), TSH, FSH, LH, ADH | Controls other glands; growth; water retention |
| **Thyroid** | Thyroxine (T4), Triiodothyronine (T3) | Regulates metabolism, growth, development |
| **Parathyroid** | Parathormone (PTH) | Calcium and phosphorus metabolism |
| **Pancreas** (Islets of Langerhans) | Insulin, Glucagon | Blood sugar regulation |
| **Adrenal** | Adrenaline (epinephrine), Cortisol | Stress response, fight-or-flight |
| **Ovaries** (female) | Estrogen, Progesterone | Female reproductive functions |
| **Testes** (male) | Testosterone | Male reproductive functions |

**Key point for CTET:**
- **Thyroid disorders:** Deficiency causes **goitre** (enlarged thyroid) and **cretinism** in children; excess causes **Graves' disease**
- **Pancreas disorders:** Deficiency of insulin causes **diabetes mellitus** (high blood sugar); treated with insulin injections
- **Adrenaline:** Secreted during fear, anger, or excitement; increases heart rate, breathing rate, and blood pressure

## Excretory System

The excretory system removes metabolic waste products from the body.

### Human Excretory System

**Kidneys → Ureters → Urinary bladder → Urethra**

### Structure of Kidney

Each kidney has approximately **1 million nephrons**, which are the functional units.

**Nephron structure:**
1. **Glomerulus:** A knot of capillaries fed by afferent arteriole; blood is filtered here under high pressure
2. **Bowman's capsule:** Cup-shaped structure surrounding glomerulus; receives filtered blood
3. **Proximal convoluted tubule (PCT):** Reabsorbs useful substances (glucose, amino acids, water, salts)
4. **Loop of Henle:** Creates concentration gradient in medulla
5. **Distal convoluted tubule (DCT):** Further adjustment of ions and water
6. **Collecting duct:** Collects urine and carries it to renal pelvis

**Urine formation:** Involves three processes:
- **Filtration:** Blood pressure forces water and small molecules from glomerulus into Bowman's capsule (formation of **primary urine** — ~180 litres/day)
- **Reabsorption:** Useful substances reabsorbed in PCT and DCT (glucose, water, amino acids)
- **Secretion:** Additional waste products secreted from blood into urine

**Urine composition:** Water (95%), urea, uric acid, creatinine, excess salts and minerals.

**CTET High-Yield:**
- The functional unit of kidney is the **nephron** (not the kidney itself)
- The kidneys regulate **water and salt balance** (osmoregulation)
- The left kidney is slightly higher than the right (due to liver position on right side)
- **Kidney failure** requires **dialysis** (artificial filtering of blood) or kidney transplantation
